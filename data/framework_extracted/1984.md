# Berkshire Hathaway 1984 Letter — Reusable Buffett Analysis Framework

Source letter: Berkshire Hathaway Chairman’s Letter for 1984 (written February 25, 1985).

## Purpose of this extraction

This document extracts the **reusable stock/business analysis framework** embedded in the 1984 Berkshire Hathaway shareholder letter. It excludes year-specific facts unless they reveal a durable analytical rule.

The goal is to surface Buffett’s repeatable logic for evaluating:
- business quality
- management quality
- capital allocation
- underwriting economics
- stock repurchases
- bond investments
- dividend policy
- accounting quality
- portfolio construction

---

## 1) Master principles

### 1.1 Economic performance must always be judged relative to capital employed
Buffett rejects absolute profit figures as a primary measure. A gain only matters when compared with the capital required to produce it.

**Reusable idea**
- Never evaluate a company by earnings growth alone.
- Ask how much capital was required to generate the gain.
- Prefer businesses that can produce high returns without needing proportionally large incremental capital.

**Metric focus**
- Return on equity
- Return on incremental capital
- Growth in intrinsic value per share
- Capital intensity

**Evaluation questions**
- How much capital is tied up to produce current earnings?
- Are earnings growing because economics improved, or simply because more capital was added?
- Is the company becoming less efficient as it grows?

---

### 1.2 Intrinsic business value per share is the true scorecard
Book value can be a useful proxy, but only when it reasonably tracks intrinsic value. Buffett cares about **per-share intrinsic business value**, not accounting presentation.

**Reusable idea**
- The real output of business analysis is an estimate of intrinsic value per share.
- Accounting measures are only tools; they are not the destination.

**Metric focus**
- Intrinsic value per share
- Book value per share as a proxy only when justified
- Change in intrinsic value versus change in stock price

**Evaluation questions**
- Does book value meaningfully approximate intrinsic value here?
- Is market value moving differently from business value?
- Is per-share value rising, not just total corporate size?

---

### 1.3 Scale creates return drag
As capital base expands, maintaining high rates of return becomes harder. Large enterprises need large opportunities; small ideas stop mattering.

**Reusable idea**
- A company’s future return profile depends partly on the size of the capital base it must deploy.
- Past compounding rates should not be projected blindly.

**Metric focus**
- Required future earnings to sustain target returns
- Size of deployable capital versus opportunity set

**Evaluation questions**
- Is the company too large for its opportunity set?
- Does management acknowledge that size lowers the odds of exceptional compounding?
- Are future targets realistic relative to capital base?

---

### 1.4 Doing nothing is often superior to lowering standards
When opportunities do not meet qualitative and quantitative thresholds, inactivity is rational.

**Reusable idea**
- Opportunity scarcity is not a reason to compromise.
- Patience is part of the process, not evidence of passivity.

**Evaluation questions**
- Is management investing because returns are attractive, or because cash is burning a hole in their pocket?
- Would inaction be better than a mediocre deployment?

---

## 2) Business quality framework

### 2.1 Prefer businesses with strong underlying economics, not merely reported growth
Buffett repeatedly separates **underlying economics** from reported accounting output.

**Reusable idea**
- Look for businesses with durable favorable economics rather than cosmetic growth.
- Treat one-time gains, asset sales, and accounting classifications as noise unless they change economic reality.

**Evaluation questions**
- What portion of earnings is operating versus incidental?
- Are gains repeatable?
- Does management distinguish operating performance from episodic gains?

---

### 2.2 Great businesses often combine strong customer value with strong returns on capital
Nebraska Furniture Mart is used to show that low prices do not imply low returns when operating efficiency is exceptional.

**Reusable idea**
- A superior business can both save customers money and earn high returns.
- Efficiency can be a moat when it structurally permits lower prices and high volume.

**Metric focus**
- Gross margin
- Operating expense ratio
- Return on capital
- Customer savings versus competitors
- Geographic reach / share gains driven by value proposition

**Evaluation questions**
- Does the company win through structural efficiency, not temporary discounting?
- Can it offer a meaningfully better value proposition than rivals?
- Are low prices supported by low costs, or by weak economics?

---

### 2.3 A superior product plus superior management can overwhelm a weak industry backdrop
See’s shows that a business can perform well even in an industry where many peers lose money.

**Reusable idea**
- Industry conditions matter, but exceptional brand/product quality plus management can create outlier economics.
- Judge the company against the industry and against the economics of weaker peers.

**Metric focus**
- Profitability versus industry peers
- Same-store volume
- Pricing power
- Cost control
- Seasonal concentration risk

**Evaluation questions**
- Is profitability due to industry tailwinds, or to company-specific advantages?
- Does the company preserve product quality when under cost pressure?
- Can it raise prices without destroying customer loyalty?

---

### 2.4 Dominance in a local or niche market can create extraordinary economics
The Buffalo News section highlights that certain business models become extremely profitable once dominance is achieved.

**Reusable idea**
- Some businesses benefit from local dominance, network effects, or quasi-monopoly “bulletin board” utility.
- Once dominance exists, economics may remain strong even if product quality fluctuates.

**Metric focus**
- Market penetration
- Revenue concentration from advertisers/customers who need reach
- Useful versus wasted circulation / audience
- Margins once scale dominance is established

**Evaluation questions**
- Is this business dominant where dominance actually matters?
- Does the market structure create persistent pricing power?
- Is the company’s product essential because of utility, habit, reach, or network position?

---

### 2.5 Sustainable competitive advantage is rare and extremely valuable
Buffett identifies GEICO’s low-cost position in its core insurance business as a major, sustainable edge.

**Reusable idea**
- A true competitive advantage is not generic “good management”; it is a structural reason the business can earn superior returns for a long time.
- In large markets, a real advantage can produce enormous value.

**Evaluation questions**
- What specific structural advantage does the company have?
- Is the advantage sustainable, or just cyclical?
- Does it exist in the core business, or only in peripheral activities?

---

### 2.6 Simplicity matters
Buffett explicitly prefers simple businesses and avoids those with too much technology or complexity.

**Reusable idea**
- Complexity raises the odds of analytical error.
- Preference should be given to businesses whose economics can be understood with confidence.

**Evaluation questions**
- Is the business simple enough to understand its drivers?
- Would a reasonable analyst be able to predict long-term economics without relying on speculative assumptions?
- Is technological complexity masking weak economics?

---

## 3) Management quality framework

### 3.1 Integrity is a primary asset
Buffett treats integrity as so valuable that it can reduce the need for elaborate verification.

**Reusable idea**
- Character is not a soft factor; it is economically material.
- High-integrity management reduces agency risk, accounting risk, and deal risk.

**Signals Buffett uses**
- Truthfulness
- Fair dealing with all counterparties
- Reputation built over time
- Behavior consistent with words

**Evaluation questions**
- Would you trust this management team with minimal contractual protection?
- Do they behave honestly when no one forces them to?
- Is their reputation matched by observable conduct?

---

### 3.2 Management should stay within its circle of competence
The Blumkin example emphasizes defining a special competence and rejecting attractive opportunities outside it.

**Reusable idea**
- Excellence requires realism about limits.
- Good managers are decisive inside their competence and disciplined outside it.

**Evaluation questions**
- Does management know what it understands and what it does not?
- Have major mistakes come from wandering outside core competence?
- Is expansion adjacent and logical, or driven by excitement?

---

### 3.3 Owner orientation must be demonstrated through actions
Buffett distinguishes between managers who talk about shareholder wealth and managers who prove it through behavior.

**Reusable idea**
- Capital allocation decisions reveal managerial motives better than rhetoric.
- Repurchases, reinvestment choices, payout policy, and deal discipline are tests of owner orientation.

**Evaluation questions**
- When the company had an obvious chance to benefit owners, did management act?
- Do actions suggest empire building or owner-mindedness?
- Are shareholders treated like partners or funding sources?

---

### 3.4 Management quality includes operational discipline under strain
Buffett praises managers who handle cost pressure, seasonality, labor pressure, and service quality without lowering standards.

**Reusable idea**
- Excellent operators preserve economics and standards simultaneously.
- The best evidence often appears during operational stress, not easy periods.

**Evaluation questions**
- Does management control costs without damaging the franchise?
- How do they behave when demand peaks, labor costs rise, or competition intensifies?
- Are quality compromises used to flatter short-term margins?

---

### 3.5 Incentives matter: owners can act more rationally than career managers
Buffett argues conventional managers often avoid unconventional but intelligent decisions because career risk is asymmetric.

**Reusable idea**
- Incentives influence capital allocation behavior.
- Owner-managers or aligned managers may make better long-term decisions because they bear the same economic consequences as shareholders.

**Evaluation questions**
- Is management compensated and exposed like owners?
- Are they likely to choose convention over correctness?
- Do they optimize for reputation safety or economic gain?

---

## 4) Capital allocation framework

### 4.1 Capital allocation is central to business quality
Buffett treats the decision to retain, distribute, repurchase, or redeploy capital as one of management’s most important jobs.

**Reusable idea**
- A good business can be turned into a poor investment by bad capital allocation.
- Always separate operating excellence from capital allocation excellence.

**Evaluation questions**
- What has management done with retained earnings?
- Has each major allocation decision increased per-share value?
- Is capital sent to its highest-return use?

---

### 4.2 Retain earnings only when each retained dollar is likely to create at least one dollar of market value
This is one of the clearest reusable rules in the letter.

**Reusable idea**
- Retention is justified only when expected returns on incremental capital are high enough to create at least equivalent owner value.
- Retention without this test is managerial self-indulgence.

**Metric focus**
- Incremental return on retained earnings
- Market value created per dollar retained
- Historical evidence plus reasoned forward analysis

**Evaluation questions**
- For every dollar retained, how much owner value is likely to be created?
- Does historical evidence support reinvestment skill?
- Are retained earnings going into high-return uses or mediocre expansions?

---

### 4.3 Not all earnings are distributable in an economic sense
Inflation and capital intensity can make reported earnings partly “restricted” or economically unavailable for distribution.

**Reusable idea**
- Reported earnings may overstate true distributable cash.
- Businesses with high asset/profit ratios may have to reinvest heavily just to stand still.

**Metric focus**
- Asset intensity
- Maintenance capital needs
- Inflation effects on replacement cost
- Economic versus reported earnings

**Evaluation questions**
- How much of reported earnings must be reinvested simply to maintain position?
- Is the business consuming capital just to preserve unit volume and competitiveness?
- Are dividend payments being funded by economic shrinkage?

---

### 4.4 Great core businesses can hide bad capital allocation elsewhere
A company may show strong overall returns because its exceptional core business offsets poor redeployment into mediocre acquisitions or side ventures.

**Reusable idea**
- Strong aggregate returns do not prove capital allocation skill.
- Separate returns earned in the core franchise from returns earned on incremental external deployment.

**Evaluation questions**
- Are excellent reported returns coming from the legacy moat while new investments underperform?
- Has management used excess cash intelligently outside the core?
- Are acquisitions masking weak deployment discipline?

---

### 4.5 Repurchases are superior when stock trades materially below intrinsic value
Buffett’s view is clear: when an outstanding business with a comfortable financial position repurchases cheap stock, it can create enormous shareholder value.

**Reusable idea**
- Repurchases are value-creating only when price is below intrinsic value and balance-sheet strength remains sound.
- Repurchases can be a better use of capital than acquisitions.

**Metric focus**
- Repurchase price versus intrinsic value
- Balance-sheet strength
- Value gained per dollar spent
- Per-share intrinsic value accretion

**Evaluation questions**
- Is the stock clearly undervalued relative to intrinsic value?
- Is the company financially strong enough to repurchase safely?
- Would this dollar create more value in buybacks than in acquisitions or expansion?

---

### 4.6 Repurchases also signal managerial alignment
Buffett views intelligently timed repurchases as proof that management favors owners over empire expansion.

**Reusable idea**
- Capital allocation sends a governance signal to the market.
- Management that repurchases when obviously attractive earns credibility.

**Evaluation questions**
- Has management ever acted decisively when buybacks were clearly the best option?
- Do they repurchase opportunistically or mechanically?
- Does the pattern suggest discipline or cosmetic EPS management?

---

### 4.7 Avoid acquisitions that exchange one dollar for less than one dollar of value
Buffett contrasts cheap repurchases with typical acquisition programs that often fail to earn even a dollar of value per dollar spent.

**Reusable idea**
- Most acquisitions should be presumed suspect until proven value-creating.
- Compare every acquisition against repurchases, dividends, debt reduction, and inactivity.

**Evaluation questions**
- What is the expected value received per dollar spent?
- Is management buying because the asset is attractive or because they prefer expansion?
- Would owners be better off with repurchases or distributions instead?

---

### 4.8 Use stock as currency only when equal or greater intrinsic value is received
Buffett is willing to issue stock only when Berkshire receives at least as much intrinsic value as it gives.

**Reusable idea**
- Stock issuance is a sale of part of the business.
- It is rational only when the company is receiving equal or greater business value in return.

**Evaluation questions**
- Is management issuing undervalued stock to buy weaker assets?
- What intrinsic value is being surrendered versus received?
- Would a knowledgeable owner approve the exchange?

---

## 5) Stock selection framework

### 5.1 Treat stocks as partial interests in businesses
Buffett explicitly says he buys marketable stocks using the same criteria he would use in buying an entire business.

**Reusable idea**
- Stock analysis should begin with business analysis.
- Ignore the distinction between public and private when evaluating economics and value.

**Evaluation questions**
- If this whole company were available, would you want to own it?
- What are the normalized economics of the business?
- Is the stock price attractive relative to business value?

---

### 5.2 Require both qualitative quality and quantitative cheapness
Buffett says opportunities must meet qualitative standards and value-versus-price standards.

**Reusable idea**
- Great business plus poor price is not enough.
- Cheap price without durable economics is also not enough.

**Evaluation questions**
- Does the business meet quality thresholds?
- Is the gap between price and value wide enough?
- Are you lowering one standard because the other looks attractive?

---

### 5.3 Bargain purchases of strong businesses create asymmetric outcomes
Buffett’s logic: if you buy small pieces of businesses with satisfactory economics at a fraction of per-share value, something favorable is likely to happen over time.

**Reusable idea**
- Margin of safety comes from buying solid economics at a meaningful discount.
- A group of such positions increases the odds that value realization will be favorable.

**Evaluation questions**
- Is this a satisfactory business at a discounted price, or a bad business at a low multiple?
- What is the downside if value realization takes longer than expected?
- Does the business quality protect the discount thesis?

---

### 5.4 Business performance matters more than market performance
Buffett prefers intrinsic value rising while stock price lags rather than stock price rising faster than the business improves.

**Reusable idea**
- A stagnant or falling market price can be good if it lets value compound without speculative overvaluation.
- The analyst should track business progress separately from stock movement.

**Evaluation questions**
- Is stock underperformance masking business progress?
- Has stock outperformance run ahead of business improvement?
- Are you reacting to price or to the business?

---

### 5.5 Concentration is rational only when analytical confidence and financial strength are high
Buffett favors concentration, but only because Berkshire’s financial position is unusually strong.

**Reusable idea**
- Concentration can improve results when based on deep understanding and when the balance sheet can survive error.
- Concentration without resilience is recklessness.

**Evaluation questions**
- How wrong can you be without permanent damage?
- Is conviction based on deep work or enthusiasm?
- Does the capital base permit concentration?

---

## 6) Bond analysis framework

### 6.1 Analyze bonds as businesses
Buffett extends business analysis even to bond purchases.

**Reusable idea**
- A bond is a financial asset with business-like economics: capital invested, cash yield, downside risk, capped upside, inflation sensitivity.
- Bond investing should be judged against what an operating business with similar economics would cost.

**Metric focus**
- After-tax yield on cost
- Downside probability and severity
- Upside ceiling
- Inflation risk
- Comparison with operating business alternatives

**Evaluation questions**
- What after-tax return does this bond “business” earn on invested capital?
- How does that compare with private business opportunities?
- What is the maximum upside and the worst plausible downside?

---

### 6.2 Fixed upside plus meaningful downside demands a special hurdle
In WPPSS, Buffett accepts limited upside only because the earnings yield relative to cost is unusually attractive.

**Reusable idea**
- Bonds with capped upside must compensate with exceptional yield and acceptable risk.
- Do not accept equity-like downside with bond-like upside.

**Evaluation questions**
- Is the return sufficiently high for the downside and inflation risk?
- Are you being paid for illiquidity, uncertainty, and capped upside?
- Is this clearly better than other available uses of capital?

---

### 6.3 Long-term bonds are dangerous in inflationary regimes
Buffett is broadly negative on long-term bonds because fixed claims are vulnerable to inflation.

**Reusable idea**
- The real risk in long-duration fixed income is not just default; it is purchasing-power destruction.
- Long-term bonds require an unusually attractive setup to justify ownership.

**Evaluation questions**
- What happens to real returns under higher inflation?
- Is the portfolio exposed to a low-probability but severe inflation wipeout?
- Are better inflation-resilient businesses available at similar prices?

---

### 6.4 Investment should be businesslike even when market convention says otherwise
Buffett argues many bond mistakes would have been avoided by viewing them through a business lens.

**Reusable idea**
- Conventional popularity is not a substitute for economic sense.
- Ask whether you would knowingly buy a business earning such a return on capital under such terms.

---

## 7) Insurance analysis framework

### 7.1 In insurance, underwriting profitability is driven by pricing discipline and industry premium growth
Buffett highlights premium growth as a leading indicator for industry combined ratios.

**Reusable idea**
- In cyclical insurance markets, pricing adequacy and industry volume growth can predict underwriting profitability.
- Rapid volume growth is not always bad; it can reflect needed price increases after weak periods.

**Metric focus**
- Combined ratio
- Premium growth
- Catastrophe normalization
- Competitive intensity
- Price changes by line

**Evaluation questions**
- Is current premium growth sufficient to support stable or improving underwriting economics?
- Is pricing firming because capacity is leaving the market?
- Are competitors disciplined or desperate?

---

### 7.2 Financial strength is itself a competitive advantage in insurance
Because insurance buyers purchase a promise, the balance sheet is part of the product.

**Reusable idea**
- Superior capital strength can win business, especially long-tail business requiring decades of trust.
- In insurance, solvency credibility is commercially valuable.

**Metric focus**
- Capital adequacy
- Reserve strength
- Ability to withstand market stress plus underwriting losses
- Counterparty confidence

**Evaluation questions**
- Could this insurer honor claims under extreme combined market and underwriting stress?
- Is financial strength being preserved as policy?
- Does the company’s strength let it write business weaker rivals cannot?

---

### 7.3 Reserve estimates are inherently imprecise; reported earnings are only a rough draft
This is one of the strongest accounting-quality lessons in the letter.

**Reusable idea**
- Insurance earnings are highly judgment-laden because loss costs are estimated long before final settlement.
- Analysts must distrust precision in reported results.

**Metric focus**
- Reserve development
- Case reserves vs supplemental reserves
- Frequency and magnitude of adverse development
- One-year-later corrected underwriting results

**Evaluation questions**
- What is the company’s reserve development history?
- Is there a persistent optimistic bias?
- How much of current profitability depends on reserve assumptions?

---

### 7.4 Management candor about reserving errors is essential
Buffett openly reports prior under-reserving and invites shareholders to remain suspicious.

**Reusable idea**
- In reserving-heavy businesses, honesty about estimation error is a major quality marker.
- The absence of candor is dangerous.

**Evaluation questions**
- Does management disclose past errors clearly?
- Do they quantify prior reserve inadequacy?
- Is the tone honest or defensive?

---

### 7.5 Weak insurers can distort industry pricing before insolvency appears
Buffett describes insolvent insurers as “broke but flush” because they still receive cash before claims are paid.

**Reusable idea**
- Reported solvency can lag economic insolvency in insurance.
- Aggressive underpricing by weak competitors can depress industry returns and create contagion risk.

**Evaluation questions**
- Is industry pricing being set by unhealthy competitors trying to survive?
- How quickly do regulators or markets identify weak players?
- Could competitor failures create indirect costs via guarantee funds or market dislocation?

---

## 8) Accounting and reporting framework

### 8.1 Accounting classification can change without changing economics
Buffett disputes the accounting treatment of certain redemptions but emphasizes intrinsic value is unchanged.

**Reusable idea**
- Accounting form should never be confused with economic substance.
- Reclassifications may alter reported earnings while leaving actual value unchanged.

**Evaluation questions**
- Does the accounting treatment change cash, tax, ownership, or intrinsic value?
- Is management helping investors separate form from substance?
- Are you analyzing economics or reported labels?

---

### 8.2 Realized gains are noisy annually but meaningful cumulatively
Buffett says annual realized capital gains or losses are meaningless, while aggregate realized and unrealized gains over time are very important.

**Reusable idea**
- Single-year realization patterns are often arbitrary.
- Multi-year value creation across realized and unrealized gains is what matters.

**Evaluation questions**
- Are you overweighting one year’s gains or losses?
- What is the longer-term record of value creation from investments?
- Is management selling for economic reasons or reporting optics?

---

### 8.3 Separate operating performance from special items
Buffett isolates unusual asset sales and other special events from operating earnings.

**Reusable idea**
- Durable analysis requires separating core earning power from unusual transactions.

**Evaluation questions**
- What would normalized operating earnings be without special items?
- Are special items recurring in disguise?
- Does management clearly distinguish operating from non-operating results?

---

## 9) Dividend policy framework

### 9.1 Dividend policy should follow economics, not custom
Buffett criticizes rote payout ratios and inflation-linked dividend formulas that lack economic justification.

**Reusable idea**
- Dividend policy should be explained through owner economics.
- Retain only what can be used well; distribute the rest.

**Evaluation questions**
- Why exactly is this payout policy in owners’ interest?
- Is the policy driven by convention, signaling, comfort, or economics?
- Could owners redeploy the cash better themselves?

---

### 9.2 Shareholders should evaluate retention using the same logic management uses across divisions
Buffett points out that CEOs often demand rational payout logic from subsidiaries but fail to apply it to themselves.

**Reusable idea**
- Parent-company capital allocation should face the same hurdle as internal divisional allocation.

**Evaluation questions**
- Would management tolerate this rate of return from a subsidiary?
- If not, why should shareholders tolerate it at the parent?
- Is management applying one standard internally and another externally?

---

### 9.3 Good dividend policy is stable, but stability is not an excuse for bad retention
Buffett accepts that shareholders value predictability, but says withheld earnings must still “earn their keep.”

**Reusable idea**
- Dividend patterns should reflect long-term economics.
- Stable dividends are good; thoughtless retention is not.

---

## 10) Buffett acquisition criteria explicitly stated in the letter

Buffett’s “business wanted” section is effectively a direct screening template.

### Desired acquisition characteristics
1. **Large purchases**  
   At least meaningful earnings size.

2. **Demonstrated consistent earning power**  
   Not projections, not turnarounds.

3. **Good returns on equity with little or no debt**  
   Profitability plus balance-sheet quality.

4. **Management in place**  
   The business must run well without Buffett supplying operators.

5. **Simple business**  
   Avoid complexity beyond the buyer’s understanding.

6. **Price provided upfront**  
   Seller seriousness and efficient decision-making.

**Reusable screening checklist**
- Does the business have a long, consistent earnings record?
- Are returns on equity attractive without leverage doing the heavy lifting?
- Is current management strong enough to remain?
- Is the business easy to understand?
- Is the size meaningful?
- Is the price known and sensible?

---

## 11) Performance logic Buffett repeatedly uses

### 11.1 Compare economics to the best alternative use of capital
Every investment should be compared with what else the same capital could buy.

Examples in the letter:
- repurchases vs acquisitions
- bond purchases vs buying whole businesses
- retention vs dividends
- concentration vs diversification

**Core question**
- Is this the best available use of capital, not merely an acceptable one?

---

### 11.2 Focus on per-share improvement, not organizational expansion
Buffett cares about the wealth of owners, not the size of the corporate empire.

**Core question**
- Does this action improve the value of each owner’s share, or just enlarge the company?

---

### 11.3 Prefer economically strong businesses with shareholder-minded managers
Buffett repeatedly pairs two conditions:
- excellent economics
- shareholder-conscious management

He wants both.

**Core question**
- Is this a good business in good hands?

---

### 11.4 Distinguish franchise strength from managerial storytelling
Where market forces do not force high standards, management must self-impose them.

**Core question**
- Would this business still be admirable if management could get away with mediocrity?

---

### 11.5 Use history as evidence, but not as blind forecast
Past performance matters, but Buffett repeatedly adjusts expectations when size, environment, or economics change.

**Core question**
- What parts of the past are structural, and what parts cannot repeat?

---

## 12) Reusable Buffett-style evaluation questions

### Business economics
- Does the business earn attractive returns on capital without requiring heavy debt?
- How much incremental capital is required for growth?
- Is the company’s competitive advantage structural and durable?
- Are margins, pricing power, and customer value driven by genuine operating superiority?

### Management
- Are management’s actions visibly pro-shareholder?
- Do they repurchase shares only when value exceeds price?
- Do they stay within their circle of competence?
- Are they candid about mistakes and accounting uncertainty?
- Do they preserve franchise quality under cost pressure?

### Capital allocation
- Has each retained dollar historically created at least one dollar of market value?
- Is the core business masking poor allocation elsewhere?
- Should excess cash be reinvested, distributed, used for repurchases, or held?
- Is the company issuing stock intelligently or carelessly?

### Accounting quality
- Are reported earnings economically real or partially restricted?
- How much of earnings depends on accounting judgment?
- Are special items separated from operating performance?
- In reserving-heavy businesses, what is the history of adverse development?

### Valuation
- What is a conservative estimate of intrinsic value per share?
- How large is the gap between price and value?
- Does the current price imply absurdly low or high business returns?
- Are you buying a good business cheaply, or rationalizing a weak business because it looks cheap?

### Risk
- What is the downside if the thesis is wrong?
- Is the risk inflation, leverage, reserve inadequacy, competition, or management behavior?
- Does the balance sheet permit concentration?
- Are you exposed to permanent capital loss or just quotation volatility?

## 13) Compact framework summary

### What Buffett wants
- understandable businesses
- durable favorable economics
- strong returns on equity without heavy leverage
- honest and able managers
- disciplined capital allocation
- price materially below intrinsic value
- willingness to wait when these are absent

### What Buffett avoids
- growth that consumes too much capital
- management rhetoric unsupported by action
- acquisitions done for empire building
- bond-like returns with hidden inflation risk
- accounting comfort that ignores economic reality
- businesses outside the analyst’s competence
- diversification used to hide weak conviction

---

## 14) Most transferable rules from the 1984 letter

1. **Judge gains relative to capital employed.**
2. **Intrinsic value per share is the real scorecard.**
3. **Retain earnings only when each retained dollar is likely to create at least one dollar of owner value.**
4. **Repurchase shares only when price is materially below intrinsic value and the balance sheet is strong.**
5. **Treat stocks as businesses and bonds as unusual businesses.**
6. **Demand both good economics and good management.**
7. **Separate accounting form from economic substance.**
8. **In insurance, distrust precise earnings and study reserve development relentlessly.**
9. **Stay within the circle of competence.**
10. **Doing nothing is superior to lowering standards.**

## 15) One-page analyst template derived from the letter

### A. Business
- What does the company do, and is it simple enough to understand?
- What is the source of its competitive advantage?
- Are returns on equity/capital strong without leverage?
- How much incremental capital does growth require?

### B. Management
- Are they honest, candid, and owner-oriented?
- Have they demonstrated good capital allocation?
- Do they repurchase shares intelligently?
- Do they stay inside their competence?

### C. Economics
- What are normalized operating earnings?
- How much of earnings are truly distributable?
- Is the core business strong enough to support reinvestment?

### D. Capital allocation
- Best use of capital today: reinvest, repurchase, acquire, distribute, or hold?
- What has management’s historical record been on retained earnings?
- Is the company using a great business to subsidize poor investments?

### E. Valuation
- Conservative intrinsic value per share?
- Current discount/premium to intrinsic value?
- What assumptions would have to fail for permanent loss to occur?

### F. Risk
- Balance-sheet resilience?
- Inflation sensitivity?
- Accounting judgment risk?
- Competitive or regulatory threats?
- Concentration risk relative to financial strength?

---

## 16) Final takeaway

The 1984 letter is fundamentally about **economic realism**:

- measure performance by capital efficiency, not raw profits
- evaluate securities as businesses
- treat management behavior as observable through capital allocation
- distinguish accounting appearance from economic truth
- retain only capital that can be redeployed productively
- insist on a meaningful gap between value and price
- remain patient when opportunities do not qualify

That is the reusable Buffett framework embedded in this letter.
