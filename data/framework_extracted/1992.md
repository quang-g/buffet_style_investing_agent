# Warren Buffett Reusable Stock/Business Analysis Framework  
## Extracted from Berkshire Hathaway Shareholder Letter (1992)

Source letter: attached Berkshire Hathaway Chairman's Letter, dated March 1, 1993, covering 1992 results.

---

## Purpose of This Extraction

This document extracts the **reusable investment and business-analysis framework** embedded in Buffett's 1992 letter. It excludes year-specific narrative except where an example clarifies a general principle.

The goal is to preserve **repeatable analytical logic** that can later be used to evaluate a stock, a company, or an acquisition candidate in a Buffett-like way.

---

# 1) Core Objective Function

## 1.1 Maximize per-share intrinsic value, not size
Buffett's governing objective is **not growth for its own sake** and not corporate expansion as a standalone target. The real aim is:

- **Increase per-share intrinsic value**
- Use book value only as a **conservative but imperfect proxy**
- Reject transactions that increase corporate size but reduce owner value per share

### Reusable principle
A decision is good only if it increases **owner wealth per share**, not merely:
- total assets
- total earnings
- total market capitalization
- empire size
- management prestige

### Evaluation questions
- Does this action increase intrinsic value **per share**?
- Is management pursuing scale or value?
- Would existing owners be better off if the company simply did nothing instead?

---

## 1.2 Think in long-term compounding terms
Buffett frames capital allocation against a **multi-year compounding target**, not next-quarter optics.

### Reusable principle
Every major capital-allocation decision should be tested against its likely effect on **long-term earning power** and **future intrinsic value**, not just current reported earnings.

### Evaluation questions
- What will this decision do to normalized earnings power in 5-10 years?
- Is management allocating capital to maximize future owner earnings or current optics?
- Are today's decisions likely to produce better harvests later?

---

# 2) Share Issuance and Dilution Framework

## 2.1 Never issue shares unless you receive at least equal value
Buffett states a strict issuance policy: Berkshire will issue shares only when it receives **as much value as it gives**.

### Reusable principle
Equity is a valuable currency. Management should treat stock issuance as a sale of a partial ownership interest, not as "free capital."

### Practical test
Only issue shares when:
- the asset/business acquired is worth at least the intrinsic value of shares issued
- the transaction benefits continuing owners on a per-share basis

### Red flags
- stock issued to fund weak acquisitions
- acquisition terms that value the target above the acquirer on unjustified economics
- management fixation on expansion regardless of dilution quality

### Evaluation questions
- What is the company giving up in intrinsic value per share?
- Is stock being issued because it is overvalued, or because management lacks discipline?
- Would a rational owner swap this slice of the business for what is being received?

---

# 3) Acquisition Framework

## 3.1 Buy good businesses at fair prices, not fair businesses at good prices
This is one of the most important shifts Buffett describes.

### Reusable principle
A superior business bought at a reasonable price is usually better than a mediocre business bought cheaply.

### Why
Mediocre businesses often remain mediocre. Cheapness alone does not create quality. Bad economics tend to overpower optimistic deal logic.

### Evaluation questions
- Is this a genuinely good business or just a statistically cheap one?
- Are returns on capital structurally attractive?
- Does the business have durable advantages that make time your friend?

---

## 3.2 Avoid acquisition hunger
Buffett compares acquisition behavior to "frog-kissing": managers repeatedly overpay for low-quality businesses while hoping for transformation.

### Reusable principle
The desire to do deals is often more dangerous than the deals themselves.

### Red flags
- management under pressure to "do something"
- serial restructuring charges after optimistic acquisitions
- repeated claims that operational fixes will transform weak economics
- buyer urgency

### Better posture
Be:
- active
- interested
- open-minded

But do **not** be:
- rushed
- emotionally committed
- deal-hungry

### Evaluation questions
- Is management acting from discipline or from the need to deploy capital?
- Does the deal depend on a turnaround fantasy?
- Is the acquirer paying for hope rather than business quality?

---

## 3.3 Prefer businesses with excellent economics and trusted managers
Buffett explicitly celebrates acquisitions of businesses with:
- excellent economic characteristics
- management he likes, trusts, and admires

### Reusable principle
Buffett does not separate business quality from management quality. Both matter.

### Evaluation questions
- Does the business possess strong economics?
- Are the managers trustworthy?
- Are they competent operators and rational capital allocators?
- Would you want to own the business **with this management in place for a long time**?

---

## 3.4 Add-on acquisitions can be high-return, low-risk
Buffett notes that small bolt-on deals can work well because they enlarge the domain of already-proven managers.

### Reusable principle
Incremental acquisitions are more attractive when:
- they fit an existing system
- they extend distribution or product lines
- they are placed under proven operators

### Evaluation questions
- Is this deal strengthening a capable existing platform?
- Does the buyer already understand the economics?
- Are synergies real and simple rather than speculative?

---

# 4) Stock Selection Framework

## 4.1 Evaluate stocks as if buying the whole business
Buffett says marketable equities should be judged the same way as whole-business acquisitions.

### Core criteria
A stock should represent a business:
1. **you can understand**
2. with **favorable long-term prospects**
3. run by **honest and competent people**
4. available at **an attractive price**

This is one of the clearest reusable Buffett checklists.

### Evaluation questions
- Do I understand how this business makes money?
- Are long-term economics favorable?
- Is management both honest and competent?
- Is the price attractive relative to value?

---

## 4.2 "Value" and "growth" are not opposites
Buffett rejects the conventional separation between value investing and growth investing.

### Reusable principle
Growth is part of value. It is neither automatically good nor automatically bad.

### Implication
A high-growth company can be a value investment.
A low-multiple company can be a poor value investment.

### Evaluation questions
- What is the value impact of future growth?
- Does growth create value or destroy it?
- Is the market misclassifying the business because it confuses growth with value?

---

## 4.3 Conventional valuation multiples are not enough
Buffett warns that low P/B, low P/E, and high dividend yield do **not** determine whether something is cheap.

### Reusable principle
Surface multiples are shorthand, not valuation.

### Implication
A stock can be:
- expensive despite low multiples
- cheap despite high multiples

### Evaluation questions
- What cash will this business generate over its life?
- What reinvestment will be required?
- What quality of returns will future capital earn?
- Is the market focusing on crude multiples instead of economics?

---

## 4.4 Use discounted future cash flows as the valuation anchor
Buffett cites John Burr Williams' core formula:

> The value of any stock, bond or business is the discounted value of future cash inflows and outflows.

### Reusable principle
Intrinsic value is a **present value of future cash generation**, not an accounting label.

### Application
Evaluate:
- expected future owner cash flows
- duration of those cash flows
- reliability of those cash flows
- appropriate discount rate

### Evaluation questions
- What are the future "coupons" of this equity?
- How predictable are those cash flows?
- What discount rate is appropriate?
- What assumptions matter most to the valuation?

---

## 4.5 Predictability matters more than brilliance
Buffett emphasizes that Berkshire sticks to businesses it understands and avoids complex or rapidly changing businesses.

### Reusable principle
The key is not knowing everything; it is **accurately defining what you do not know**.

### Preferred business traits
- simple
- stable
- understandable
- economically legible

### Red flags
- complexity that prevents forecasting
- constant change
- fragile economics
- management stories substituting for clarity

### Evaluation questions
- Can future cash flows be estimated with reasonable confidence?
- Is the business simple enough to understand?
- Does complexity create genuine opportunity or just analyst illusion?

---

## 4.6 Margin of safety is foundational
Buffett restates Graham's principle as the cornerstone of investment success.

### Reusable principle
Even a good business should not be purchased without adequate discount to conservatively estimated value.

### Why
Valuation error is inevitable.
The future is uncertain.
Analysts can be wrong even when they are smart and experienced.

### Evaluation questions
- What is my estimated value range?
- How wrong could I be?
- What discount protects against forecast error, industry shocks, and managerial mistakes?

---

# 5) Business Quality Framework

## 5.1 Growth is valuable only if incremental capital earns attractive returns
This is among the most reusable ideas in the letter.

### Reusable principle
Growth benefits owners **only** when additional capital can be invested at attractive incremental returns.

### Strong version
Each additional dollar retained should create **more than one dollar of long-term market value**.

### Implications
Good growth:
- high-return reinvestment opportunities
- compounding economics
- limited need for external capital
- retained earnings create more value than dividends would

Bad growth:
- low-return reinvestment
- capital-hungry expansion
- more assets but poor economics
- owners fund growth that does not enrich them

### Evaluation questions
- What return does incremental capital earn?
- Does each retained dollar create more than one dollar of value?
- Would owners be better off if excess cash were distributed instead?

---

## 5.2 The best business can deploy large incremental capital at high returns
Buffett identifies an ideal business as one that can use **large amounts** of additional capital at **very high rates of return** over time.

### Reusable principle
The rarest and most valuable businesses combine:
- high returns on capital
- long reinvestment runway
- durability

### Why this matters
Many good businesses earn high returns but cannot absorb much additional capital.
Those businesses may still be good, but their best use of earnings may be payouts or repurchases.

### Evaluation questions
- Is this a high-return business with a long runway?
- Can it reinvest meaningful sums internally at high rates?
- If not, will management distribute capital rationally?

---

## 5.3 The worst business requires more capital at low returns
Buffett defines the worst business as one that must or will keep absorbing capital while earning poor returns.

### Red flags
- capital intensity without pricing power
- recurring reinvestment just to stand still
- growth that demands cash but yields little owner value
- structurally weak economics masked by scale

### Evaluation questions
- Does this business need heavy capital just to maintain its position?
- Are new investments attractive or compulsory?
- Is growth actually a shareholder burden?

---

## 5.4 High reported earnings are less impressive if they require large capital
Buffett praises Scott Fetzer not just for earnings, but for producing them with little equity capital and limited asset intensity.

### Reusable principle
Always relate earnings to the capital required to produce them.

### What to favor
- high earnings relative to equity employed
- low need for fixed assets and inventory
- ability to remit cash upstream
- earnings growth without proportional capital growth

### Evaluation questions
- How much equity capital is required?
- How asset-light is the model?
- Can the business distribute most of its earnings while still growing?

---

# 6) Management Framework

## 6.1 Management integrity and competence are both required
Buffett's stock and acquisition criteria explicitly include "honest and competent people."

### Reusable principle
A talented but untrustworthy manager is unacceptable.
An honest but incompetent manager can still destroy value.

### Evaluation questions
- Is management candid?
- Do words and actions match?
- Have they allocated capital intelligently?
- Are incentives aligned with owners?

---

## 6.2 Strategy should be clearly articulated, rational, focused, and urgent
Buffett's shift from arbitrage to long-term ownership in General Dynamics came from observing leadership with:
- a clearly articulated strategy
- rationality
- focus
- urgency
- strong execution

### Reusable principle
Good management is visible in a coherent strategic logic plus observable execution.

### Evaluation questions
- Is management strategy clear and economically rational?
- Do actions follow the stated strategy?
- Is there evidence of disciplined execution?
- Are results improving because of managerial skill rather than luck?

---

## 6.3 Great managers matter more in hard businesses, but bad economics still dominate
Buffett notes that difficult industries require more managerial skill, yet even strong managers may only achieve survival rather than prosperity.

### Reusable principle
Do not confuse managerial excellence with favorable business economics.

### Implication
A great manager in a bad business may prevent disaster but still fail to generate attractive owner returns.

### Evaluation questions
- Is success coming from business quality or heroic management?
- Would an average manager still do fine here?
- Is management merely fighting industry structure?

---

## 6.4 Evaluate managerial capital allocation, not just operating ability
Buffett repeatedly ties value creation to how managers deploy retained earnings, structure financing, and pursue acquisitions.

### Evaluation questions
- Does management reinvest only when returns are attractive?
- Do they repurchase shares intelligently?
- Are they dilution-conscious?
- Do they avoid leverage unless it clearly benefits owners?

---

# 7) Performance Measurement Framework

## 7.1 Look through accounting to underlying economic earnings
Buffett prefers "look-through earnings" to raw GAAP earnings.

### Reusable principle
Economic ownership matters even when accounting does not fully reflect it.

### Buffett's logic
True owner earnings should include:
1. reported operating earnings from controlled businesses
2. Berkshire's share of retained earnings at major investees
3. minus a hypothetical tax adjustment

### Broader takeaway
Reported earnings can understate the economics of partially owned, high-quality businesses that retain earnings productively.

### Evaluation questions
- What earnings belong economically to owners but are absent from reported EPS?
- How much value is being created inside investees?
- Are retained earnings being used intelligently by management teams you own through minority stakes?

---

## 7.2 Use metrics management itself would use if buying the business
Buffett says he wants to give shareholders the same financial information he and Munger use in evaluating Berkshire.

### Reusable principle
When analyzing a company, prefer internal economic measures over external presentational conventions, provided they are honest and decision-useful.

### Evaluation questions
- What would a rational buyer of the whole business care about?
- Which reported figures obscure economic reality?
- What adjustments better reflect true earning power?

---

## 7.3 Short-term volatility is acceptable if long-term economics are superior
Buffett openly accepts annual volatility.

### Reusable principle
A rational investor should tolerate short-term fluctuations when they improve long-term expected return.

### Memorable framing
Focus on **slugging percentage, not batting average**.

### Meaning
Do not judge an investment process by smoothness.
Judge it by long-term payoff quality.

### Evaluation questions
- Is volatility coming from quotation changes or business deterioration?
- Does tolerance for volatility create better opportunities?
- Is the investment process designed for long-term expectancy rather than cosmetic stability?

---

## 7.4 Do not excuse endless underperformance with "long-term focus"
Buffett explicitly warns against managers who invoke long-term thinking while repeatedly failing to produce results.

### Reusable principle
Long-term thinking is not a license for chronic non-delivery.

### Evaluation questions
- Are earlier investments now showing payoffs?
- Has management earned the right to request patience?
- Is "long-term" being used as a shield for weak economics?

---

# 8) Insurance and Float Framework

## 8.1 In insurance, the key metric is cost of float
Buffett says what counts in insurance is the **cost of funds developed from insurance**, i.e., the cost of float.

### Reusable principle
Insurance should be viewed not just as underwriting plus investment income separately, but as a financing business where float is valuable only if acquired cheaply or profitably.

### Core concepts
- **Float** = policyholder funds held temporarily by the insurer
- **Cost of float** = underwriting loss divided relative to average float

### Interpretation
- underwriting profit => float costs less than zero
- modest underwriting loss may still be acceptable if float is cheaper than alternative funding sources
- chronic expensive float destroys value

### Evaluation questions
- Is float growing?
- What is its cost?
- Is float durable?
- Is underwriting discipline real or subsidized by optimism?

---

## 8.2 Insurance economics should be compared with alternative funding costs
Buffett compares cost of float with long-term government bond yields.

### Reusable principle
Float is attractive only if it is cheaper than other sources of funds.

### Evaluation questions
- Is the insurer obtaining funds more cheaply than normal financing alternatives?
- Is the cost advantage persistent?
- Is management sacrificing underwriting discipline to grow float?

---

## 8.3 Underwriting discipline matters more than volume
Buffett notes Berkshire rejects more than 98% of reinsurance proposals.

### Reusable principle
The willingness to say no is a core competitive advantage in risky industries.

### Evaluation questions
- What percentage of opportunities does management reject?
- Is premium growth driven by discipline or by appetite for volume?
- Are prices adequate for the risk assumed?

---

## 8.4 Scale plus skill is powerful only when pricing is right
Berkshire's advantage in super-cat insurance is a combination of:
- exceptional capital strength
- willingness to assume large risks
- superior underwriting judgment

But Buffett adds: they remain active **only when prices are appropriate**.

### Reusable principle
Capacity alone is not an edge unless paired with discipline.

### Evaluation questions
- Does the company have a true advantage in balance sheet or capability?
- Is that advantage being monetized rationally?
- Does management walk away when pricing is inadequate?

---

## 8.5 Catastrophe risk cannot be priced by naive extrapolation
Buffett warns that catastrophe underwriting cannot simply rely on past averages because exposure changes and tail events can surprise.

### Reusable principle
In nonlinear risk businesses, historical data alone is insufficient.

### Implications
Adjust for:
- changing exposure concentrations
- changing asset values
- structural shifts in risk environment
- low-frequency, high-severity events

### Evaluation questions
- Is management relying too heavily on historical averages?
- Have exposure bases changed?
- Does pricing reflect tail risk and uncertainty?

---

## 8.6 Conservative revenue recognition can better reflect risk reality
Buffett defers recognition of super-cat premiums until later because risk is back-end loaded.

### Reusable principle
When risk is uneven over time, conservative accounting may better approximate economic reality than mechanical straight-lining.

### Evaluation questions
- Does accounting match the true timing of risk?
- Are reported profits front-loaded relative to economic exposure?
- Are management adjustments conservative or promotional?

---

# 9) Capital Allocation Framework

## 9.1 Allocate capital based on future earning power
Buffett says current decisions are guided by what will maximize look-through earnings years ahead.

### Reusable principle
Capital allocation should be evaluated based on the **future stream of owner earnings** it creates.

### Evaluation questions
- Which use of capital offers the best long-term owner return?
- Is management comparing all uses of capital rationally?
- Are they acting like portfolio managers of owner capital?

---

## 9.2 Repurchases and dividends can be superior to reinvestment
Buffett notes that many high-return businesses require little capital and thus may best serve owners by paying out earnings or repurchasing stock.

### Reusable principle
Retained earnings are justified only when management can deploy them at attractive rates.

### Evaluation questions
- Should this business retain capital at all?
- Would repurchases create more value?
- Is dividend retention a habit or an economically justified choice?

---

## 9.3 Maintain aversion to debt, especially short-term debt
Buffett states a general dislike of debt, particularly short-term debt, though he allows modest, well-structured debt that clearly benefits shareholders.

### Reusable principle
Leverage should be exceptional, not habitual.

### Good debt characteristics
- modest
- long-dated or well structured
- clearly beneficial to owners
- matched to business resilience

### Red flags
- short-term funding for long-term assets
- leverage masking mediocre economics
- debt used to support weak capital allocation

### Evaluation questions
- Why is debt necessary here?
- What happens under stress?
- Is management using debt because the business is great, or because it isn't?

---

# 10) Secondary Market vs New Issue Framework

## 10.1 Secondary markets can offer bargains because they are periodically irrational
Buffett says the secondary market is periodically ruled by mass folly and therefore can offer securities at far below business value.

### Reusable principle
Mispricing is more likely in markets where forced sellers and crowd emotion set prices.

### Evaluation questions
- Is current pricing driven by business value or market emotion?
- Is there temporary dislocation creating a bargain?
- Are we looking at a clearing price or a rational value?

---

## 10.2 New issues and negotiated deals usually favor sellers
Buffett contrasts this with the new-issue market, where sellers usually control timing and are unlikely to offer bargains.

### Reusable principle
When the seller controls timing and can wait, bargains are less likely.

### Evaluation questions
- Who has timing power in this transaction?
- Why is this security being offered now?
- Is the seller choosing to transact because the terms are favorable to them?

---

# 11) Accounting and Economic Reality Framework

## 11.1 Accounting should serve economic truth, not cosmetic presentation
Buffett repeatedly attacks accounting that obscures real economics.

### Reusable principle
Always distinguish between:
- accounting appearance
- economic reality

### Application areas in this letter
- deferred taxes
- post-retirement liabilities
- goodwill/purchase accounting
- stock-option expense
- investee retained earnings

### Evaluation questions
- What liabilities are real even if under-recognized?
- What earnings are reported but low quality?
- What value is omitted by formal accounting treatment?

---

## 11.2 A cost is a cost even if no cash changes hands
Buffett's stock-option discussion generalizes into a broader rule.

### Reusable principle
Economic cost does not disappear simply because it is hard to measure or is paid in non-cash form.

### Evaluation questions
- What compensation or liability is economically real but under-expensed?
- Is management benefiting from accounting conventions that flatter earnings?
- What owner dilution or burden is being ignored?

---

## 11.3 Hidden or deferred liabilities can destroy value
Buffett is wary of post-retirement health obligations and says such liabilities can be severe enough to threaten competitiveness.

### Reusable principle
A business with large, hard-to-control future obligations may be far less valuable than reported earnings suggest.

### Evaluation questions
- What long-tail obligations exist off the income statement?
- Are benefit promises capped or open-ended?
- Could hidden liabilities impair future competitiveness or solvency?

---

## 11.4 Honest estimation is better than false precision or omission
Buffett argues that hard-to-measure costs should still be estimated and recorded.

### Reusable principle
Imperfect estimation is superior to pretending a real cost does not exist.

### Evaluation questions
- Is management confronting uncertainty honestly?
- Are estimates conservative and credible?
- Is omission being used to inflate performance?

---

# 12) Corporate Structure and Overhead Framework

## 12.1 Low headquarters cost preserves owner value
Buffett points out that excessive corporate expense not only hurts earnings but also reduces capital value.

### Reusable principle
Corporate overhead is not neutral; it is a recurring tax on owner returns.

### Implication
Lean structures often outperform bloated bureaucracies.

### Evaluation questions
- How much of operating earnings is consumed by headquarters?
- Does central overhead create real value?
- Is complexity justified?

---

## 12.2 Simplicity is often a performance advantage
Buffett prefers simple, low-cost organizational models.

### Reusable principle
Organizational simplicity can improve both economics and managerial effectiveness.

### Evaluation questions
- Is this business operationally lean?
- Is bureaucracy impairing performance?
- Could a simpler structure unlock value?

---

# 13) Owner Alignment Framework

## 13.1 Shareholder base quality matters
Buffett values shareholders who behave like rational long-term owners.

### Reusable principle
Management should cultivate an owner-oriented shareholder culture, because investor base quality affects trading behavior, capital-allocation freedom, and corporate stability.

### Evaluation questions
- Does management communicate to attract long-term owners or short-term traders?
- Are policies designed for owner quality or promotional popularity?
- Is turnover imposing frictional costs on owners?

---

## 13.2 Avoid actions that attract the wrong investor base
Buffett argues that stock splits can modestly degrade shareholder quality.

### Reusable principle
Not every action that increases accessibility improves ownership quality.

### Evaluation questions
- Will this policy attract more rational owners or more speculative activity?
- Are management decisions optimizing for intrinsic value or stock-market theater?

# 14) Practical Buffett Checklist Derived from the 1992 Letter

## A. Business Understanding
- Is the business understandable?
- Is it simple and relatively stable?
- Can future cash flows be estimated with reasonable confidence?

## B. Business Economics
- Are long-term prospects favorable?
- Does the business earn high returns on capital?
- What are the returns on **incremental** capital?
- Does growth create or destroy value?
- Is the business capital-light or capital-hungry?
- Can it distribute cash without harming its position?

## C. Management
- Are management honest and competent?
- Do they think like owners?
- Are they disciplined in acquisitions and share issuance?
- Do they communicate candidly?
- Is their strategy clear, rational, focused, and well executed?

## D. Valuation
- What is the discounted value of future owner cash flows?
- What assumptions drive that valuation?
- Is there a sufficient margin of safety?
- Is the market using crude multiples instead of business reality?

## E. Capital Allocation
- Are retained earnings creating more than one dollar of value per dollar retained?
- Should capital be reinvested, distributed, or used for repurchases?
- Is debt modest and well structured?
- Is equity issuance done only at fair-or-better value exchange?

## F. Accounting Reality
- What economics are missing from GAAP?
- What liabilities are under-recognized?
- Are non-cash costs being ignored?
- What owner earnings are obscured by accounting conventions?

## G. Special Case: Insurance
- Is float growing?
- What is the cost of float?
- Is underwriting disciplined?
- Are risks priced conservatively?
- Does management reject most unattractive business?

# 15) Performance Logic Embedded in the Letter

This letter implies a consistent performance logic:

1. **Intrinsic value grows when owner earnings grow.**
2. **Owner earnings grow when capital is allocated to high-return uses.**
3. **Growth is good only if incremental returns are attractive.**
4. **Management quality determines both operating outcomes and capital-allocation outcomes.**
5. **Accounting numbers must be adjusted toward economic reality.**
6. **Volatility is acceptable if long-term economic expectancy is high.**
7. **Mistakes usually come from overpaying, overestimating, overcomplicating, or ignoring hidden costs.**
8. **A few sound decisions, combined with avoidance of major errors, are enough for excellent long-term investing results.**

---

# 16) Compressed Framework for Reuse in a Stock Analysis System

Use the following decision template when analyzing a stock or business:

## Step 1: Understandability
- Can I understand the business model, drivers, and risks?

## Step 2: Economic Quality
- Are long-term economics favorable?
- What are return on capital and return on incremental capital?
- Is growth value-creating?

## Step 3: Management Quality
- Are managers honest, competent, and owner-oriented?
- Have they allocated capital well?

## Step 4: Earnings Reality
- What are normalized owner earnings?
- What accounting adjustments are required?
- Are there hidden liabilities or omitted economic costs?

## Step 5: Valuation
- What is the discounted value of future owner cash flows?
- What margin of safety exists?

## Step 6: Capital Allocation Outlook
- Can the business reinvest at high returns?
- If not, will cash be distributed intelligently?

## Step 7: Risk of Major Mistake
- What could permanently impair capital?
- Where might I be wrong?
- Does the purchase price protect against that?

# 17) Most Reusable Buffett Ideas from This Letter

If only a handful of ideas are retained from the 1992 letter, these are the highest-value ones:

1. **Buy good businesses at fair prices rather than fair businesses at good prices.**
2. **Evaluate stocks as partial interests in whole businesses.**
3. **Value and growth are not opposites; growth is a component of value.**
4. **Growth adds value only when incremental capital earns attractive returns.**
5. **The best business can deploy large incremental capital at high returns over time.**
6. **Stick to understandable, stable businesses and define your circle of competence honestly.**
7. **Demand a margin of safety.**
8. **Look through accounting to underlying economic earnings and liabilities.**
9. **Capital allocation should maximize long-term owner earnings, not reported short-term optics.**
10. **Volatility is tolerable; permanent capital impairment is not.**

## End note
This extraction is intended as a reusable knowledge-base artifact, not a summary of Berkshire's 1992 activities. It captures the parts of the letter that can be generalized into a repeatable Buffett-style framework for analyzing companies, securities, acquisitions, management teams, and capital-allocation decisions.
