# Berkshire Hathaway 1986 Shareholder Letter — Reusable Buffett Analysis Framework

Source letter: Berkshire Hathaway Chairman's Letter (1986 reporting year, dated February 27, 1987)

## Purpose of this extraction

This document extracts the **reusable stock/business analysis framework** embedded in Buffett's 1986 Berkshire Hathaway shareholder letter. It excludes year-specific narrative except where that narrative reveals a repeatable analytical rule.

---

## 1) Core meta-framework: what Buffett is actually trying to optimize

### A. Increase **intrinsic business value per share**, not just accounting net worth
**Framework principle**
- Book value is an accounting reference point, but **intrinsic business value** is the real objective.
- Book value can sometimes be a rough proxy, but only when the gap between accounting value and economic value is relatively stable.

**Why it matters**
- Investors should judge performance by whether management is compounding **per-share intrinsic value**, not merely growing assets, reported earnings, or total size.

**Metric / signal**
- Per-share book value as a rough proxy only.
- Evidence of unrecorded economic value creation.
- Stability or widening of the gap between book value and intrinsic value.

**Evaluation questions**
- Is book value a useful proxy here, or does it materially misstate economic value?
- What important value is being created that accounting does not capture?
- Is management increasing value per share or merely increasing scale?

---

### B. The two central jobs of top management are:
1. **Select and retain exceptional operating managers**
2. **Allocate capital intelligently**

**Framework principle**
- For a decentralized company, operating excellence and capital allocation are distinct skills.
- Great businesses can be damaged by poor capital allocation even if operations are strong.

**Performance logic**
- If operating subsidiaries generate substantial cash and require little incremental capital, the parent company's long-term outcome will depend disproportionately on reinvestment quality.

**Evaluation questions**
- Does management excel at operations, capital allocation, or both?
- How much of future value creation depends on capital allocation rather than operations?
- Is retained capital being deployed productively?

---

## 2) Business-quality framework

### A. Prefer businesses that can grow earnings with **little additional capital**
**Framework principle**
- A superior business can improve earnings materially while using little incremental capital.
- This creates economic goodwill that may not appear on the balance sheet.

**Why it matters**
- The best businesses are not just profitable; they are **capital-light relative to their earnings power**.
- Such businesses produce high incremental returns and free up cash for redeployment.

**Metric / signal**
- High returns on capital employed.
- Low incremental capital required to maintain competitiveness and growth.
- Earnings growth without commensurate growth in net tangible assets.

**Evaluation questions**
- How much incremental capital is needed for each extra dollar of earnings?
- Can the business grow while remaining capital-light?
- Is reported asset growth necessary, or is value being built through franchise strength?

---

### B. Strong market position + high returns on capital + great management is the ideal combination
**Framework principle**
- Buffett repeatedly highlights businesses with:
  - strong market position,
  - high returns on capital employed,
  - and unusually capable operating managers.

**Performance logic**
- Durable economics come from the interaction of competitive strength and management quality, not from either alone.

**Evaluation questions**
- Does the company have a defensible position in its market?
- Are returns on capital structurally high or just cyclically elevated?
- Is management operationally excellent in a way that strengthens the moat?

---

### C. Customer value and operational discipline can coexist
**Framework principle**
- Cost control is desirable, but not if it damages the product or franchise.
- A strong business can preserve customer value while maintaining margins.

**Illustrated logic from Buffalo News / See's**
- Maintain editorial quality rather than optimize short-term margins.
- Maintain product personality, service quality, and fair pricing rather than exploit the franchise.

**Evaluation questions**
- Is margin improvement being achieved by genuine efficiency or by underinvesting in the franchise?
- What core customer value is non-negotiable?
- Is management protecting long-term customer attachment?

---

### D. A great business often has a distinctive **consumer or economic “personality”**
**Framework principle**
- Exceptional businesses are often differentiated by an intangible identity customers feel: trust, habit, quality, service, reputation, or superior economics.

**Examples implied**
- See's: product personality + controlled distribution + service.
- GEICO: structural cost advantage.
- NFM: reputation for lowest prices and vast selection.

**Evaluation questions**
- What makes customers specifically prefer this business?
- Is the advantage emotional, behavioral, cost-based, distribution-based, or brand-based?
- Can competitors copy the advantage, or only admire it?

---

## 3) Management framework

### A. The best managers are **owner-minded**
**Framework principle**
- The highest compliment for a manager is that they “think like owners.”
- Buffett values managers who love the business, pursue excellence for its own sake, and treat capital rationally.

**Signals**
- Passion for the business independent of financial need.
- Long-term orientation.
- Frugality and intensity.
- Rational, unsupervised decision-making.

**Evaluation questions**
- Would this manager still perform well if already wealthy?
- Does the manager behave like a steward of capital?
- Can the business run well without headquarters interference?

---

### B. Great top management often adds value by **not interfering**
**Framework principle**
- When the operating manager is exceptional, HQ should avoid needless directives.
- Decentralization works if managers are able, honest, and passionate.

**Performance logic**
- Bureaucracy is not a substitute for talent and character.
- The span of control depends more on manager quality than org-chart theory.

**Evaluation questions**
- Is the parent adding insight or just adding friction?
- Does the company rely on systems because it lacks exceptional people?
- How much managerial complexity is self-inflicted?

---

### C. Character matters as much as competence
**Framework principle**
- Buffett wants to work only with people he likes and admires.
- Deceitful, inept, or disengaged people impose nonlinear managerial costs.

**Why it matters**
- Talent without integrity increases hidden risk.
- High-character managers enable decentralization, trust, and scale.

**Evaluation questions**
- Are management incentives aligned with owners?
- Is management straightforward in discussing mistakes and uncertainty?
- Would this team be trusted with autonomy?

---

## 4) Capital allocation framework

### A. Retained earnings are valuable only if reinvested at attractive rates
**Framework principle**
- A business that earns high returns and retains all earnings can compound rapidly, but only if retained capital is deployed well.
- If retained earnings are reinvested unproductively, economics deteriorate quickly.

**Performance logic**
- The more a company earns and retains, the more capital allocation drives long-term outcomes.

**Evaluation questions**
- What is management doing with retained earnings?
- Are new investments meeting or exceeding the returns of the existing business?
- Is cash piling up because management lacks ideas?

---

### B. Size is the enemy of extraordinary returns
**Framework principle**
- As capital base grows, finding opportunities large enough to matter becomes harder.
- Large numbers force a shift from easy wins to scarce, high-quality major decisions.

**Performance logic**
- A larger company needs very large good ideas to sustain prior compounding rates.

**Evaluation questions**
- Has scale reduced the opportunity set?
- What size must a new investment be to matter?
- Are return expectations realistic given current scale?

---

### C. Sometimes the correct capital allocation decision is to do little
**Framework principle**
- Paying down debt or holding cash is acceptable when attractive opportunities are absent.
- Forced action is inferior to disciplined inactivity.

**Evaluation questions**
- Is management patient when markets are unattractive?
- Does the company act only to avoid looking idle?
- Are they preserving optionality for better opportunities?

---

## 5) Acquisition framework

### A. Buffett's explicit acquisition criteria
He states that Berkshire looks for:

1. **Large purchases**
   - at least **$10 million of after-tax earnings**
2. **Demonstrated consistent earning power**
   - not projections
   - not turnarounds
3. **Good returns on equity with little or no debt**
4. **Management in place**
   - Berkshire cannot supply it
5. **Simple businesses**
   - avoid businesses requiring technical understanding beyond Berkshire's circle of competence
6. **An offering price**
   - price must be known early

**Reusable screen**
- Size sufficient to matter
- Proven economics
- Sensible leverage
- Existing managerial quality
- Simplicity / understandability
- Seller realism on price

---

### B. Buffett prefers negotiated deals, not auctions or unfriendly takeovers
**Framework principle**
- Attractive acquisition environments are those where seller goals align with Berkshire's style: permanence, trust, autonomy, confidentiality, and speed.
- Auction dynamics often impair value.

**Why it matters**
- Process quality affects price and future relationship quality.

**Evaluation questions**
- Is this deal being sold competitively?
- Why does the seller want this buyer?
- Is the business choosing its long-term home, or merely chasing the highest bidder?

---

### C. The “right business” includes the “right people”
**Framework principle**
- Buffett is not buying only economics; he is buying into a long-term human partnership.

**Signals**
- Family or management wants to remain invested.
- Multi-generational continuity exists.
- Seller values permanence and autonomy over maximum price.

**Evaluation questions**
- Do the sellers want a partner or just liquidity?
- Will key managers stay and thrive post-acquisition?
- Is cultural fit a hidden source of value?

---

### D. Plant visits are secondary; economic characteristics and people matter more
**Framework principle**
- Buffett does not rely on operational theater or facility tours for conviction.
- He prioritizes understanding competitive economics and managerial quality.

**Evaluation questions**
- Am I being persuaded by tangible impressions that do not drive economics?
- What are the real strengths and weaknesses of the business model?
- What matters more here: appearance, process, or economic structure?

---

## 6) Stock-investing framework

### A. A stock is attractive only when a good business with good management sells well below intrinsic value
**Framework principle**
- Buffett wants:
  1. good economics,
  2. good management,
  3. price well below intrinsic business value.

**Evaluation questions**
- Is the business high quality?
- Is management able and trustworthy?
- Is the discount to intrinsic value large enough?

---

### B. No market forecasting
**Framework principle**
- Buffett explicitly rejects predicting short- or intermediate-term market direction.
- Market timing is replaced by valuation discipline.

**Evaluation questions**
- Am I making a valuation decision or a market forecast?
- Is my thesis dependent on macro prediction rather than business value?

---

### C. Use crowd psychology, but do not try to time it
**Framework principle**
- Fear and greed are permanent features of markets.
- The investor should be **behaviorally prepared**, not temporally predictive.

**Reusable rule**
- Be fearful when others are greedy.
- Be greedy when others are fearful.

**Important nuance**
- This is not a slogan about being contrarian for its own sake.
- It is a rule about **valuation opportunity created by emotional extremes**.

**Evaluation questions**
- Is current pricing driven by euphoria or fear?
- Are rewards becoming uncoupled from business performance?
- Is pessimism creating prices below intrinsic value?

---

### D. Stocks cannot outperform businesses indefinitely
**Framework principle**
- Over time, shareholder returns are tethered to underlying business economics.
- Market enthusiasm can temporarily break the link, but cannot repeal it.

**Performance logic**
- Aggregate investor returns must lag aggregate business returns after fees and frictional costs.

**Evaluation questions**
- Are market prices implying business performance that is unlikely to occur?
- Is multiple expansion doing all the work?
- Are expectations detached from underlying economics?

---

### E. Preferred holding period is effectively permanent for exceptional businesses
**Framework principle**
- If Berkshire would not sell a wholly owned great business simply because it became overpriced, it should not reflexively sell a great stock holding for the same reason.
- Great businesses are to be owned, not traded around.

**Important nuance**
- Buffett is not saying price never matters.
- He is saying great assets with exceptional economics and managers should not be treated as trading inventory.

**Evaluation questions**
- Is this a business to own or just a security to trade?
- Would I want to own the whole business under similar economics?
- Am I letting market activity override business quality?

---

## 7) Insurance framework

### A. Insurance economics must be understood through **combined ratio + float**
**Framework principle**
- Underwriting profitability is measured by the combined ratio.
- Overall insurance economics depend on both underwriting result and investment income earned on float.

**Metric**
- Combined ratio:
  - below 100 = underwriting profit
  - above 100 = underwriting loss
- Float quality and cost

**Evaluation questions**
- Is underwriting profitable?
- If underwriting loses money, is float still low-cost or profitable overall?
- Is float being invested intelligently?

---

### B. Insurance is often a commodity business; industry structure drives results
**Framework principle**
- In commodity-like insurance lines, pricing improves only when capacity is constrained.
- High profits attract capital, which restores excess capacity and destroys pricing.

**Performance logic**
- The cycle is structural:
  1. shortage → high prices/profits
  2. new capital floods in
  3. capacity expands
  4. competition intensifies
  5. prices fall
  6. bad underwriting returns follow

**Evaluation questions**
- Is current insurance profitability structural or cyclical?
- Is capital entering the industry aggressively?
- Are prices adequate relative to loss trends?

---

### C. Social and judicial inflation can outrun general inflation
**Framework principle**
- Claim-cost inflation may materially exceed ordinary inflation.
- Insurers must price for true loss-cost trends, not CPI-style comfort.

**Evaluation questions**
- What is happening to claims severity and legal environment?
- Is premium growth keeping pace with underlying loss-cost growth?
- Are reported margins masking under-reserving or underpricing?

---

### D. Price discipline matters more than volume stability
**Framework principle**
- Berkshire accepts volume swings rather than write underpriced business.
- Shrinking when prices are irrational is a strength, not a weakness.

**Evaluation questions**
- Does management sacrifice underwriting standards to preserve volume?
- Can the insurer tolerate cyclical contraction without layoffs or panic?
- Is growth coming from opportunity or from price foolishness?

---

### E. Reserve accuracy is crucial; euphemisms should be distrusted
**Framework principle**
- “Reserve strengthening” is often just late recognition of earlier under-reserving.
- Insurance liabilities are estimation-heavy; honesty and conservatism matter.

**Signals**
- Frequent adverse reserve development
- Euphemistic communication
- Management overconfidence in liability estimates

**Evaluation questions**
- How reliable is reserve setting?
- Does management candidly discuss estimation error?
- Are past reserves proving conservative or deficient?

---

### F. An insurer needs multiple edges at once
**Buffett's implied formula**
To prosper in insurance, it helps to have:
- the right attitude,
- superior capital position,
- strong personnel,
- and an investment edge in float deployment.

**Evaluation questions**
- What real edge does this insurer have?
- Is its advantage structural, human, or merely cyclical?
- Can it survive unpleasant surprises?

---

## 8) Moat framework

### A. A moat is a structural advantage that protects economic profits
**Framework principle**
- Buffett describes GEICO's low cost structure as a moat around a valuable castle.
- The moat is not branding rhetoric; it is an **economic advantage that competitors struggle to match**.

**Metric / signal**
- Persistently lower operating cost ratio than peers
- Ability to maintain service and underwriting standards while lowering cost
- Long-lived share or profitability advantage

**Evaluation questions**
- What exactly is the moat?
- Is it cost, brand, switching costs, distribution, regulation, network, or habit?
- Is management widening the moat, or merely benefiting from it?

---

### B. Management should widen the moat, not just enjoy it
**Framework principle**
- A franchise is strongest when management continually reinforces the advantage.

**Evaluation questions**
- Is management actively improving the structural edge?
- Are efficiency gains being reinvested into stronger positioning?
- Is the moat stable, widening, or eroding?

---

## 9) Fixed-income and opportunity-cost framework

### A. Investment choice is relative, not absolute
**Framework principle**
- Sometimes a mediocre asset is purchased because it is the least objectionable among available alternatives.
- Opportunity cost governs rational choice.

**Evaluation questions**
- Compared to cash, bonds, arbitrage, and equities, what is best now?
- Is this investment genuinely attractive, or just the least bad option?

---

### B. Long-term bonds are dangerous when inflation risk is underappreciated
**Framework principle**
- Long-duration fixed income is vulnerable when future currency purchasing power is uncertain.

**Evaluation questions**
- What is embedded inflation risk?
- Am I lending long at a rate that assumes monetary stability?
- Is nominal safety disguising real risk?

---

### C. Arbitrage should be selective, publicly announced, and size-conscious
**Framework principle**
- Arbitrage can be a short-term parking place for capital, but only when the facts are clear and downside is limited.
- Buffett avoids speculative deal-betting.

**Evaluation rules**
- Focus on announced deals
- Prefer large, understandable situations
- Expect modest profits, aim for few disappointments

**Evaluation questions**
- Is this arbitrage based on clear facts or speculation?
- What is the downside if the deal breaks?
- Is this better than Treasury bills after risk?

---

## 10) Accounting and valuation framework

### A. GAAP is a starting point, not the truth about value
**Framework principle**
- Accounting records transactions; it does not evaluate business worth.
- Investors and managers must move from accounting figures to economic reality.

**Evaluation questions**
- What do the accounts show?
- What do the economics actually imply?
- Which reported numbers are informative, and which are distortive?

---

### B. Purchase-price accounting can distort reported earnings without changing economics
**Framework principle**
- Two identical businesses can report meaningfully different GAAP earnings solely because of acquisition accounting.
- Therefore, valuation cannot rely mechanically on reported earnings.

**Evaluation questions**
- How much of current expense is a real economic cost versus an accounting artifact?
- Are purchase-price adjustments obscuring operating earning power?
- Would the same business appear more attractive if owned by someone else?

---

### C. “Owner earnings” is the relevant valuation concept
**Buffett's formula**
Owner earnings =
1. **reported earnings**
2. **plus** depreciation, depletion, amortization, and certain other non-cash charges
3. **minus** the average annual capitalized expenditure required to fully maintain long-term competitive position and unit volume
4. **minus** any additional working capital needed to maintain competitiveness and unit volume

**Why it matters**
- This is Buffett's attempt to approximate distributable economic cash without impairing the franchise.

**Critical nuance**
- Maintenance capital expenditure is a judgment estimate, not a mechanically precise number.

**Evaluation questions**
- What are true owner earnings?
- How much maintenance capex is actually required?
- Is depreciation below, near, or above real maintenance needs?

---

### D. “Cash flow” is often a misleading promotional number
**Framework principle**
- Reported earnings plus depreciation/amortization is not enough if maintenance capex is ignored.
- For most operating businesses, failing to subtract maintenance needs overstates economic cash generation.

**Performance logic**
- If a business must reinvest heavily just to stand still, then simplistic cash-flow measures are inflated.

**Evaluation questions**
- Is this cash-flow presentation subtracting maintenance capex?
- Does the business require ongoing reinvestment to defend volume and competitiveness?
- Is someone “justifying the unjustifiable” with a flattering metric?

---

### E. Better to be approximately economically right than precisely accounting wrong
**Framework principle**
- Valuation requires informed estimation, not blind precision.
- An approximate owner-earnings estimate is superior to a formally precise but economically irrelevant GAAP number.

**Evaluation questions**
- What is the key estimate that matters most?
- Am I hiding behind precision where judgment is required?
- Which approximation best reflects business reality?

---

## 11) Tax and franchise framework

### A. The economic incidence of tax changes depends on franchise strength
**Framework principle**
- Whether tax changes benefit shareholders or customers depends on the business's competitive position and regulatory setting.

**Three cases**
1. **Regulated strong franchise**  
   - tax changes mostly flow through prices
2. **Weak-franchise, price-competitive industry**  
   - market competition tends to pass tax changes through over time
3. **Strong unregulated franchise**  
   - tax benefits largely accrue to the business and its owners

**Evaluation questions**
- How much pricing power does the business really have?
- Is the business regulated in a way that redirects benefits to customers?
- In a tax change, who actually captures the economics?

---

### B. Tax law can change asset values without changing operating quality
**Framework principle**
- Legal or tax regime changes can alter what shareholders can realize from a business even when operating economics are unchanged.

**Example logic**
- Repeal of favorable liquidation treatment lowers realizable value for some asset-heavy or sale-oriented businesses.

**Evaluation questions**
- Is a change in value due to business deterioration or rule changes?
- How do tax structures affect realizable asset value?
- Is the market fully recognizing the effect of legal changes?

---

## 12) Circle-of-competence framework

### A. Simplicity is a feature, not a limitation
**Framework principle**
- Buffett avoids businesses requiring technological understanding he does not possess.
- Omission is a discipline, not a weakness.

**Evaluation questions**
- Do I actually understand how this business makes money?
- Are key drivers stable and intelligible?
- Am I relying on forecasts because I lack understanding?

---

### B. Future projections are of little value when consistency is absent
**Framework principle**
- Proven earning power is preferable to speculative forecasting.
- Turnarounds and story stocks are unattractive because the evidence base is weak.

**Evaluation questions**
- Is this thesis based on demonstrated economics or imagined future improvement?
- Does the business already show consistent earning power?
- Am I substituting narrative for proof?

---

## 13) Performance logic Buffett uses across businesses

### The repeatable pattern he rewards
A business becomes highly attractive when it has most of the following:

- understandable operations
- strong and durable competitive position
- capable, honest, owner-oriented management
- high returns on capital
- low need for incremental capital
- ability to maintain or strengthen customer attachment
- rational pricing discipline
- significant cash generation for redeployment
- purchase price below intrinsic value

### The repeatable pattern he avoids
He is skeptical of situations with:

- reliance on rosy projections
- turnaround dependence
- auction-style sale processes
- high leverage
- weak or commodity economics without a durable edge
- accounting presentations flattering economics
- management that needs supervision
- businesses outside his circle of competence
- market euphoria disconnecting price from business value

## 14) Reusable Buffett-style evaluation checklist derived from the 1986 letter

### Business quality
- Is the business simple enough to understand?
- Does it have a strong market position?
- What is the moat, exactly?
- Are returns on capital high?
- How much incremental capital is needed to grow?
- Does the business have pricing power, cost advantage, or customer captivity?

### Management quality
- Do managers think like owners?
- Are they honest, able, and passionate?
- Can they run the business with little oversight?
- Would you want to partner with them permanently?

### Capital allocation
- What happens to retained earnings?
- Does management reinvest at attractive rates?
- Is cash being hoarded because opportunity is absent or because judgment is weak?
- Is the company big enough that size is impairing future returns?

### Valuation
- What is intrinsic value versus accounting value?
- What are owner earnings?
- What maintenance capex is actually required?
- Are accounting charges obscuring or overstating true economics?
- Is the stock available below intrinsic business value?

### Industry structure
- Is this a commodity business or a franchise business?
- Is capacity entering the industry?
- Are current profits cyclical, structural, or accounting-driven?
- How will competition behave when profits rise?

### Balance-sheet / risk
- How much debt is employed?
- Is leverage essential to the thesis?
- Are liabilities, especially insurance reserves, honestly stated?
- Does the business need favorable conditions to survive?

### Deal / acquisition suitability
- Is the seller relationship-friendly and realistic on price?
- Is management staying?
- Is the business large enough to matter?
- Is the business being bought for cash generation, franchise quality, and people — not for synergies or financial engineering?

## 15) Highest-value concepts from this letter for a Buffett knowledge base

### Tier 1 concepts
- Intrinsic value vs. book value
- Capital allocation as a central management function
- High returns on capital with low incremental capital needs
- Owner-oriented management
- Acquisition criteria: consistency, low debt, simplicity, management in place, known price
- Good business + good management + discount to intrinsic value
- Fear and greed as opportunity creators, not timing tools
- Moat as structural cost or franchise advantage
- Insurance evaluated through combined ratio, float, reserve honesty, and cycle awareness
- Owner earnings as valuation anchor

### Tier 2 concepts
- Size reduces future return potential
- Strong unregulated franchises capture tax benefits
- Purchase accounting can obscure true economics
- Reported cash flow can be deceptive without maintenance capex
- Selective inactivity is rational capital allocation
- Permanent ownership mindset for exceptional businesses

## 16) One-sentence synthesis

The 1986 letter shows Buffett evaluating businesses by asking: **Is this an understandable, well-managed, strongly positioned business that earns high returns on capital, needs little additional capital, can be trusted with autonomy, and is available at a price below intrinsic value when judged by owner earnings rather than accounting appearances?**
