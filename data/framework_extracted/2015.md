# Berkshire Hathaway 2015 Shareholder Letter — Reusable Buffett Analysis Framework

Source letter: Berkshire Hathaway 2015 Shareholder Letter

## Purpose of this extraction

This document extracts **reusable investment and business-analysis principles** from the 2015 Berkshire Hathaway shareholder letter. It is **not** a summary of 2015 events. It is a framework document designed for later use in evaluating companies, stocks, managers, and capital allocation decisions in a Buffett-like way.

---

# 1) Core Valuation Philosophy

## 1.1 Intrinsic value matters more than accounting value

### Principle
Book value, GAAP earnings, and reported balance-sheet values are often imperfect proxies for economic reality. The investor’s real task is to estimate **intrinsic business value**.

### Buffett logic
- Accounting can understate value when good businesses are held at old carrying values.
- Accounting can also distort performance because winners are often not marked up while losers may be written down.
- Therefore, **reported numbers are inputs, not conclusions**.

### Reusable evaluation questions
- What is the business worth economically, independent of its accounting carrying value?
- Which assets are understated, overstated, or missing from the balance sheet?
- Are reported earnings a good representation of owner earnings and normalized earning power?
- Is book value becoming less relevant because the business owns strong operating subsidiaries or intangible economic assets?

### Analytical implication
Avoid treating book value, reported equity, or GAAP net income as the final measure of value. Instead, translate accounting outputs into economic reality.

---

## 1.2 Use a small number of high-signal valuation pillars

### Principle
Buffett’s framework in this letter reduces intrinsic value estimation to a few high-signal components rather than many noisy metrics.

### Buffett logic
He emphasizes two quantitative pillars and one qualitative pillar:
1. **Per-share cash and investments**
2. **Per-share earnings from operating businesses**
3. **Qualitative assessment of business quality / durability**

### Reusable evaluation questions
- What cash, securities, or excess financial resources belong to shareholders on a per-share basis?
- What are the normalized pre-tax earnings of the operating businesses?
- How durable are those earnings?
- Is the per-share value growing, not just aggregate size?

### Analytical implication
A strong business valuation model can start with:
- Excess assets / investments
- Normalized operating earnings
- A quality/durability multiplier or judgment layer

---

## 1.3 Focus on per-share intrinsic value, not empire size

### Principle
A good capital allocator does not merely grow total earnings or total assets. The goal is to increase **per-share intrinsic value**.

### Buffett logic
A company can grow while diluting owners. True success requires growth that benefits each continuing share.

### Reusable evaluation questions
- Is growth occurring on a per-share basis?
- Has management issued shares intelligently or recklessly?
- Did acquisitions improve per-share earning power after considering dilution?
- Does buyback policy increase value per share or merely support optics?

### Analytical implication
Always translate corporate actions into per-share consequences.

# 2) Business Quality Framework

## 2.1 Prefer businesses with increasing normalized earning power

### Principle
A superior business is one whose **normalized earning power** rises over time, even if reported earnings fluctuate year to year.

### Buffett logic
Temporary shocks may hit reported earnings, but a strong business should show rising normalized economic power across time.

### Reusable evaluation questions
- What is normalized earning power, excluding temporary noise?
- What events are cyclical or incidental, and what reflects true business deterioration?
- Over 5–10 years, is the earning power trend up, flat, or down?

### Analytical implication
Separate:
- cyclical weakness
- catastrophe or one-off losses
- structural decline
- enduring earning power

---

## 2.2 Favor businesses with wide and durable moats

### Principle
Buffett repeatedly emphasizes operations protected by **hard-to-replicate advantages**.

### Types of moat highlighted in this letter
- **Cost advantage** (GEICO)
- **Scale and network position** (BNSF)
- **Regulatory position / essential-service status** (utilities)
- **Brand / consumer habit / basic-need demand** (Kraft Heinz, Coca-Cola, American Express)
- **Specialized capability / unique expertise** (Precision Castparts, IMC, Ajit Jain’s reinsurance operation)
- **Low-cost, efficient culture** that competitors cannot easily copy

### Reusable evaluation questions
- Why can’t competitors easily copy this business?
- Is the moat rooted in cost, habit, network, regulation, switching cost, brand, or unique know-how?
- Does the moat widen with time, scale, data, or customer entrenchment?
- Is the moat visible in returns, market share, pricing power, or customer economics?

### Analytical implication
A moat is not a slogan. It should show up in economics: higher returns, better customer economics, resilience, or lasting share gains.

---

## 2.3 Low cost is one of the strongest moats

### Principle
A structural cost advantage is one of the most powerful and durable competitive advantages.

### Buffett logic
When a product is necessary but undifferentiated, the low-cost producer can offer customers meaningful savings and steadily take share.

### Reusable evaluation questions
- Is the company the low-cost producer in an important customer decision?
- Can the company pass savings to customers while still earning superior returns?
- Does cost advantage drive share gains over long periods?
- Is the cost edge operationally embedded or easily copied?

### Analytical implication
For commodity-like or frequently purchased products, cost advantage can be more important than brand glamour.

---

## 2.4 Productivity is a core driver of long-term value creation

### Principle
Productivity gains create prosperity and business value. Buffett admires companies that improve output per unit of labor, capital, and time.

### Buffett logic
A business that becomes more efficient can lower costs, improve service, protect margins, strengthen customer value, and compound advantage.

### Reusable evaluation questions
- Is the company improving output per employee, per asset, or per dollar of capital?
- Are customers benefiting through lower prices, better service, or reliability?
- Is management obsessed with productivity or complacent under a protected model?
- Does efficiency improvement come from systems, process, culture, technology, or scale?

### Analytical implication
Productivity is not just a macro idea. It is a company-level predictor of durable competitive strength.

---

# 3) Management Evaluation Framework

## 3.1 Great managers matter enormously

### Principle
Buffett treats exceptional managers as a major source of intrinsic value.

### Traits repeatedly praised
- Rational capital allocation
- Operational excellence
- Cost consciousness
- Shareholder orientation
- Discipline under pressure
- Ability to improve service and productivity
- Ability to grow without bureaucracy

### Reusable evaluation questions
- Is management operationally excellent or merely promotional?
- Does management think like owners?
- Are they disciplined when opportunities look tempting but economics are poor?
- Have they improved service, efficiency, market share, or returns over time?
- Is success dependent on one star operator or embedded in culture?

### Analytical implication
Manager quality is not soft fluff. It should be visible in long-term economics and behavior.

---

## 3.2 Judge managers by behavior, not narrative

### Principle
Buffett values actions over corporate storytelling.

### Positive signals in the letter
- Investing heavily when economics justify it
- Walking away from badly priced business
- Avoiding bloat and bureaucracy
- Running decentralized, efficient operations
- Treating shareholders fairly and equally
- Maintaining strong regulatory relationships through good conduct

### Negative signals implied in the letter
- Issuing misleading “adjusted earnings” that ignore real costs
- Pursuing volume at bad economics
- Empire building
- Bureaucratic bloat
- Shareholder-unfriendly disclosure or selective access

### Reusable evaluation questions
- Where has management chosen discipline over activity?
- Does management discuss economics honestly or manipulate presentation?
- Does management avoid real expenses in non-GAAP storytelling?
- Is the company becoming more bureaucratic as it grows?

### Analytical implication
Quality managers are revealed by what they refuse to do as much as by what they do.

---

## 3.3 Management integrity and ability are separate filters

### Principle
Buffett notes that mistakes can come from misjudging either the economics of a business or the fidelity/ability of managers.

### Reusable evaluation questions
- Is management honest?
- Is management competent?
- Are both present simultaneously?
- Has leadership succession preserved the operating culture?

### Analytical implication
Do not assume character implies ability, or ability implies alignment.

---

# 4) Capital Allocation Framework

## 4.1 The blueprint for long-term compounding

### Principle
This letter gives a direct five-part blueprint for building per-share intrinsic value.

### Buffett blueprint
1. Constantly improve the earning power of existing subsidiaries
2. Increase earnings through bolt-on acquisitions
3. Benefit from growth of investees
4. Repurchase shares when available at a meaningful discount to intrinsic value
5. Occasionally make large acquisitions

### Reusable evaluation questions
- Is management improving the base business before chasing new deals?
- Are bolt-on acquisitions logical, adjacent, and value-creating?
- Are retained earnings from investees compounding value?
- Are buybacks price-sensitive and value-accretive?
- Is large-scale M&A rare, rational, and sized only when justified?

### Analytical implication
Capital allocation should be ranked by expected value creation, not by managerial excitement.

---

## 4.2 Buybacks only create value when done below intrinsic value

### Principle
Repurchases are good only if done at a meaningful discount to intrinsic value.

### Buffett logic
A buyback at the wrong price transfers value from continuing shareholders to selling shareholders. At the right price, it instantly increases per-share intrinsic value.

### Reusable evaluation questions
- Is management explicit about valuation discipline in buybacks?
- Are buybacks occurring because shares are cheap or because management wants EPS optics?
- How much intrinsic value per share is likely created by repurchases at current prices?

### Analytical implication
Treat buybacks as capital allocation decisions, not automatic positives.

---

## 4.3 Rarely issue shares

### Principle
Because equity is precious, Buffett prefers to issue shares rarely, if ever.

### Buffett logic
Issuing shares means giving up part of the business. It should be done only when the value received clearly exceeds the value surrendered.

### Reusable evaluation questions
- Does management treat equity like cash or like a valuable ownership asset?
- Has dilution historically been disciplined?
- Were shares issued at times of undervaluation or overvaluation?

### Analytical implication
Frequent share issuance is often a red flag in supposedly compounding businesses.

---

## 4.4 Bolt-on acquisitions are attractive when priced sensibly

### Principle
Small acquisitions can be excellent uses of capital when they fit existing operations and can be managed by already-strong operators.

### Buffett logic
Bolt-ons can add earnings with low integration friction and little added headquarters complexity.

### Reusable evaluation questions
- Does the acquisition fit an existing business with proven operators?
- Is price sensible relative to earning power and strategic fit?
- Does the deal require new bureaucracy or major oversight?
- Is it truly adjacent, or is it disguised diworsification?

### Analytical implication
Good M&A often looks boring, adjacent, and operationally natural.

---

## 4.5 Keep flexibility across both whole-business acquisitions and passive investments

### Principle
Buffett values the ability to deploy capital either into controlled businesses or non-controlled stakes.

### Buffett logic
Flexibility broadens the opportunity set and increases the odds of finding sensible uses for capital.

### Reusable evaluation questions
- Can the allocator shift between acquisitions and marketable securities?
- Is the opportunity set artificially constrained by organizational dogma?
- Does the firm keep liquidity and optionality for large future opportunities?

### Analytical implication
The best capital allocators are format-agnostic and return-driven.

---

# 5) Investment Portfolio Framework

## 5.1 Prefer a substantial interest in a wonderful business over full ownership of a mediocre one

### Principle
A partial stake in an excellent company can be superior to 100% ownership of a low-quality business.

### Buffett logic
Business quality dominates ownership percentage if the economics are sufficiently superior.

### Reusable evaluation questions
- Would you rather own all of a mediocre company or part of a superior one?
- Are management and business quality strong enough that non-control is acceptable?
- Does the investee compound retained earnings intelligently?

### Analytical implication
Do not fetishize control when minority ownership gives access to better economics.

---

## 5.2 Retained earnings of good investees belong economically to shareholders

### Principle
Dividends received understate the true economic benefit of owning strong companies.

### Buffett logic
If investees retain earnings and deploy them well—especially through repurchases or high-return reinvestment—those retained earnings are economically valuable to the owner even if not reported in GAAP earnings.

### Reusable evaluation questions
- What is the company’s look-through earnings from investees?
- Are retained earnings being reinvested at attractive rates?
- Are investees using capital to repurchase shares intelligently?
- Is reported income understating economic earnings power?

### Analytical implication
For high-quality investments, look-through earnings often matter more than reported dividend income.

---

## 5.3 Concentrate in high-conviction businesses with strong economics and shareholder-oriented management

### Principle
Buffett’s “Big Four” framing highlights concentration in outstanding businesses led by talented, shareholder-oriented managers.

### Reusable evaluation questions
- Does the portfolio concentrate in businesses with enduring moats and strong returns on tangible equity?
- Are management teams skilled and aligned?
- Is ownership percentage increasing through direct purchases or issuer buybacks?

### Analytical implication
A concentrated portfolio can be rational if business quality and valuation discipline are high.

---

# 6) Insurance-Specific Framework

## 6.1 Understand float as an economic asset, not just an accounting liability

### Principle
Insurance float can be a major source of value when it is durable and low-cost.

### Buffett logic
Accounting treats float purely as a liability, but economically it can function like a revolving fund that finances investment assets.

### Reusable evaluation questions
- How large is float relative to equity and premium volume?
- Is float growing, stable, or shrinking?
- What is the cost of float over time?
- Is float durable and slow-moving, or vulnerable to sudden runoff?
- Is management disciplined enough that float does not become value-destructive?

### Analytical implication
In insurance analysis, the key question is not simply “How much float?” but “What is the quality and cost of the float?”

---

## 6.2 Costless or profitable float is extraordinary

### Principle
The best insurers are paid to hold float because underwriting is profitable.

### Buffett logic
If premiums exceed losses plus expenses, the insurer both earns underwriting profit and gets investable funds.

### Reusable evaluation questions
- Has the insurer produced underwriting profits over a long period?
- Is underwriting profitability structural or merely luck during soft-cat years?
- How cyclical are results across the insurance cycle?

### Analytical implication
Treat long-term underwriting profitability as evidence of real underwriting skill and culture.

---

## 6.3 Underwriting discipline has four mandatory tests

### Principle
Buffett gives a reusable four-part underwriting commandment:
1. Understand exposures
2. Conservatively estimate probability and severity of loss
3. Set a premium that covers losses and expenses with a profit
4. Walk away if the proper premium is unavailable

### Reusable evaluation questions
- Does management truly understand exposures?
- Are severity and probability assumptions conservative?
- Is pricing adequate after expense load?
- Does the company walk away in soft markets?

### Analytical implication
Many insurers fail not on analysis but on discipline.

---

## 6.4 Capacity without risk discipline is dangerous

### Principle
Specialized insurers can have unique opportunities, but only if they size risks appropriately relative to resources.

### Reusable evaluation questions
- Is management taking risks no one else can take because of skill and capital, or because of recklessness?
- Could one event impair the enterprise?
- Does the company preserve liquidity under extreme scenarios?

### Analytical implication
In insurance, boldness is only valuable when paired with conservatism.

---

## 6.5 Evaluate insurer strength under extreme stress, not normal conditions

### Principle
An insurer’s quality is best seen in catastrophe scenarios.

### Reusable evaluation questions
- Under a severe catastrophe, would the insurer remain liquid and opportunistic?
- Could it continue writing attractive business while weaker peers retrench?
- Is the balance sheet built to exploit stress rather than merely survive it?

### Analytical implication
The strongest insurers use crises to widen their moat.

---

# 7) Regulated / Capital-Intensive Business Framework

## 7.1 Capital intensity is acceptable if returns are reasonable and durable

### Principle
Buffett is willing to invest massive sums in infrastructure-like assets when returns are dependable and socially necessary.

### Reusable evaluation questions
- Does the business require heavy ongoing capital investment?
- Are returns on that capital reasonable and predictable?
- Is the service essential enough that society and regulators must support continued investment?
- Can the business finance itself safely without parent guarantees?

### Analytical implication
Capital intensity is not bad by itself. The key is whether capital earns acceptable, durable, low-risk returns.

---

## 7.2 Use real interest coverage, not flattering substitutes

### Principle
Buffett explicitly distrusts EBITDA-based comfort measures in certain contexts and prefers real earnings-before-interest-and-taxes coverage.

### Reusable evaluation questions
- Does the company comfortably cover interest with true operating earnings?
- Is management using EBITDA to obscure capital intensity or depreciation reality?
- How resilient is interest coverage in weak years?

### Analytical implication
Debt analysis should emphasize economic earning power, not cosmetically adjusted metrics.

---

## 7.3 Regulation can be an ally when the business is efficient, safe, and customer-friendly

### Principle
In regulated businesses, long-term success depends not only on legal rights but also on being the kind of operator regulators want to support.

### Buffett logic
Governments need capital for essential services. Capital providers need fair treatment. The relationship works best when the operator is efficient and delivers low prices and reliability.

### Reusable evaluation questions
- Does the company run a safe, reliable, low-cost operation?
- Is management trusted by regulators?
- Does the operator create political goodwill by delivering customer value?
- Is the regulatory compact likely to remain supportive over time?

### Analytical implication
In regulated sectors, culture and public legitimacy are part of the moat.

---

## 7.4 Essential-service businesses can support large debt loads when earnings are resilient

### Principle
Some businesses can safely carry significant debt because demand is essential and cash flows are durable.

### Reusable evaluation questions
- Is the service essential and recession-resistant?
- Are earnings diversified across jurisdictions or revenue streams?
- Is leverage ring-fenced at the subsidiary level?
- Could the business withstand terrible economic conditions and still cover obligations?

### Analytical implication
Debt tolerance should be matched to durability of cash generation.

---

# 8) Accounting and Earnings Quality Framework

## 8.1 GAAP is necessary but not sufficient

### Principle
Investors should read GAAP numbers but then adjust them toward economic reality.

### Buffett logic
Some accounting charges are economically real; others arise from acquisition accounting and may not reflect ongoing economic cost.

### Reusable evaluation questions
- Which expenses are unquestionably real recurring costs?
- Which accounting charges may overstate economic expense?
- Which metrics understate maintenance capital needs?
- How far do GAAP earnings differ from economic earnings, and why?

### Analytical implication
Serious analysis requires disciplined adjustment, not blind acceptance and not promotional add-backs.

---

## 8.2 Distinguish between real and non-real amortization

### Principle
Not all intangible amortization is economically equivalent.

### Buffett logic
Some assets, such as software, genuinely deplete. Others, such as certain purchased customer relationships, may not represent real ongoing economic consumption.

### Reusable evaluation questions
- Which amortization charges reflect real economic decline?
- Which arise from purchase accounting conventions?
- If these charges disappeared over time, would reported earnings rise without any improvement in true earning power?

### Analytical implication
Acquisition-heavy companies require extra care in earnings interpretation.

---

## 8.3 Depreciation is usually a real cost, sometimes understated economically

### Principle
Unlike many management teams, Buffett treats depreciation as economically important and warns that true maintenance capital can exceed accounting depreciation.

### Reusable evaluation questions
- Does depreciation understate the capital required to keep the asset base competitive?
- Is management using EBITDA or other pre-depreciation metrics to mask economic reality?
- What is maintenance capex versus reported depreciation?

### Analytical implication
For asset-heavy businesses, owner earnings must reflect real maintenance needs.

---

## 8.4 Never ignore stock-based compensation just because management asks you to

### Principle
A real expense remains a real expense even when excluded from “adjusted earnings.”

### Reusable evaluation questions
- Is management excluding stock compensation from performance metrics?
- How much dilution or economic cost does stock compensation create?
- Are analysts parroting management’s preferred numbers without economic scrutiny?

### Analytical implication
Buffett’s stance here is a broader rule: reject adjustments that flatter management but violate economic sense.

---

# 9) Return on Capital Framework

## 9.1 Favor businesses with high returns on tangible capital

### Principle
A recurring theme is admiration for businesses earning very high returns on net tangible assets or tangible equity.

### Reusable evaluation questions
- What are returns on net tangible assets and tangible equity?
- Are those returns durable or temporarily inflated?
- Do high returns stem from a moat, leverage, accounting quirks, or underinvestment?

### Analytical implication
High returns on tangible capital often signal business quality, especially when achieved without excessive leverage.

---

## 9.2 High-quality businesses can still be bad investments if purchased too expensively

### Principle
Business quality and purchase price must both be right.

### Buffett logic
Even terrific businesses can produce poor investor outcomes if bought at too high a price.

### Reusable evaluation questions
- How much premium to tangible capital or normalized earnings is being paid?
- Does the moat and reinvestment runway justify that premium?
- What return is likely on the actual capital deployed by the investor?

### Analytical implication
Separate “good company” from “good investment.”

---

# 10) Risk Framework

## 10.1 The most important risks are often obvious but hard to quantify

### Principle
Risk disclosure lists are rarely useful by themselves. The real challenge is estimating probability, timing, and magnitude.

### Reusable evaluation questions
- What are the obvious structural threats to this business?
- What is the probability they occur?
- If they occur, how large is the loss?
- When might the impact matter economically to current investors?

### Analytical implication
Risk analysis is scenario analysis, not checklist compliance.

---

## 10.2 Distinguish existential risk from manageable adaptation risk

### Principle
Many industry threats are real, but not all are fatal. Good businesses adapt.

### Reusable evaluation questions
- Is the threat likely to impair economics permanently or merely force adaptation?
- Does the company have capital, talent, and culture to reposition?
- Is the company diversified enough that one problem area cannot sink the enterprise?

### Analytical implication
Avoid overreacting to visible threats if the organization is adaptable and financially strong.

---

## 10.3 Financial fortress matters

### Principle
A company with ample cash, diversified earnings streams, and conservative liability structure can endure shocks and exploit them.

### Reusable evaluation questions
- How much liquidity exists relative to potential near-term claims or obligations?
- Could the company remain opportunistic during a crisis?
- Are liabilities structured to avoid forced actions?

### Analytical implication
Strength is partly measured by what a company can do when others are impaired.

---

# 11) Business Model and Customer Economics Framework

## 11.1 Businesses serving basic needs and desires are especially attractive

### Principle
Buffett repeatedly favors businesses that meet enduring, recurring human needs.

### Examples implied in the letter
- Food
- Insurance
- Energy
- Transportation
- Housing
- Industrial components essential to manufacturing

### Reusable evaluation questions
- Does the business serve a fundamental and recurring need?
- Is demand likely to persist across economic cycles and decades?
- Does the product remain important even if delivery methods evolve?

### Analytical implication
A product tied to basic needs often gives a better base for long-term compounding than one tied to fads.

---

## 11.2 Customer value reinforces moat

### Principle
Businesses that reliably lower customer costs or deliver important savings tend to strengthen their competitive position.

### Reusable evaluation questions
- Does the customer save meaningful money or gain meaningful utility?
- Is the value proposition concrete and easy to understand?
- Does this advantage produce repeat business, loyalty, or share gains?

### Analytical implication
The strongest moats often coincide with obvious customer benefit.

---

# 12) Culture Framework

## 12.1 Decentralization works when paired with high-quality managers and aligned incentives

### Principle
Buffett’s model depends on extreme decentralization, but only because the managers are exceptional and trustworthy.

### Reusable evaluation questions
- Can the company decentralize because managers are strong, or is headquarters chaos being romanticized?
- Does culture avoid bureaucracy while preserving discipline?
- Are local operators empowered within rational capital-allocation guardrails?

### Analytical implication
Decentralization is not universally good; it works when talent and alignment are unusually strong.

---

## 12.2 Avoid bureaucracy and bloat

### Principle
Buffett treats lean organizational design as a competitive and cultural advantage.

### Reusable evaluation questions
- Is headcount growth at headquarters proportional to economic need?
- Are “restructuring charges” chronic?
- Does scale create efficiency or managerial sludge?

### Analytical implication
Bureaucracy is often an invisible tax on shareholders.

---

# 13) Lending and Financial Products Framework

## 13.1 Keep skin in the game

### Principle
Buffett strongly favors loan origination models where the lender retains the economic risk.

### Buffett logic
When originators do not retain risk, volume and fee incentives can overwhelm credit discipline.

### Reusable evaluation questions
- Does the lender keep meaningful exposure after origination?
- Are underwriting incentives aligned with long-term loan performance?
- Is credit quality driven by real repayment ability or by securitization demand?

### Analytical implication
Alignment in lending is a major signal of underwriting quality.

---

## 13.2 Underwrite for reality, not for packaging

### Principle
Complex structuring and financial engineering can hide poor economics.

### Reusable evaluation questions
- Is the underlying asset understandable without heroic analysis?
- Are incentives aligned across originator, packager, and ultimate risk-bearer?
- Is apparent profitability coming from spread economics or from selling risk to someone else?

### Analytical implication
Buffett’s bias is toward transparent finance, understandable loans, and retained risk.

---

## 13.3 Match funding structure to balance-sheet realities

### Principle
Funding mismatches can be dangerous unless there is a natural hedge or liquidity offset elsewhere in the enterprise.

### Reusable evaluation questions
- Is the company borrowing short and lending long?
- What natural offsets or liquidity reserves protect against rate shocks?
- Is the enterprise asset-sensitive or liability-sensitive?

### Analytical implication
Funding structure should be analyzed at the consolidated economic level, not only at the subsidiary level.

---

# 14) Disclosure and Shareholder Alignment Framework

## 14.1 Treat all shareholders equally

### Principle
Buffett explicitly rejects selective disclosure to favored institutions or analysts.

### Reusable evaluation questions
- Does management provide equal access to important information?
- Is communication designed for owners or for market theater?
- Does management explain the business as if speaking to intelligent long-term partners?

### Analytical implication
Communication style is evidence of governance quality.

---

## 14.2 Report as you would wish to be reported to

### Principle
Buffett frames disclosure around reciprocity: give owners the information you would want if roles were reversed.

### Reusable evaluation questions
- Is disclosure genuinely useful for understanding economics?
- Does the company explain segment economics in a decision-useful way?
- Are adjustments and judgments explained candidly?

### Analytical implication
Owner-oriented reporting is a meaningful mark of management quality.

# 15) Buffett-Style Screening Criteria Derived from the Letter

A Buffett-like analyst could convert this letter into the following screening criteria:

## Business quality
- Rising normalized earning power
- Durable moat
- Strong customer value proposition
- High returns on tangible capital
- Essential or recurring demand
- Productivity improvement over time

## Management quality
- Honest communication
- Shareholder orientation
- Strong capital allocation
- Operational discipline
- Ability plus integrity
- Anti-bureaucratic culture

## Capital allocation
- Per-share focus
- Sensible buybacks
- Rare dilution
- Rational bolt-on M&A
- Large acquisitions only when compelling
- Flexible deployment across public and private assets

## Balance-sheet quality
- Fortress liquidity
- Sensible leverage
- Stress survivability
- Funding matched to business model

## Valuation
- Discount to intrinsic value
- Quality-adjusted but price-disciplined purchase
- Look-through earnings considered
- Economic earnings preferred over headline accounting

## Sector-specific add-ons
- Insurance: quality/cost/durability of float; underwriting discipline
- Regulated assets: allowed return durability, regulator relations, true interest coverage
- Asset-heavy businesses: maintenance capex realism
- Lenders: skin in the game, conservative underwriting, understandable risk

# 16) Buffett-Style Company Evaluation Checklist

## A. Understanding the business
1. What does the company do that customers will still need in 10 years?
2. What is the moat, exactly?
3. Why can’t competitors easily copy the economics?
4. Does the business become stronger with scale, efficiency, or customer entrenchment?

## B. Economics
5. What is normalized earning power?
6. What are returns on tangible capital?
7. How much maintenance capital is truly required?
8. Are reported earnings overstating or understating economic earnings?

## C. Management
9. Are management’s communications candid and owner-oriented?
10. Do they act with discipline when pricing is unattractive?
11. Have they improved productivity, service, and returns over time?
12. Do they think in per-share terms?

## D. Capital allocation
13. Are retained earnings invested intelligently?
14. Are acquisitions adjacent, sensible, and value-creating?
15. Are buybacks valuation-sensitive?
16. Has dilution been avoided except when clearly accretive?

## E. Risk
17. What obvious structural threats exist?
18. Are these survivable, adaptable, or existential?
19. Is the balance sheet strong enough for severe stress?
20. Could the company exploit industry distress rather than merely endure it?

## F. Valuation
21. What is intrinsic value based on assets, earning power, and business quality?
22. What is the per-share value today?
23. What assumptions are most important to the valuation?
24. Is the current market price offering a meaningful margin of safety?

# 17) Performance Logic Embedded in the Letter

This letter implies a specific causal chain for long-term compounding:

1. **Strong business model**
2. **Protected by moat**
3. **Run by disciplined, able, shareholder-oriented managers**
4. **Produces high returns on capital or valuable investable float**
5. **Retains and redeploys capital intelligently**
6. **Avoids dilution and buys back shares when cheap**
7. **Maintains fortress finances**
8. **Adapts to threats without jeopardizing the enterprise**
9. **Compounds per-share intrinsic value over time**
10. **Market value eventually follows intrinsic value**

This is the repeatable Buffett logic of business success and investment success extracted from the 2015 letter.

# 18) Most Reusable Buffett Principles from the 2015 Letter

If this letter had to be distilled into a compact reusable doctrine, it would be:

1. **Think in intrinsic value, not accounting optics.**
2. **Think per share, not in aggregate size.**
3. **Prefer wonderful businesses, even without control, to mediocre businesses with control.**
4. **Demand moats that show up in economics.**
5. **Trust normalized earning power more than year-to-year noise.**
6. **Reward managers who are disciplined, efficient, and candid.**
7. **Treat capital allocation as the central managerial job.**
8. **Repurchase shares only below intrinsic value.**
9. **Respect real costs; reject flattering accounting adjustments.**
10. **Value financial strength because it creates survival and opportunity.**
11. **In insurance, float is valuable only when underwriting is disciplined.**
12. **In regulated/capital-heavy sectors, reasonable returns and sound regulation can make huge investments attractive.**
13. **Productivity and low cost are enduring sources of competitive advantage.**
14. **A good company can still be a bad investment if bought too dearly.**
15. **Long-term compounding comes from business quality plus intelligent capital allocation plus valuation discipline.**

# 20) Final takeaway

The 2015 letter is especially valuable because it does more than praise good businesses. It lays out a **full operating system for Buffett-style analysis**:
- estimate intrinsic value economically,
- judge business quality by moat and earning power,
- judge managers by discipline and candor,
- judge capital allocation by per-share outcomes,
- judge risk by survivability and adaptability,
- and never forget that even a great business must be bought at the right price.
