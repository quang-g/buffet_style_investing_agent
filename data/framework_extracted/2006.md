# Berkshire Hathaway 2006 Shareholder Letter — Reusable Buffett Analysis Framework

**Source:** Berkshire Hathaway Chairman's Letter dated February 28, 2007 (covering 2006 results)

**Extraction objective:** distill only the **reusable stock/business analysis framework** embedded in the letter — i.e., principles, criteria, mental models, evaluation questions, and performance logic that can be applied to analyzing any company or investment in a Buffett-like way.

**What this is not:** a summary of Berkshire’s 2006 events.

---

## 1) Core Buffett Lens From This Letter

This letter reinforces a recurring Buffett idea: **a great investment is not just a statistically cheap asset, but a high-quality business run by trustworthy, capable managers, purchased or owned with disciplined capital allocation and protected by rigorous risk control.**

The 2006 letter especially emphasizes six reusable lenses:

1. **Business economics matter more than managerial narratives.**
2. **Management quality is real, observable, and investable.**
3. **Capital allocation is a central source of long-term returns.**
4. **Risk is primarily mispricing, bad underwriting, leverage, and permanent capital loss — not day-to-day volatility.**
5. **Customer value creation is the cleanest route to durable business success.**
6. **Incentives, culture, and ownership structure often determine whether reported success is durable.**

---

## 2) Reusable Principles

### Principle 1: Use multiple yardsticks; no single metric captures intrinsic value
Buffett explicitly says Berkshire’s progress and intrinsic value cannot be judged by a single criterion. Some important realities are quantitative, while others — especially succession quality, managerial depth, and culture — are only partially measurable.

**Framework implication:**
A serious business analysis should never rely on one metric such as EPS growth, P/E, ROE, or book value alone. Intrinsic value must be triangulated from:
- asset value or investable resources,
- earnings power,
- capital allocation quality,
- managerial quality,
- reinvestment runway,
- risk exposure,
- and business durability.

**Reusable question:**
- What combination of metrics best captures this company’s intrinsic value, and what important factors are still not visible in the numbers?

---

### Principle 2: Focus on per-share progress, not aggregate growth
Buffett continues to judge progress using **per-share** growth in investments and earnings, not just total corporate expansion.

**Framework implication:**
A company can become bigger without becoming better for owners. Share issuance, dilution, overpayment for acquisitions, and capital inefficiency can hide behind aggregate growth.

**Reusable question:**
- Is the company creating more value **per share**, or merely becoming larger?

---

### Principle 3: A business should create value by helping customers make or save money
In discussing ISCAR, Buffett highlights an ideal business formula: **the company succeeds because it enables its customers to succeed**.

**Framework implication:**
One of the strongest signs of durable economics is when the product is mission-critical, improves customer productivity, lowers customer cost, or raises customer profit. This makes the seller harder to displace and often strengthens pricing power.

**Reusable question:**
- Does the company make money because it measurably helps its customers make more money, save time, reduce risk, or increase productivity?

---

### Principle 4: Favor businesses with strong underlying economics; management cannot permanently outrun bad industry structure
Buffett states that when a business’s core economics deteriorate, talented management may slow the decline, but cannot ultimately overcome structural decay.

**Framework implication:**
Industry structure matters enormously. A great CEO in a melting industry is often less valuable than an adequate CEO in a structurally advantaged one.

**Reusable questions:**
- Are the industry economics getting stronger, weaker, or simply more competitive?
- Is management truly creating value, or merely managing decline well?
- If the industry headwinds persist for ten years, what happens to margins and returns on capital?

---

### Principle 5: Low cost is a strategic weapon when it is genuine and earned through productivity
GEICO is praised not simply for growth but for maintaining low-cost producer status while scaling and increasing advertising. Productivity gains preserved its cost advantage.

**Framework implication:**
Low cost is not just an accounting outcome; it is often the result of process quality, scale discipline, technology, and managerial execution. A true low-cost position can widen moats.

**Reusable questions:**
- Is the company truly a low-cost producer?
- If yes, what operational engine sustains that position?
- Are productivity gains real, repeatable, and shared with customers, or merely temporary margin extraction?

---

### Principle 6: Trustworthy and able managers are part of intrinsic value
Buffett repeatedly emphasizes character, energy, autonomy, and operating skill. He values managers who can be trusted to run the business after a sale with the same dedication as before.

**Framework implication:**
Management quality is not a soft extra. It directly affects capital allocation, culture, efficiency, acquisition integration, risk, and owner outcomes.

**What Buffett appears to look for in managers:**
- operational excellence,
- candor,
- rationality,
- owner-like thinking,
- high energy without bureaucracy,
- ability to improve productivity,
- and willingness to continue performing after liquidity is achieved.

**Reusable questions:**
- Would these managers act intelligently and energetically even if they did not need the money?
- Have they improved economics through execution, not merely favorable conditions?
- Do they think like owners and allocate resources rationally?

---

### Principle 7: Culture and decentralization can outperform bureaucracy
Buffett describes his role as cheering on managers, shaping culture, and allocating capital. He distrusts the typical pathologies of size: slow thinking, resistance to change, and smugness.

**Framework implication:**
When analyzing large organizations, do not assume scale equals strength. Scale can create advantage or sclerosis. The key question is whether the company’s structure preserves entrepreneurial energy.

**Reusable questions:**
- Does scale improve economics, or has it introduced sluggishness and bureaucracy?
- Is decision-making decentralized to capable operators?
- Is headquarters adding value through capital allocation and culture, or just adding layers?

---

### Principle 8: Be the buyer of choice, not just the highest bidder
Buffett’s acquisition discussion shows that attractive sellers often care about permanence, culture, leverage, and treatment of employees — not only price.

**Framework implication:**
For acquisitive firms, competitive advantage can come from reputation, trust, speed, certainty, permanence, and non-disruptive ownership.

**Reusable questions:**
- Why would a desirable seller choose this acquirer over strategic buyers or private equity?
- Does the acquirer have a reputation that lowers deal friction and improves access to proprietary opportunities?
- Does management integrate acquisitions in a way that preserves what made the target valuable?

---

### Principle 9: Use proven managers as platforms for tuck-in acquisitions
Buffett likes “tuck-in” acquisitions when they can be placed under managers who have already demonstrated superior execution.

**Framework implication:**
The quality of an acquisition program is not just price paid; it is also the existence of an operating platform capable of absorbing and improving acquired businesses.

**Reusable questions:**
- Does the company have proven operators who can redeploy their talent across bolt-on deals?
- Have prior tuck-ins produced results above management’s original promises?
- Is acquisition skill embedded in the organization, or dependent on one-off dealmaking?

---

### Principle 10: Discipline means passing when prices are inadequate, even in attractive markets
In insurance, Buffett stresses willingness to write nothing if pricing does not reflect risk. He would rather shrink exposure than accept underpriced risk.

**Framework implication:**
A great business or investor is not defined by always being active, but by selectively acting only when expected value is favorable.

**Reusable questions:**
- Does management preserve discipline when competitors become aggressive?
- Will the company sacrifice volume to protect economics?
- Can management say “no” during euphoric periods?

---

### Principle 11: Appropriate prices do not guarantee profits, but inappropriate prices guarantee eventual losses
This is one of the letter’s clearest reusable ideas. Buffett applies it to insurance underwriting, but it generalizes to lending, acquisitions, stock purchases, derivatives, and capital deployment.

**Framework implication:**
Price is the bridge between a good business and a good investment. Quality alone does not protect returns if paid for at foolish terms.

**Reusable questions:**
- Is the company earning adequate compensation for the risks it assumes?
- Are management’s growth actions priced to win headlines or priced to earn returns?
- What decisions being made today mathematically lock in poor outcomes later?

---

### Principle 12: Be fearful when others are greedy, and greedy when others are fearful
Buffett explicitly applies this to catastrophe insurance pricing and links it to behavior in financial markets.

**Framework implication:**
This is not a slogan but an allocation rule: expand when capital flees and pricing improves; contract when capital floods in and terms deteriorate.

**Reusable questions:**
- Is this industry or asset class currently attracting excess capital?
- Are competitors behaving aggressively because recent calm has distorted perceived risk?
- Is management countercyclical or merely conformist?

---

### Principle 13: Risk should be sized so that mistakes are survivable
In the Equitas deal Buffett acknowledges uncertainty but emphasizes that Berkshire can handle a bad outcome. He repeatedly prefers exposures that remain absorbable even if wrong.

**Framework implication:**
Buffett-style risk management is not about eliminating uncertainty; it is about avoiding exposures that can impair the enterprise.

**Reusable questions:**
- If management is wrong, is the company still fine?
- What is the downside under severe but plausible scenarios?
- Are leverage, liquidity, or contractual obligations turning an error into a franchise-threatening event?

---

### Principle 14: Favor opportunities where time structure and capital strength create edge
Berkshire wins retroactive reinsurance business because it can credibly assume liabilities that may last decades. Few competitors can match that balance-sheet durability.

**Framework implication:**
Competitive advantage can come from **time horizon** and **financial staying power**, not just product differentiation. A strong balance sheet can itself be a moat.

**Reusable questions:**
- Does this company possess a balance-sheet advantage that weaker competitors cannot replicate?
- Does long-duration capital allow it to serve customers or transact in ways others cannot?
- Is financial strength being converted into superior deal flow or better economics?

---

### Principle 15: Look through accounting to economic reality
Buffett explains retroactive reinsurance accounting in plain language and warns that opaque accounting can hide important economics.

**Framework implication:**
Reported earnings can mislead when accounting treatment obscures timing, embedded liabilities, or the true economics of a contract. Serious analysis requires reconstructing the business model beneath the accounting presentation.

**Reusable questions:**
- What is the real economic engine here, separate from GAAP presentation?
- Which accounting items are timing-related, and which reflect genuine value creation or destruction?
- Are disclosures designed to clarify economics or to discourage understanding?

---

### Principle 16: Good businesses often earn high returns with modest leverage
Buffett highlights the manufacturing/service/retailing group’s strong return on tangible net worth and notes that only minor financial leverage was used.

**Framework implication:**
High returns produced without heavy leverage are more valuable than superficially high returns driven by debt.

**Reusable questions:**
- What are returns on tangible capital?
- How much of the return comes from genuine business quality versus leverage?
- If leverage were normalized, would returns still look exceptional?

---

### Principle 17: Distinguish returns on operating capital from returns on purchase price
Buffett notes that businesses acquired at large premiums to net worth may earn excellent returns on tangible net worth while producing lower returns on carrying value.

**Framework implication:**
There are two different questions:
1. Is this a good business?
2. Was it or is it a good investment at this price?

A business can be superb operationally yet mediocre as an investment if bought at too high a price.

**Reusable questions:**
- What return does the business earn on the capital actually required to run it?
- What return does the owner earn on the full price paid?
- Is goodwill masking a low return on acquisition cost?

---

### Principle 18: Durability depends on competitive position, not nostalgia
The newspaper discussion shows Buffett separating affection for a product from its economic reality. He loves newspapers but recognizes the structural collapse of their historical economics.

**Framework implication:**
Emotional admiration for a business category must not override objective analysis of competitive deterioration.

**Reusable questions:**
- Am I attracted to this business because of history, prestige, or personal affection rather than future economics?
- Has technology or distribution permanently weakened its moat?
- Is management fighting a cyclical downturn or a structural decline?

---

### Principle 19: Turnarounds should be judged by evidence, not story
NetJets is discussed as an operation whose growth was never the problem; profitability was. Buffett credits the turnaround only after economics actually improved.

**Framework implication:**
In troubled companies, separate growth from profitable growth. Turnaround claims are credible only when unit economics, operating discipline, and segment performance visibly improve.

**Reusable questions:**
- Is the business truly turning around, or just growing?
- Which specific economics have improved: margins, utilization, customer mix, pricing, cost structure, returns on capital?
- Has the turnaround crossed from hope to evidence?

---

### Principle 20: Strong financing can be a hidden but decisive competitive advantage
Clayton’s success is linked partly to Berkshire’s financial strength, which enables funding of receivables when industry conditions are weak.

**Framework implication:**
Some businesses are far stronger than peers because their parent or balance sheet allows them to continue serving customers during credit stress.

**Reusable questions:**
- Does superior access to funding create share gains, resilience, or pricing advantage?
- Can the company operate when the rest of the industry is capital-constrained?
- Is capital strength temporary, or a durable structural edge?

---

### Principle 21: Currency, macro, and derivatives are secondary tools, not the core thesis
Buffett notes that currency exposure is only one of many equity considerations, and that derivatives are used selectively only when mispriced and without meaningful counterparty risk.

**Framework implication:**
Macro views may inform decisions, but they should not replace business analysis. Derivatives should be used only when the edge is explicit and risk structure is controllable.

**Reusable questions:**
- Is management relying on macro forecasting rather than business-level edge?
- Is a derivative or financial structure being used because it is mispriced, or because it magnifies returns cosmetically?
- What counterparty, liquidity, and tail risks sit behind the trade?

---

### Principle 22: In investing, temperament is as important as intellect
Buffett says successful long-term investing requires independent thinking, emotional stability, and understanding of human and institutional behavior. He also warns that a single major mistake can erase many successes.

**Framework implication:**
Investment process should be evaluated partly as a behavioral system. A brilliant analyst with weak temperament is dangerous.

**Reusable questions:**
- Does the decision-maker have the temperament to resist crowd pressure?
- Does the process explicitly guard against rare but catastrophic mistakes?
- Is this a record of skill, or a record that has not yet encountered stress?

---

### Principle 23: Incentives should match controllable operating performance
Buffett says compensation should be simple, fair, and tied to results the executive actually controls. He rejects rewards driven by unrelated market swings or the performance of other units.

**Framework implication:**
Incentive design reveals whether a company thinks clearly about performance. Misaligned incentives can destroy culture and encourage manipulation.

**Reusable questions:**
- Are managers rewarded for outcomes they actually influence?
- Do incentives promote long-term value creation or short-term optics?
- Is compensation simple enough that owners can understand it?

---

### Principle 24: Governance quality depends on genuine independence and owner orientation
Buffett argues that directors should be owner-oriented, business-savvy, interested, and truly independent.

**Framework implication:**
Formal independence is not enough. Real governance requires directors whose judgment is not compromised by dependence on fees, status, or management favor.

**Reusable questions:**
- Do directors think like long-term owners?
- Are they capable of evaluating business strategy and capital allocation, not just complying with process?
- Is their independence economic and psychological, or merely formal?

---

### Principle 25: Fees and intermediation are a structural drag on investor outcomes
Buffett criticizes high-fee arrangements that siphon off returns regardless of whether genuine skill exists.

**Framework implication:**
For any fund, vehicle, or externally managed structure, fee design is a central part of expected return analysis.

**Reusable questions:**
- What share of gross returns is being transferred to intermediaries?
- Does the fee structure create symmetry, or does the manager win even when clients lose?
- How much must the underlying asset outperform merely to overcome fee drag?

---

### Principle 26: Simple, rational methods can outperform complex but misguided theory
Buffett’s discussion of Walter Schloss emphasizes that simple value discipline can beat fashionable theory when grounded in sound principles and risk avoidance.

**Framework implication:**
Analytical complexity is not proof of insight. A robust, understandable method can be superior to ornate models that ignore behavior, valuation, or downside.

**Reusable questions:**
- Is this thesis strong because it is economically sound, or merely because it is technically elaborate?
- Would the conclusion still hold if stripped of jargon and model complexity?
- Is there evidence of durable edge, or only theoretical elegance?

## 3) Buffett Criteria for Judging a Business

From the letter, a Buffett-style evaluator would heavily favor companies with the following traits:

### A. Business Quality
- Clear customer value proposition
- Ability to help customers make/save money
- Strong competitive position
- Low-cost status or another genuine operating advantage
- Durable demand or mission-critical use case
- Resilience to competitive and technological change

### B. Economic Quality
- High returns on tangible capital
- Ability to grow without heavy leverage
- Good unit economics
- Pricing discipline
- Capacity to reinvest at attractive rates
- Preferably countercyclical opportunity set when others retreat

### C. Management Quality
- Trustworthy
- Rational capital allocator
- Operationally excellent
- Non-bureaucratic
- Owner-minded
- Strong under adversity, not only in favorable conditions
- Able to maintain performance after liquidity events or scale increases

### D. Capital Allocation Quality
- Sensible acquisitions
- Discipline on price paid
- Willingness to shrink when terms are poor
- Intelligent use of balance sheet strength
- Thoughtful incentives
- Candor in communicating economics

### E. Risk Quality
- Avoidance of catastrophic, franchise-threatening errors
- Exposure sized to survive being wrong
- Low dependence on leverage or fragile funding
- Skepticism toward opaque accounting and fashionable finance
- Clear distinction between volatility and permanent loss

## 4) Performance Logic Buffett Uses

This letter reveals a recurring **performance logic chain** Buffett seems to apply:

### Performance Logic 1: Customer utility -> competitive strength -> economic durability
If a company measurably improves customer economics, customers become less price-sensitive and more loyal, which supports enduring margins and reinvestment.

### Performance Logic 2: Productivity -> low cost -> pricing flexibility -> share gains
Operational excellence lowers cost, which can be converted into either better margins or lower prices that drive market-share gains.

### Performance Logic 3: Strong culture + able managers -> decentralized execution -> superior results without bureaucratic drag
A good culture allows headquarters to stay lean while operating managers perform at a high level.

### Performance Logic 4: Financial strength + long horizon -> ability to take risks others cannot -> superior deal flow and economics
A fortress balance sheet can create both defensive resilience and offensive opportunity.

### Performance Logic 5: Price discipline -> fewer bad decisions -> stronger long-term compounding
Refusing underpriced risk or overpriced acquisitions may reduce current growth but improves long-run returns.

### Performance Logic 6: Misaligned incentives -> distorted behavior -> eventual economic disappointment
When rewards are disconnected from controllable operating performance, bad behavior eventually surfaces in capital allocation or reported results.

### Performance Logic 7: Bad industry economics -> shrinking moat -> declining returns despite effort
Even excellent managers struggle to create enduring value in structurally broken industries.

### Performance Logic 8: Low fees and simple process -> more of the gross return retained by owners
For funds and investment vehicles, what the owner keeps matters more than gross headline returns.

## 5) Reusable Mental Models Embedded in the Letter

### Mental Model 1: The business-owner lens
Treat a stock as a business. Ask what economics, managers, and reinvestment opportunities you actually own.

### Mental Model 2: The survivability test
A good decision is not only one with favorable odds, but one whose downside is absorbable.

### Mental Model 3: The structure-over-story test
Industry structure and economics matter more than charisma, fashion, or optimistic narrative.

### Mental Model 4: The controllables principle
Judge people on what they control. This applies to CEO compensation, operating evaluation, and managerial accountability.

### Mental Model 5: The countercyclical capital model
When capital floods into a field, expected returns usually fall. When capital withdraws, opportunity often rises.

### Mental Model 6: The look-through economics model
Accounting, organizational charts, and labels may obscure reality. Reconstruct the real economics yourself.

### Mental Model 7: The acquisition selectivity model
A deal is attractive only if the business is good, the managers are right, the price is sensible, and post-deal economics remain favorable.

### Mental Model 8: The permanent-loss definition of risk
Volatility is tolerable; impairment is not. Avoid setups where one large error can erase years of gains.

### Mental Model 9: The edge-from-temperament model
Behavioral steadiness can be a source of investment edge, especially when markets become euphoric or fearful.

### Mental Model 10: The simplicity filter
If a thesis only works inside a complicated model, it may not be robust enough.

## 6) Buffett-Style Evaluation Questions You Can Reuse for Any Company

### Business economics
1. What problem does the company solve for its customers?
2. Does it help customers make more money, save money, save time, or reduce risk?
3. Why does the company win: cost, brand, switching costs, distribution, regulation, habit, or embedded workflow?
4. Are these advantages strengthening or eroding?
5. Is the industry structurally healthy or structurally deteriorating?

### Operating quality
6. Is the company a low-cost producer or otherwise operationally advantaged?
7. Has management improved productivity in a measurable way?
8. Can growth occur without proportionate growth in headcount, capital, or overhead?
9. Are recent improvements cyclical, structural, or accounting-driven?

### Management
10. Are management’s decisions rational and owner-oriented?
11. Do they tell the truth about weak areas, or only celebrate strengths?
12. Have they created value through execution, not just through favorable conditions?
13. Would you trust them to run the business with minimal supervision?
14. Do they allocate capital better than peers?

### Capital allocation
15. Where does retained earnings go?
16. Are acquisitions sensible and well-integrated?
17. Is the company the buyer of choice for attractive targets, and why?
18. Does management repurchase shares intelligently?
19. Does the company prioritize per-share value over empire building?

### Financial quality
20. What are returns on tangible capital?
21. How much leverage is supporting those returns?
22. What economic return is earned on the full acquisition price or enterprise value?
23. Is balance-sheet strength creating opportunity or merely sitting idle?

### Risk
24. What could cause permanent capital loss here?
25. If management is wrong, does the company survive comfortably?
26. Are there hidden liabilities, opaque accounting issues, or long-tail contractual risks?
27. Is the company writing, lending, insuring, or promising anything today that is underpriced?
28. Has excess capital entered this field and weakened future returns?

### Governance and incentives
29. Are incentives simple, fair, and tied to controllable results?
30. Does the board appear truly owner-oriented and independent?
31. Are compensation structures encouraging real value creation or cosmetic performance?

### Valuation / investment decision
32. Is this a great business?
33. Is it also a great investment at the current price?
34. What assumptions must be true for the current valuation to work?
35. Are you being paid appropriately for the risks you are taking?

## 7) Practical Extraction Rules for a Buffett Knowledge Base

When converting this letter into a reusable research system, the following rules emerge:

### Rule A: Separate **business quality** from **investment attractiveness**
Do not confuse admiration for a company with a justified purchase price.

### Rule B: Always evaluate **industry economics** before celebrating management
Good managers in bad industries often produce only temporary victories.

### Rule C: Explicitly assess **capital allocation**
For Buffett, capital allocation is not a side topic; it is one of the main determinants of owner returns.

### Rule D: Treat **balance sheet strength** as a strategic asset
Especially in insurance, finance, cyclical industries, and acquisitions, capital strength can be a decisive moat.

### Rule E: Embed **risk-of-ruin analysis**
A Buffett-style framework must ask not just whether the thesis is attractive, but whether it is survivable if wrong.

### Rule F: Examine **incentives and governance** as economic variables
These are not “soft” ESG-style footnotes; they directly affect future outcomes.

### Rule G: Prefer **clarity of economics** over analytical complexity
The best thesis should remain persuasive even when stripped to plain English.

## 8) Condensed Buffett Checklist From the 2006 Letter

Use this as a quick-screen framework:

- **Does the business have favorable underlying economics?**
- **Does it create clear value for customers?**
- **Is there a durable competitive advantage, especially a low-cost or mission-critical position?**
- **Are returns on tangible capital strong without heavy leverage?**
- **Are managers trustworthy, able, energetic, and owner-like?**
- **Does the company allocate capital rationally?**
- **Will management walk away when pricing is poor?**
- **Can the company survive major errors or shocks?**
- **Are accounting and disclosures economically intelligible?**
- **Are incentives aligned with controllable performance?**
- **Is the industry improving, stable, or deteriorating?**
- **Is the current price sensible relative to quality and risk?**

---

## 9) One-Sentence Synthesis

**The core reusable Buffett framework in the 2006 letter is to buy or own understandable businesses with strong underlying economics, customer-value-based moats, trustworthy and able managers, disciplined capital allocation, and survivable risk — but only when the price and terms are sensible enough to preserve long-term compounding.**
