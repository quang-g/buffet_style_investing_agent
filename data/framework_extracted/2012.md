# Buffett Reusable Analysis Framework Extraction — Berkshire Hathaway Shareholder Letter (2012)

Source letter: Berkshire Hathaway 2012 Shareholder Letter (dated March 1, 2013).

## What this extraction is

This document extracts the **reusable investment and business-analysis framework** embedded in Buffett’s 2012 shareholder letter.

It is **not** a summary of 2012 events.
It is a **Buffett-style evaluation framework** that can be reused to analyze a stock, a company, or a business.

---

# 1) Ultimate objective and scorecard

## Core objective
Buffett’s objective is to **increase per-share intrinsic business value over time**, not merely to grow absolute size, reported earnings, or book value.

## Proxy metric logic
Intrinsic value cannot be measured precisely, so Buffett uses **book value as a significantly understated proxy** for intrinsic value in Berkshire’s case.

## Practical framework rule
When analyzing a company, always ask:

- What is the best measure of **per-share intrinsic value creation**?
- If management uses a proxy, is it a **good proxy** or a misleading one?
- Does the company create value **per share**, or does it merely get bigger?

## Reusable evaluation questions
- What is management explicitly trying to maximize?
- Is the scorecard based on **per-share economics** or aggregate growth?
- Is the chosen yardstick economically grounded?
- Does management keep the same yardstick in good and bad market environments?

## Buffett performance logic
A manager adds value only if the company’s intrinsic value compounds faster than what an owner could get from a passive alternative.

### Reusable test
Compare management’s value-add against the reasonable default alternative:
- For a diversified public investor: low-cost index fund
- For a business owner: reinvestment elsewhere at comparable risk
- For a capital allocator: what shareholders could have done without management

# 2) Per-share value creation beats empire building

## Principle
Buffett distinguishes between **growing** and **increasing per-share results**.
Growth that comes with dilution or weak economics is inferior to growth that increases owner value per share.

## Reusable framework rule
Treat share issuance, acquisitions, and expansion as successful only if they improve:
- per-share earning power
- per-share intrinsic value
- ownership claim on future cash flows

## Evaluation questions
- Did growth come from true economic gains or from issuing stock?
- How much dilution accompanied the growth?
- Would the same capital allocation have improved per-share value if financed differently?
- Is management disciplined about not issuing undervalued shares?

---

# 3) A business should be judged by economic reality, not just accounting presentation

## Principle
GAAP can be useful, but Buffett repeatedly adjusts accounting figures to get closer to economic reality.

## Reusable accounting framework

### A. Reported earnings may understate real owner economics
Examples from the letter:
- retained earnings of investees are economically valuable even if not reported in Berkshire’s earnings
- some amortization charges are economically real, others are not
- float is recorded as a liability at face value even though its economic cost may be far lower

### B. Separate accounting form from economic substance
Do not automatically treat these as equal:
- reported liability = economic burden
- reported asset = economic value
- reported expense = true economic cost
- reported earnings = owner earnings

## Evaluation questions
- What economically valuable earnings are omitted from reported earnings?
- Which expenses are real cash/economic costs, and which are accounting conventions?
- Which liabilities are overstated or understated economically?
- Which assets are worth more or less than carrying value?

## Reusable investor habit
Always build a parallel view:
1. reported accounting view
2. economic owner view

# 4) Intrinsic value is driven by multiple components, not one headline number

## Principle
Buffett emphasizes that intrinsic value is not one neat formula. It depends on both quantitative and qualitative drivers.

## Reusable intrinsic value architecture
A sensible appraisal should consider at least:

### Quantitative drivers
- investment assets per share
- operating earnings per share
- growth in both
- durability of those earnings
- capital intensity and reinvestment requirements
- financing structure and hidden liabilities

### Qualitative drivers
- quality of managers
- shareholder orientation
- culture
- resilience of business model
- competitive position

## Evaluation questions
- What are the main quantitative engines of intrinsic value here?
- What qualitative traits make those numbers durable or fragile?
- Which value drivers are being underappreciated by standard metrics?

---

# 5) Focus strongly on operating earning power

## Principle
Buffett likes increases in investments and asset values, but he emphasizes **building operating earnings**.

## Reusable rule
When comparing businesses, place the highest value on:
- durable operating earning power
- earnings that can grow without heroic assumptions
- earnings backed by real business strength rather than market revaluation

## Evaluation questions
- How much of value creation comes from operating progress versus asset appreciation?
- Would the business still look attractive if the market never rerated it?
- Is operating earnings growth dependent on leverage, acquisitions, or favorable cycles?

---

# 6) Buy wonderful businesses at fair prices, not fair businesses at wonderful prices

## Principle
Buffett restates Munger’s idea: a wonderful business at a fair price is superior to a mediocre business bought cheaply.

## Reusable framework rule
Business quality can outweigh apparent cheapness.
A bargain purchase is not enough if the business has poor economics.

## Business-quality indicators in the letter
- high returns on unleveraged net tangible assets
- durable competitive position
- pricing power or strong customer value proposition
- long runway for growth
- excellent managers
- shareholder-oriented culture

## Evaluation questions
- Is this a genuinely wonderful business or just statistically cheap?
- What are returns on unleveraged tangible capital?
- Does the business improve with scale, or just get bigger?
- Would you want to own all of it indefinitely?

---

# 7) Good business economics are best seen through returns on tangible capital

## Principle
Buffett repeatedly looks through to returns on capital actually required by the business.

## Reusable metric lens
Prefer analyzing:
- return on tangible net assets
- return on incremental invested capital
- earnings relative to capital truly needed for operations

instead of relying only on:
- EBITDA
- adjusted growth narratives
- headline revenue growth

## Why this matters
A business that earns high returns on little incremental tangible capital has superior economics and stronger compounding potential.

## Evaluation questions
- How much capital must the business actually employ to earn what it earns?
- Are returns high because of leverage, accounting, or true business economics?
- What return is earned on incremental capital deployed?

# 8) Insurance framework: float quality matters more than float size

## Principle
Insurance is attractive not merely because it generates float, but because it can generate **costless or profitable float**.

## Reusable insurance framework
Float should be evaluated on four dimensions:

### A. Size
How much investable float exists?

### B. Cost
Is underwriting profitable, breakeven, or loss-making over time?

### C. Durability
Is float stable, growing, shrinking, or vulnerable to sudden runoff?

### D. Safety
Are risks assumed appropriate for the insurer’s resources?

## Buffett’s core insight
Not all float is equal.
“Berkshire-quality float” is rare because most insurers pay for float through underwriting losses.

## Evaluation questions
- Is underwriting profitable across the cycle?
- What is the long-run cost of float?
- How durable is the float base?
- How catastrophe-resistant is the insurer?
- Does management avoid writing underpriced business merely to maintain volume?

---

# 9) Insurance underwriting discipline: four commandments

Buffett gives a reusable four-part insurance test.
A sound insurer must:

1. understand exposures that could create loss
2. conservatively assess probability and severity
3. charge a premium that should produce profit after losses and expenses
4. walk away when adequate pricing is unavailable

## Reusable application beyond insurance
This logic generalizes to any risk-taking business:
- understand downside exposures
- estimate them conservatively
- price for risk adequately
- decline activity when pricing is irrational

## Evaluation questions
- Does management understand the risk, or just the upside story?
- Is the pricing of risk conservative or aggressive?
- Does management show willingness to forgo revenue when economics are poor?
- Is discipline strongest when competitors behave irrationally?

---

# 10) A key sign of quality: willingness to walk away

## Principle
Many operators pass analysis and pricing tests but fail the discipline test.
They cannot resist doing business when others are doing it.

## Reusable framework rule
One of the clearest signs of managerial quality is the ability to say **no**:
- no to underpriced business
- no to overvalued acquisitions
- no to weak projects dressed up with narratives
- no to leverage that creates fragility

## Evaluation questions
- Where has management demonstrably walked away from unattractive opportunities?
- Is volume prioritized over economics?
- Does behavior worsen in competitive booms?

---

# 11) Risk is not volatility; risk is exposure to ruin or forced dependence

## Principle
Buffett emphasizes redundancy of liquidity, aversion to collateral calls, and survival under extreme events.

## Reusable risk framework
Prioritize protection against:
- forced asset sales
- collateral drains
- refinancing dependence
- insolvency under stress
- single-event fragility

## Buffett-style risk logic
A structure that slightly depresses returns most years can still be superior if it ensures survival in the rare disastrous year.

## Evaluation questions
- Could this business be forced into distress by a low-probability event?
- Does it rely on fragile funding or collateral-sensitive obligations?
- How many layers of liquidity or earnings protection exist?
- Under severe stress, does the company remain profitable, solvent, and in control of its decisions?

---

# 12) Liquidity is strategic optionality

## Principle
Buffett treats cash and liquidity not as idle drag alone, but as protection plus future opportunity.

## Reusable framework rule
Excess liquidity can be rational if it:
- prevents forced action
- lets the company act opportunistically in crises
- supports large acquisitions or investments without strain

## Evaluation questions
- Is liquidity excessive because management is timid, or prudent because opportunities and risks justify it?
- Would current liquidity allow the company to exploit distress in its industry?
- Does the company need liquidity because its obligations are structurally dangerous?

---

# 13) Capital-intensive regulated businesses can be excellent if returns are fair and durability is high

## Principle
Buffett views certain regulated, capital-intensive businesses favorably because they provide essential services, enjoy long asset lives, and can deploy large sums at reasonable returns.

## Reusable framework for regulated infrastructure businesses
These businesses can be attractive when they have:
- essential-service demand
- long-lived assets
- predictable earning streams
- funding structures matched to asset duration
- regulators likely to allow fair returns over time
- management that behaves in ways regulators and communities can support

## Evaluation questions
- Is the service essential and recession-resistant?
- Are returns on capital likely to remain reasonable over long periods?
- Is the debt self-supporting at the subsidiary level?
- Does the business depend on benevolent regulation, or on economically necessary treatment of capital providers?
- Does management act in a way that preserves its social and regulatory license?

---

# 14) Don’t evaluate debt service using weak metrics

## Principle
Buffett explicitly rejects EBITDA/interest as a preferred coverage measure and favors **pre-tax earnings / interest**.

## Reusable framework rule
Use measures that reflect true economic earning power rather than looser cash proxies that may ignore real capital needs.

## Evaluation questions
- Does the chosen solvency metric understate capital intensity?
- Is depreciation economically real in this business?
- Would the company still look safe using stricter coverage measures?

---

# 15) Invest heavily when opportunities are worthwhile, not when headlines feel comfortable

## Principle
Buffett rejects paralysis caused by macro “uncertainty.”
He prefers investing heavily in projects with good economics regardless of near-term noise.

## Reusable framework rule
Separate:
- uncertainty, which is always present
from
- unattractive economics, which is the real reason not to invest

## Evaluation questions
- Is management avoiding action because economics are poor, or because headlines are scary?
- Does the company continue funding high-return projects during noisy periods?
- Is capital spending tied to long-term need and return, or to short-term sentiment?

---

# 16) Long-term optimism should not override project-level discipline

## Principle
Buffett is structurally optimistic about productive economies, but that does not justify careless deployment.

## Reusable mental model
There are two separate judgments:
1. the long-term economic game is favorable
2. a specific investment is attractive at a specific price and risk level

Both must be true.

## Evaluation questions
- Is management confusing general optimism with investment discipline?
- Does a good macro backdrop mask poor business economics?
- Is long-term secular growth being used to justify weak returns on capital?

---

# 17) The best capital allocators use a hierarchy of uses for cash

## Buffett’s capital allocation ladder
The letter implies a repeatable hierarchy:

1. improve current operations and widen moats
2. do bolt-on acquisitions that fit proven managers and existing businesses
3. benefit from retained earnings and growth of investees
4. repurchase shares when clearly below intrinsic value
5. make occasional large acquisitions when they improve per-share value
6. pay dividends only if retained capital cannot be deployed advantageously

## Reusable framework rule
Evaluate management by whether it has a rational, ranked capital allocation process.

## Evaluation questions
- What is management’s explicit order of capital allocation priorities?
- Does each use of capital clear a per-share value creation hurdle?
- Are buybacks, M&A, capex, and dividends all assessed against one coherent standard?

---

# 18) Bolt-on acquisitions are often superior to transformative deals

## Principle
Buffett likes small acquisitions added to existing platforms because they are often low risk, require little headquarters attention, and leverage proven managers.

## Reusable M&A framework
Bolt-ons are attractive when they:
- fit existing capabilities
- are managed by already-successful operators
- require little integration drama
- deepen moat or scope
- avoid share issuance and heavy bureaucracy

## Evaluation questions
- Is this acquisition adjacent to existing strengths?
- Does the buyer already have the management and infrastructure to absorb it?
- Is the deal complexity low relative to expected benefit?
- Is the acquisition expanding economics or just expanding organization charts?

---

# 19) Large acquisitions must be judged by per-share value, not excitement or size

## Principle
Buffett remains open to large deals, but only if they likely make shareholders wealthier on a per-share basis.

## Reusable acquisition test
A major acquisition should pass these hurdles:
- understandable business
- durable earning power
- rational financing
- no dangerous loss of liquidity
- favorable price relative to intrinsic value
- high probability of increasing per-share intrinsic value

## Evaluation questions
- Would shareholders be better off if the capital were used for buybacks, dividends, or nothing?
- Is the transaction large because it is attractive, or attractive because it is large?
- Is financing structure creating hidden dilution or fragility?

---

# 20) Share issuance is expensive when your stock is undervalued

## Principle
Buffett rarely wants to issue Berkshire shares. Stock should not be used casually as acquisition currency if it transfers value from existing owners.

## Reusable framework rule
Treat stock issuance as a sale of part of the business.
If shares are undervalued, issuing them is a bad bargain for continuing owners.

## Evaluation questions
- At what implied valuation is management issuing stock?
- Is stock-financed M&A actually an overpayment hidden inside dilution?
- Would management still do the deal if it had to use cash only?

---

# 21) Repurchases are excellent only below intrinsic value

## Principle
Buybacks are highly attractive when a company can buy its own shares at a meaningful discount to conservatively estimated intrinsic value.
Above intrinsic value, buybacks destroy value.

## Reusable buyback framework
A good repurchase program requires:
- a conservative estimate of intrinsic value
- a meaningful discount to that value
- sufficient liquidity after purchase
- discipline not to buy just because cash is available

## Evaluation questions
- Is the buyback price below conservative intrinsic value?
- How strong is the evidence that management understands intrinsic value?
- Is the repurchase threshold explicit and credible?
- Are buybacks countercyclical and disciplined, or automatic and cosmetic?

---

# 22) Dividend policy should be subordinate to capital allocation quality

## Principle
Dividends are not automatically virtuous. They make sense only when retained capital cannot be deployed at attractive rates or when owners are better served by distribution.

## Reusable framework rule
The right dividend policy depends on:
- reinvestment opportunity quality
- acquisition opportunity quality
- buyback opportunity quality
- tax consequences to owners
- shareholder flexibility

## Buffett’s deeper logic
If the company can compound retained earnings well and owners can create their own “homemade dividend” by selling small amounts of stock, forced dividends may be inferior.

## Evaluation questions
- Can retained earnings earn attractive returns?
- Would owners be richer if capital stayed inside the business?
- Does the dividend policy reflect economics or convention?
- Are taxes and shareholder choice being respected?

---

# 23) Management quality includes rationality under self-deception risk

## Principle
Buffett warns that managers often begin with the answer they want and then work backward to rationalize it. This is especially dangerous because it is subconscious.

## Reusable behavioral framework
Watch for these red flags:
- strategic narratives designed to justify predetermined spending
- “synergy” arguments with weak economics
- persistence in clearly losing businesses because management wants success to be true
- unwillingness to admit capital allocation mistakes

## Evaluation questions
- Is management reasoning forward from facts or backward from desire?
- Where has management acknowledged error and changed course?
- Are investments supported by evidence or by hope?

---

# 24) Wonderful minority stakes can be superior to full ownership of mediocre businesses

## Principle
Buffett prefers owning a substantial non-controlling stake in a wonderful business over owning 100% of a so-so business.

## Reusable framework rule
Control is not the highest goal; business quality is.
Flexibility in capital allocation can be a competitive advantage.

## Evaluation questions
- Would you rather own all of a mediocre company or a meaningful stake in an exceptional one?
- Is management biased toward acquisitions because it wants control rather than best economics?
- Does the holding company structure allow capital to flow to the best opportunities regardless of control?

---

# 25) Look through reported dividends to underlying earnings power of investees

## Principle
Buffett treats Berkshire’s share of retained earnings from investees as economically belonging to Berkshire, even when not reported in GAAP earnings.

## Reusable framework rule
For strong minority holdings, assess:
- share of total earnings, not just dividends received
- reinvestment quality of retained earnings
- buyback effects that increase ownership percentage over time
- unrealized value creation in the investee

## Evaluation questions
- What is the company’s true economic interest in investees’ earnings?
- Are retained earnings inside the investee being used well?
- Do investee buybacks increase your ownership without added capital?

---

# 26) Business quality includes customer behavior metrics that map to intrinsic value

## Principle
In GEICO, Buffett highlights persistency and closure rates because small improvements in these variables materially change intrinsic value.

## Reusable framework rule
Identify the few operating variables where small changes have outsized value impact.
Examples may include:
- retention
- renewal rates
- conversion rates
- same-store sales productivity
- unit economics
- churn
- pricing power

## Evaluation questions
- Which operational metrics have the highest elasticity to intrinsic value?
- Does management track and explain them clearly?
- Are improvements real and durable?

---

# 27) A company’s moat is widened through reinvestment, not slogans

## Principle
Buffett says management should first examine projects that improve efficiency, expand intelligently, improve products, or widen the moat.

## Reusable moat framework
Moat-widening reinvestment includes:
- cost advantages
- network density or scale economies
- customer captivity or habit strength
- better service reliability
- stronger brand relevance
- distribution advantages
- regulatory or infrastructural depth

## Evaluation questions
- What concrete reinvestments widen the moat?
- Are returns on moat-widening projects attractive?
- Is the moat strengthening, stable, or eroding?

---

# 28) Some industries are structurally bad even if individual firms can be good

## Principle
Buffett notes that the P/C insurance industry overall has poor economics, even though Berkshire’s insurance businesses are exceptional.

## Reusable framework rule
Always separate:
- industry economics
from
- company-specific advantages

A good company in a bad industry must possess unusual advantages and discipline.

## Evaluation questions
- Is the industry structurally attractive?
- If not, what specific edge allows this company to outperform?
- Is the edge durable, or just temporary good fortune?

---

# 29) Legacy asset yields can flatter current earnings

## Principle
Buffett warns that insurers’ current earnings benefit from older bond portfolios with higher yields, which will fade as assets roll over.

## Reusable framework rule
Beware earnings supported by legacy conditions that cannot be repeated.

## Evaluation questions
- Which parts of earnings are benefiting from yesterday’s more favorable environment?
- What happens as assets, contracts, pricing, or rates reset?
- Are investors capitalizing peak or normalized earnings?

---

# 30) Essentiality and endurance matter

## Principle
Buffett favors businesses likely to remain central to the economy for decades or even a century.

## Reusable framework rule
A great business is often tied to a long-duration human or economic need:
- transportation
- energy
- insurance
- payments
- food brands
- indispensable local information in niche cases

## Evaluation questions
- Will this product or service still matter in 10–20 years?
- Is demand cyclical, essential, or habitually recurring?
- Does technological change threaten relevance or merely change delivery format?

---

# 31) Adaptability matters when the core need survives but delivery changes

## Principle
In newspapers, Buffett distinguishes between a declining old format and a still-valuable local information function.

## Reusable framework rule
Do not ask only whether an industry is declining.
Ask whether the **underlying customer need** remains strong and whether the company can adapt its delivery and pricing model.

## Evaluation questions
- Is the company losing relevance, or just changing format?
- What part of the value proposition remains indispensable?
- Does management have a sensible digital/pricing strategy?
- Is local monopoly, habit, or community attachment still meaningful?

---

# 32) Cheapness alone is not enough; demanded purchase multiple must reflect decline risk

## Principle
Buffett is willing to buy newspapers only at a very low multiple of current earnings because the economics are declining.

## Reusable framework rule
Required purchase multiple must reflect:
- business quality
- durability
- reinvestment opportunities
- decline risk
- capital intensity

## Evaluation questions
- What multiple is appropriate for a shrinking but still cash-generative asset?
- Does the valuation compensate for secular erosion?
- Is the price low enough that modest decline still yields acceptable returns?

---

# 33) Segment businesses separately when their economics differ materially

## Principle
Buffett says lumping Berkshire’s very different operations together impedes analysis.

## Reusable framework rule
When analyzing a conglomerate, platform company, or diversified firm, separate businesses by economic type:
- insurance/float businesses
- regulated capital-intensive utilities
- manufacturing/service/retail operations
- financial products
- investment portfolio

## Evaluation questions
- Which segments have fundamentally different balance sheets, earnings characteristics, and valuation methods?
- Is consolidated reporting hiding quality or weakness?
- What sum-of-the-parts view is needed?

# 34) Great managers are part of intrinsic value

## Principle
Buffett repeatedly treats managerial talent, integrity, discipline, and cultural fit as intrinsic value multipliers.

## Reusable management framework
Assess managers on:
- integrity
- rationality
- operational skill
- risk discipline
- capital allocation judgment
- cultural alignment with owners
- ability to operate independently without bureaucracy

## Evaluation questions
- Would you trust this manager with a large amount of incremental capital?
- Does the manager think like an owner?
- Does the manager improve both operations and capital allocation?
- Is decentralization possible because quality is so high?

# 35) Culture is an economic asset

## Principle
Buffett emphasizes shareholder orientation and cultural fit, not as soft language but as a real contributor to future results.

## Reusable framework rule
Culture matters economically when it influences:
- pricing discipline
- acquisition discipline
- honesty in reporting
- lean overhead
- trust and autonomy
- long-term decision making

## Evaluation questions
- Does culture improve capital allocation and risk control?
- Is there evidence of owner-minded behavior across the organization?
- Does culture survive beyond one leader?

# 36) Headquarters should add value, not bureaucracy

## Principle
Berkshire highlights extraordinary scale with minimal headquarters staff.

## Reusable framework rule
For holding companies and roll-ups, lean central overhead is a sign of discipline if local managers are strong.

## Evaluation questions
- Is headquarters solving capital allocation and governance problems, or merely adding cost?
- How much central expense is required to run the group?
- Could decentralization work because operating managers are exceptional?

# 37) The best businesses combine growth with resilience

## Principle
Buffett likes businesses that can both grow and withstand bad environments.

## Reusable framework rule
Favor businesses where downside resilience and upside reinvestment coexist.
Examples in the letter include:
- insurance with profitable float
- essential-service utilities
- high-quality investees retaining earnings well
- businesses still investing during uncertain periods

## Evaluation questions
- How does the business perform in weak markets or recessions?
- Can it still reinvest productively during adversity?
- Is relative performance stronger when conditions are difficult?

---

# 38) Market environments affect relative performance; know when the model shines

## Principle
Buffett says Berkshire is likely to do relatively better when markets are down or flat, and relatively worse when markets are very strong.

## Reusable framework rule
Every strategy has an environment in which it looks best and one in which it may lag.
Understand the built-in behavior of the model.

## Evaluation questions
- In what environment should this company or strategy outperform?
- When might it lag despite remaining sound?
- Are investors misjudging it because they expect one style to win in all climates?

---

# 39) Price and value must always be separated

## Principle
Buffett repeatedly distinguishes business quality from investment quality.
Even a terrific business can be a poor investment if bought at too high a price.

## Reusable framework rule
Always maintain two separate conclusions:
1. Is it a good business?
2. Is it a good investment at today’s price?

## Evaluation questions
- What would make this wonderful business a bad stock?
- What discount or premium to intrinsic value exists today?
- Is the current valuation assuming too much permanence or too little?

---

# 40) Conservative estimation should govern valuation and capital allocation

## Principle
Buffett repeatedly uses “conservatively calculated intrinsic value” as the hurdle for buybacks and decisions.

## Reusable framework rule
When estimates are uncertain, the response should be conservative assumptions, not false precision.

## Evaluation questions
- Which assumptions in the valuation require the most humility?
- Is there a margin of safety against error?
- Would the thesis still hold under more conservative normalization?

# 41) Buffett-style one-page checklist derived from the 2012 letter

## Business quality
- Is this a wonderful business or merely a cheap one?
- What are returns on tangible capital and incremental capital?
- How durable is demand and competitive position?
- Does the business widen its moat through reinvestment?

## Management quality
- Are managers rational, honest, disciplined, and owner-oriented?
- Do they walk away from bad business?
- Do they allocate capital by per-share value, not ego or size?

## Earnings quality
- What part of earnings is economic versus accounting noise?
- What off-income-statement value is being created or destroyed?
- Are current earnings flattered by temporary legacy conditions?

## Balance sheet and risk
- Can the company survive extreme stress without forced action?
- How dependent is it on leverage, collateral, or refinancing?
- Is liquidity a real strategic asset?

## Capital allocation
- Is retained capital earning attractive returns?
- Are capex, M&A, buybacks, and dividends handled under one rational framework?
- Are buybacks done only below intrinsic value?
- Is share issuance rare and value-conscious?

## Valuation
- What is conservative intrinsic value?
- What is the gap between price and value?
- Does today’s price already assume best-case outcomes?

## Per-share owner outcome
- Will this decision increase per-share intrinsic value?
- Would owners be better off versus the most sensible alternative use of funds?

# 42) Distilled Buffett framework from this letter

The 2012 letter suggests that Buffett evaluates businesses and stocks through the following master logic:

1. **Start with intrinsic value, not reported numbers.**
2. **Judge everything on a per-share basis.**
3. **Prefer durable operating earning power over accounting optics.**
4. **Buy high-quality businesses at rational prices.**
5. **Demand disciplined management that can say no.**
6. **Respect risk as the possibility of permanent impairment or forced vulnerability.**
7. **Treat liquidity and balance-sheet strength as strategic assets.**
8. **Allocate capital only where it raises per-share intrinsic value.**
9. **Repurchase only below intrinsic value; issue shares rarely and carefully.**
10. **Use accounting as a starting point, never as the final truth.**
11. **Segment businesses by their true economics.**
12. **Prefer models that can both endure stress and compound over time.**

# 43) Reusable template for applying this framework to any company

## A. Objective and scorecard
- What should be the correct measure of per-share intrinsic value here?
- What proxy, if any, is management using?
- Is the proxy reliable?

## B. Business economics
- What are returns on tangible and incremental capital?
- How much capital is required to sustain and grow earnings?
- Is the business wonderful, okay, or mediocre?

## C. Competitive durability
- What moat exists?
- Is it widening, stable, or shrinking?
- What could break it?

## D. Management and culture
- Are managers skilled, honest, and disciplined?
- Do they think like owners?
- Do they avoid rationalization and empire building?

## E. Earnings quality
- What reported numbers understate or overstate economic reality?
- What owner earnings are hidden?
- What accounting items require adjustment?

## F. Balance sheet and risk
- Can the business survive severe stress?
- Are there liquidity, collateral, or refinancing dangers?
- Is leverage appropriate for the business model?

## G. Capital allocation
- What is management’s ranked use-of-cash framework?
- Are reinvestment, M&A, buybacks, and dividends rational?
- Has management created value per share historically?

## H. Valuation
- What is conservatively estimated intrinsic value?
- What is today’s margin of safety?
- Is this a good business at a bad price, or a good investment now?

## I. Final Buffett-style decision
- Does this opportunity combine quality, rational management, resilience, and a favorable price?
- Will ownership likely improve long-term per-share intrinsic value?
