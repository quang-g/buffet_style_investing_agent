# Berkshire Hathaway 2002 Shareholder Letter — Reusable Buffett Analysis Framework

Source letter: Berkshire Hathaway 2002 shareholder letter (dated February 21, 2003). Extracted from the user-provided PDF for framework-building purposes.

## Purpose of this extraction

This document does **not** summarize Berkshire’s 2002 year. It extracts the **reusable investment and business-analysis logic** embedded in the letter so it can be reused as a Buffett-style evaluation framework for stocks, companies, and management teams.

---

# 1) Core Buffett Operating Principles

## 1.1 Buy businesses, not tradable pieces of paper
Buffett repeatedly evaluates assets as **businesses with economics**, not as tickers.

Reusable implication:
- Ask what the business economically is.
- Ask how it makes money.
- Ask what returns it earns on capital.
- Ask whether those economics are durable.
- Ask whether management behaves like an owner.

## 1.2 Business quality, management quality, and purchase price are the three-legged entrance test
Buffett explicitly describes Berkshire’s acquisition approach as having three legs:
1. good or great **economic characteristics**
2. great or great-to-great **managers**
3. a **sensible purchase price**

Reusable framework:
- Never evaluate quality without price.
- Never evaluate price without business economics.
- Never evaluate business economics without management.

## 1.3 Buy to keep, not buy to flip
Buffett contrasts Berkshire with LBO/private-equity style “exit” thinking.

Reusable implication:
- Analyze a business as though you may own it indefinitely.
- Prefer businesses whose economics remain attractive without requiring financial engineering or a resale catalyst.
- Ask whether the thesis depends on a future buyer paying more, or on the business itself compounding value.

## 1.4 Capital allocation matters as much as operations
Buffett frames his own role as staying out of the way of strong operators and allocating excess capital.

Reusable implication:
- Separate **operating excellence** from **capital allocation excellence**.
- A good business can be damaged by poor reinvestment, leverage, acquisitions, or compensation policy.
- A mediocre operation can improve if capital discipline and incentives improve.

## 1.5 Substance over form
Throughout the letter, Buffett rejects cosmetic presentation, pro-forma distortions, governance theater, and accounting theatrics.

Reusable implication:
- Prefer economic reality over adjusted narratives.
- Discount management teams that emphasize presentation, metrics engineering, or process theater over real economics.

# 2) What Makes a Good Business?

## 2.1 Strong competitive position can offset a hard industry
Buffett says some acquired companies operate in industries with “tough economics,” yet still possess competitive strengths that allow decent returns on capital.

Reusable principle:
- A difficult industry does not automatically eliminate an investment.
- But in hard industries, only companies with real advantages deserve attention.

Evaluation questions:
- Is the industry structurally attractive or structurally difficult?
- If difficult, what specific advantage lets this firm earn acceptable returns anyway?
- Is the advantage based on cost, brand, distribution, switching costs, regulation, scale, or niche dominance?

## 2.2 Return on capital matters more than growth alone
Buffett repeatedly focuses on whether a business can earn “decent returns on capital,” not merely grow.

Reusable principle:
- Growth without satisfactory returns on capital is weak growth.
- Growth that requires heavy incremental capital but produces poor economics is lower quality than slower growth with high returns.

Evaluation questions:
- What are returns on tangible capital and incremental capital?
- Does growth improve owner value or merely expand the asset base?
- Is management increasing margins while trimming invested capital?

## 2.3 Productivity gains and expense control are real sources of advantage
Buffett praises businesses that improved earnings through productivity and cost discipline, even with little pricing help.

Reusable principle:
- Quality operators can create value through internal efficiency, not only through market growth or pricing power.

Evaluation questions:
- Are margin gains coming from true efficiency or temporary accounting/supportive market conditions?
- Is management cost-conscious in a cultural sense, not just during downturns?
- Can the business improve profits without requiring heroic revenue assumptions?

## 2.4 Dominance plus customer value is powerful
GEICO is praised for combining lower customer prices, strong service, retention, productivity, and growth.

Reusable principle:
- One of the strongest business models is: **lower customer cost + high satisfaction + efficient scale + repeat behavior**.

Evaluation questions:
- Does the business create obvious customer surplus?
- Is customer retention evidence of a real moat?
- Does scale reinforce cost advantage and growth?

## 2.5 Some businesses are attractive despite cyclicality
Buffett notes that some businesses will “always be cyclical,” but can still be attractive.

Reusable principle:
- Cyclicality is not disqualifying.
- The real question is whether the business has favorable long-term economics, survivability, and disciplined acquisition/reinvestment behavior.

Evaluation questions:
- Can the business remain profitable and financially sound through a full cycle?
- Does management buy assets sensibly rather than extrapolate peaks?
- Is cyclicality understood and priced in?

# 3) Management Evaluation Framework

## 3.1 Look for managers who behave like owners
Buffett praises subsidiary CEOs who run businesses “as if they were their own.”

Reusable principle:
- Owner-oriented behavior is a major qualitative edge.

Signals:
- rational capital allocation
- cost discipline
- candor about mistakes
- no empire building
- focus on profitability, not reported volume
- willingness to shrink if pricing or economics are unattractive

## 3.2 Back winners
Buffett’s “Eddie Bennett” story encodes a management principle: outcomes improve when you partner with exceptional operators.

Reusable principle:
- A portfolio of strong managers compounds results beyond what centralized control can achieve.
- One great operator can be worth more than a detailed operating plan.

Evaluation questions:
- Is this a business where management quality truly moves the needle?
- Has the manager demonstrated unusually strong judgment through multiple environments?
- Do results suggest skill, or were they mainly helped by tailwinds?

## 3.3 Judge managers by economics, not scale
Buffett praises Gen Re’s new discipline because management judges itself by underwriting profitability, not size.

Reusable principle:
- Growth in revenue, premiums, assets, or volume is not an achievement if economics deteriorate.

Evaluation questions:
- What metric does management implicitly optimize?
- Would the company walk away from volume if pricing is poor?
- Is compensation tied to size or true economic value creation?

## 3.4 Candor is a key asset
Buffett openly discusses favorable breaks, reserving failures, accounting overstatement risk, and prior mistakes.

Reusable principle:
- Honest managers discuss both good luck and bad judgment.
- Management candor is predictive because it affects future capital allocation, controls, and culture.

Evaluation questions:
- Does management normalize away losses but fully count gains?
- Do they explain mistakes plainly?
- Do they distinguish favorable environment from repeatable skill?

## 3.5 Passion plus operational focus can matter
GEICO’s leader is described as deeply mission-driven and proud of savings, service, employees, and profits.

Reusable principle:
- Mission alignment can reinforce customer value, employee performance, and long-term economics.
- This matters when passion is connected to measurable outcomes, not rhetoric.

# 4) Acquisition Framework

## 4.1 Prefer understandable businesses with proven operators
In several acquisitions, Buffett emphasizes simple business logic and exceptional managers over complexity.

Reusable principle:
- Complexity is not a virtue in acquisitions.
- Simple, proven economics plus strong operators is often superior to exciting strategic narratives.

## 4.2 Small acquisitions can still be attractive if economics are sound
Buffett notes acquisitions below Berkshire’s normal size threshold that still earned meaningful pre-tax profits.

Reusable principle:
- Opportunity size should be judged relative to the investor’s scale.
- Small businesses can be excellent if they earn decent returns and are run well.

## 4.3 Sensible price is part of quality
Buffett explicitly includes sensible purchase price as part of the entrance criteria.

Reusable principle:
- Great business + great manager + bad price can still be a bad investment.

Evaluation questions:
- What normalized pre-tax and after-tax yield is embedded in the purchase price?
- How much optimism must be true for the deal to work?
- Does the deal need margin expansion, leverage, or multiple expansion to produce acceptable returns?

## 4.4 Permanent ownership is a selling advantage when buying whole businesses
Buffett notes Berkshire is often the preferred buyer because sellers know the business will be kept and managers trusted.

Reusable principle:
- Buyer reputation can be a competitive advantage in acquisitions.
- Trust, permanence, and decentralization can improve deal flow and terms.

# 5) Insurance Analysis Framework

Buffett explicitly states that understanding Berkshire requires understanding how to evaluate an insurer.

## 5.1 Three key determinants of insurance value
The key determinants are:
1. the **amount of float** generated
2. the **cost of float**
3. the **long-term outlook** for both

Reusable framework:
- Insurance is attractive when it generates large, durable investable funds at low or negative cost.
- Insurance is unattractive when float is expensive, unstable, or paired with badly understood risk.

## 5.2 Float is valuable only if economically cheap
Float is not inherently valuable. It is valuable only if its cost is lower than alternative funding.

Reusable principle:
- Treat float as funding.
- Ask whether management is being paid to hold it, paying modestly for it, or overpaying for it.

Evaluation questions:
- What is the long-run underwriting result?
- Is float cost below market funding cost?
- Is reported cheap float real or merely the result of temporarily favorable loss experience?

## 5.3 The long-term outlook matters more than a single year
Buffett emphasizes that the most critical variable is the long-term outlook for float amount and cost.

Reusable principle:
- One-year underwriting success can be misleading.
- The key is whether the insurer’s culture and risk selection support low-cost float over time.

## 5.4 The three operating requirements for durable low-cost float
Buffett says low-cost float over time requires that insurers:
- underwrite with unwavering discipline
- reserve conservatively
- avoid aggregating exposures so that an “impossible” event threatens solvency

This is a reusable insurer checklist.

### A. Underwriting discipline
Evaluation questions:
- Will the company walk away from underpriced business?
- Is volume sacrificed when pricing is irrational?
- Is management judged on underwriting profitability or premium growth?

### B. Conservative reserving
Evaluation questions:
- Is management biased toward underreserving?
- Have prior-year reserve developments been adverse?
- Does management explicitly treat reserving accuracy as a top priority?
- Can investors actually know the true economics if reserves are weak?

### C. Exposure aggregation control
Evaluation questions:
- Could a supposedly remote event impair solvency?
- Are concentrations hidden across products/geographies/perils/counterparties?
- Is tail-risk management based on true stress thinking rather than historical comfort?

## 5.5 “Impossible” events must still be planned for
Buffett criticizes risk concentrations that would prove fatal under highly improbable events.

Reusable principle:
- A sound insurer must survive not only expected loss distributions but also low-probability, high-severity events.
- Never dismiss tail risk because it seems unlikely.

## 5.6 Reinsurance counterparty quality matters enormously
Buffett warns that “cheap” reinsurance can be a fool’s bargain if claims are not paid.

Reusable principle:
- Protection is only as good as the counterparty.
- Reported risk transfer may be fake if recovery depends on weak reinsurers.

Evaluation questions:
- What is the quality of counterparties?
- How much of balance-sheet strength depends on collecting from others years later?
- Is the insurer buying price or buying certainty?

## 5.7 Accept volatility if price is right and risk is bounded
Buffett praises Ajit Jain for accepting huge retained risks when paid appropriately, preferring “a lumpy 15% over time” to “a smooth 12%.”

Reusable principle:
- Volatility is acceptable when it is compensated, understood, and survivable.
- Smoothness is not the objective; superior long-run economics are.

Evaluation questions:
- Is volatility merely cosmetic, or does it threaten permanent capital impairment?
- Is the business being paid enough for taking short-term earnings swings?

## 5.8 Retroactive insurance teaches a broader investment lesson
Buffett explains intentionally accepting an underwriting loss in exchange for capped liabilities and long use of funds.

Generalizable principle:
- Some deals that look unattractive on one accounting dimension can be attractive economically if funding duration, caps, and reinvestment opportunity are favorable.
- Always analyze the full economic package, not just the accounting label.

# 6) Investment Framework for Stocks and Securities

## 6.1 Good business is not enough; price must imply an adequate return
Buffett says he will stay on the sidelines unless he sees a very high probability of at least a 10% pre-tax return.

Reusable principle:
- Opportunity cost matters.
- Only act when expected return clears a meaningful hurdle with high probability.

Evaluation questions:
- What is the probable pre-tax return from today’s price?
- How much confidence is there in that estimate?
- Is the spread over cash and other opportunities sufficient?

## 6.2 Inactivity is often rational
Buffett says successful investing sometimes requires inactivity.

Reusable principle:
- Opportunity scarcity is normal.
- Forcing action in mediocre conditions is usually inferior to patient selectivity.

## 6.3 Falling prices do not automatically create cheap stocks
Buffett notes that after three years of falling prices, very few equities even mildly interested him.

Reusable principle:
- Price decline is not the same as undervaluation.
- Always compare price to value, not price to prior price.

## 6.4 Prefer strong businesses, sensible prices, and low loss frequency
Buffett describes Berkshire’s stock investing as focused on conservatively financed businesses with strong competitive strengths and able, honest management.

Reusable principle:
- Equity investing should aim for a low incidence of permanent loss by combining quality with valuation discipline.

Checklist:
- conservative financing
- strong competitive strength
- able management
- honest management
- sensible purchase price

## 6.5 In weaker credits, expect occasional losses and size accordingly
On junk bonds, Buffett notes these are more marginal businesses with more debt, often poorer economics, and sometimes questionable management.

Reusable principle:
- Different asset classes require different loss expectations.
- In low-quality credits, loss frequency and severity are part of the game.

Evaluation questions:
- Are you being paid enough for default, restructuring, governance, and industry risk?
- Is the upside capped while downside remains meaningful?
- Are interests aligned between management and creditors?

## 6.6 Reward/risk ratio comes from scanning many opportunities but selecting few
Buffett describes reviewing hundreds of securities to find a small number with attractive reward/risk.

Reusable principle:
- Breadth of search + narrow selectivity is a core investing process.
- Good investing is often an elimination exercise.

# 7) Accounting and Earnings Quality Framework

## 7.1 Reported earnings are not the same as economic earnings
Buffett repeatedly distinguishes reported results from normalized or economic reality.

Reusable principle:
- Start with reported numbers, but do not stop there.
- Adjust for unusual favorable conditions, temporary market effects, acquisition accounting distortions, and management presentation bias.

## 7.2 Be skeptical of “pro-forma” and “except-for” narratives
Buffett criticizes the practice of excluding recurring-but-unpleasant economic realities.

Reusable principle:
- If a business model structurally includes certain losses or costs, they must be counted.
- Normalization should be symmetric: remove unusual gains as well as unusual losses.

Evaluation questions:
- Is management excluding only bad items, or also stripping out favorable anomalies?
- Are “one-time” charges actually part of the business?
- Are catastrophic events in fact part of the economic model?

## 7.3 Favor managers who reduce, not inflate, normalized earnings
Buffett unusually notes Berkshire’s pro-forma earnings would have been lower than reported earnings because favorable factors should be normalized downward.

Reusable principle:
- Upward adjustment is easy; honest downward normalization is rare and informative.

## 7.4 Weak accounting is often a broad cultural warning sign
Buffett warns that visible weak accounting usually signals broader issues: “There is seldom just one cockroach in the kitchen.”

Reusable principle:
- Aggressive accounting in one area should raise suspicion everywhere else.

Red flags:
- no option expensing
- fanciful pension assumptions
- EBITDA promotion as a substitute for real earnings
- unintelligible footnotes
- earnings guidance obsession

## 7.5 Depreciation is real
Buffett strongly attacks EBITDA-based thinking that ignores depreciation.

Reusable principle:
- Non-cash does not mean non-economic.
- Any analysis that ignores the replacement cost of assets overstates economic earnings.

Evaluation questions:
- What maintenance capital expenditure is required to sustain current earnings?
- Is depreciation understated relative to economic wear and replacement needs?
- Is management using EBITDA to distract from capital intensity?

## 7.6 Unintelligible disclosures are a warning
Buffett says when a footnote is unintelligible, often the CEO does not want you to understand it.

Reusable principle:
- Complexity in disclosure can be evidence of either genuine complexity or deliberate obscurity.
- Investors should treat opacity itself as a risk factor.

## 7.7 Beware managers who promise to “make the numbers”
Buffett warns that managers who consistently promise to hit targets may eventually make up the numbers.

Reusable principle:
- Precision promises in an uncertain business environment often indicate cultural pressure that can lead to manipulation.

Evaluation questions:
- Does management over-guide?
- Are results suspiciously smooth despite volatile business drivers?
- Is the communication style probabilistic and candid, or promotional and target-driven?

# 8) Derivatives and Complex Financial Risk Framework

## 8.1 Avoid what cannot be reliably valued
Buffett criticizes derivatives for weak valuation reliability, especially when markets do not exist and models dominate.

Reusable principle:
- If value depends heavily on assumptions, complexity, and long-dated models, reported economics deserve a large discount.

Evaluation questions:
- Is the position mark-to-market or effectively mark-to-model?
- How sensitive is value to assumptions?
- Could two honest experts produce widely different values?

## 8.2 Incentives can corrupt accounting in complex instruments
Buffett notes traders and CEOs may benefit from overstated model-based profits.

Reusable principle:
- Complexity plus incentive pay is a dangerous combination.
- The harder something is to audit, the more important incentives become.

## 8.3 Counterparty and collateral triggers can turn a problem into a crisis
Buffett describes downgrade-triggered collateral calls and liquidity spirals.

Reusable principle:
- Hidden contractual liquidity demands can matter more than reported leverage.

Evaluation questions:
- What happens under downgrade scenarios?
- Are there contingent collateral or margin requirements?
- Could unrelated adversity trigger a liquidity crisis?

## 8.4 Correlation rises in crises
Buffett warns that exposures appearing diversified may become correlated under stress.

Reusable principle:
- Stress diversification, not just ordinary-period diversification.
- In crisis analysis, assume linkages strengthen.

## 8.5 Systemic and chain-reaction risk matter
Buffett sees derivatives and reinsurance as chains where one failure can infect others.

Reusable principle:
- Ask not only “What is this company’s direct risk?” but also “How vulnerable is it to the weakness of others?”

## 8.6 Prefer fortress balance sheets when risks are hard to map
Buffett wants Berkshire to be a “fortress of financial strength.”

Reusable principle:
- The less knowable the risk environment, the more valuable excess financial strength becomes.

Evaluation questions:
- Can the business survive severe but plausible shocks without dependence on external rescue?
- Is the firm over-optimized for normal times?

# 9) Corporate Governance and Stewardship Framework

## 9.1 Directors should think like a single absentee owner
Buffett’s standard for directors is that they act as if representing one absentee owner’s long-term interests.

Reusable principle:
- Governance should be evaluated through alignment, not box-checking.

## 9.2 Directors have two primary jobs
Buffett highlights two core board duties:
1. remove mediocre or worse managers
2. stop able but greedy managers from overreaching into shareholder pockets

Reusable principle:
- Governance failure often occurs either through tolerance of incompetence or tolerance of greed.

## 9.3 Independence without business savvy and shareholder orientation is weak
Buffett argues “independent” directors are often ineffective unless they also are:
- business-savvy
- interested
- shareholder-oriented

Reusable principle:
- Formal independence is not enough.
- Evaluate whether the board is actually capable and motivated.

## 9.4 Incentives and atmosphere shape board behavior
Buffett emphasizes “boardroom atmosphere” and the social reluctance to challenge CEOs.

Reusable principle:
- Governance quality depends on culture, courage, and incentives, not merely rules.

Evaluation questions:
- Are directors economically aligned with owners?
- Do they meaningfully challenge acquisitions, pay, and accounting?
- Are meetings structured to allow real dissent?

## 9.5 Large true ownership improves alignment
Buffett prefers directors with significant purchased ownership, not gifted stock or options.

Reusable principle:
- Real money at risk can matter more than nominal independence tests.

## 9.6 Compensation committees should negotiate like owners
Buffett criticizes consultant-led pay escalation.

Reusable principle:
- Pay should reflect true exceptional performance, not peer-group inflation or consultant rationalization.

Evaluation questions:
- Is pay justified by actual value creation?
- Is the committee capable of resisting management?
- Is performance measurement clear and economically grounded?

# 10) Audit and Disclosure Framework

## 10.1 Audit committees should force clarity, not perform ritual oversight
Buffett says audit committees cannot audit; their real job is to get auditors to divulge what they know.

Reusable principle:
- The value of the audit committee lies in its ability to extract honesty and surface disagreements.

## 10.2 Buffett’s four audit questions are a reusable due-diligence template

### Question 1
If the auditor alone prepared the statements, would anything be done differently?

Use:
- surfaces management aggressiveness
- reveals judgment areas hidden inside “acceptable GAAP”

### Question 2
If the auditor were an investor, would they have received the plain-English information needed to understand performance?

Use:
- tests disclosure quality
- tests whether communication is informative rather than merely compliant

### Question 3
Would the auditor follow the same internal audit procedures if the auditor were CEO?

Use:
- tests control adequacy
- reveals whether internal safeguards are weaker than a prudent owner would choose

### Question 4
Is the auditor aware of any accounting or operational actions meant to move revenues or expenses across periods?

Use:
- tests earnings management directly
- identifies smoothing behavior

## 10.3 Haste reduces reporting quality
Buffett argues that rushed reporting harms accuracy.

Reusable principle:
- Speed is not always shareholder-friendly if it reduces truthfulness and careful review.

# 11) Buffett-Style Red Flags

These warning signs are reusable across company analysis:

## 11.1 Growth or size prioritized over profitability
- premium growth without underwriting discipline
- acquisitions justified by scale rather than returns
- revenue celebration without capital efficiency

## 11.2 Weak or promotional accounting
- EBITDA emphasis
- no option expensing
- fanciful assumptions
- unreadable footnotes
- recurring “one-time” adjustments

## 11.3 Overconfidence in forecasts
- management that routinely promises exact targets
- suspiciously smooth outcomes in volatile businesses

## 11.4 Misaligned incentives
- pay linked to volume or optics
- consultants effectively setting compensation
- boards dependent on fees or prestige

## 11.5 Tail risk blindness
- aggregation exposure not understood
- reliance on weak counterparties
- hidden liquidity triggers
- too much trust in statistical normality

## 11.6 Complexity that masks economics
- hard-to-value derivative books
- long-dated contracts with subjective models
- disclosures that obscure rather than explain

# 12) Buffett-Style Positive Signals

## 12.1 Management candor
- openly admits mistakes
- distinguishes luck from skill
- normalizes both good and bad anomalies

## 12.2 Discipline over activity
- willing to do nothing when opportunities are poor
- willing to shrink or walk away when economics are unattractive

## 12.3 Cost consciousness
- visible cultural frugality
- productivity and margin gains from operations, not just accounting

## 12.4 Real customer value
- lower customer cost
- better service
- strong retention or repeat usage

## 12.5 Financial strength
- survivability under severe stress
- no dependence on fragile counterparties or short-term markets

## 12.6 Honest reporting
- plain-English disclosures
- recognition of true expenses
- limited use of adjusted metrics

# 13) Reusable Buffett Question Bank for Analyzing a Company

## Business economics
1. What are the core economics of this business?
2. Does it earn attractive returns on tangible and incremental capital?
3. Does it possess a real competitive strength, especially in a tough industry?
4. Are margins supported by moat, efficiency, or temporary conditions?
5. Does growth create value or just consume capital?

## Management
6. Does management behave like an owner?
7. Is management candid about mistakes, favorable breaks, and accounting reality?
8. Does management optimize for value creation or for size, volume, and appearances?
9. Would this team walk away from unattractive business?
10. Is capital allocated rationally?

## Financial quality
11. Are reported earnings close to economic earnings?
12. What adjustments are needed to normalize results fairly?
13. Are there signs of weak accounting culture?
14. Are disclosures understandable?
15. Is depreciation or another real cost being mentally ignored by the market?

## Balance sheet and risk
16. What could cause permanent capital impairment here?
17. Are there hidden concentrations, counterparty risks, or collateral triggers?
18. Can the firm survive an “impossible” but plausible severe event?
19. Is leverage conservative relative to business risk?
20. Is volatility cosmetic, or existential?

## Governance
21. Are directors aligned, capable, and shareholder-oriented?
22. Will the board restrain greed as well as incompetence?
23. Is executive compensation tied to real value creation?
24. Are auditors likely to surface problems, or merely tolerate them?

## Valuation
25. What high-probability pre-tax return does today’s price imply?
26. Is the stock actually undervalued, or merely down?
27. Does the expected return clear a meaningful hurdle versus alternatives?
28. How much optimism is required for the investment to work?
29. What is the downside if business quality is lower than it appears?

# 14) Performance Logic Embedded in the Letter

This letter implies a consistent Buffett performance logic:

1. **Business quality** drives long-term economics.
2. **Management quality** determines whether those economics are protected or wasted.
3. **Accounting quality** determines whether the investor can see reality.
4. **Balance-sheet strength** determines survivability under stress.
5. **Valuation discipline** determines whether good economics become a good investment.
6. **Patience** is required because acceptable opportunities are infrequent.
7. **Avoidance of hidden risk** matters as much as pursuit of upside.

In shorthand:

**Good business + able/honest management + strong finances + understandable accounting + sensible price + patience = Buffett-compatible opportunity.**

# 15) Compact Extraction for a Future Knowledge Base Schema

- Buy businesses, not tickers.
- Demand business quality, management quality, and sensible price together.
- Prefer permanent-ownership economics.
- Emphasize economic reality over adjusted presentation.

- strong competitive position
- decent to high returns on capital
- conservative financing
- understandable business model
- honest and able management
- transparent accounting

# 16) Bottom Line

The 2002 Berkshire letter provides a reusable Buffett framework centered on six recurring tests:

1. **Economic quality of the business**
2. **Quality and alignment of management**
3. **Truthfulness of accounting and disclosure**
4. **Strength against tail risk and hidden linkages**
5. **Governance that protects owners**
6. **Valuation that offers a high-probability satisfactory return**

Its deeper lesson is that great investing is not built on prediction, activity, or presentation. It is built on disciplined selection, honest accounting, durable economics, strong stewardship, and refusal to overpay.
