# Berkshire Hathaway 2003 Shareholder Letter  
## Reusable Buffett-Style Stock/Business Analysis Framework Extraction

Source letter: Berkshire Hathaway Chairman's Letter dated February 27, 2004, covering 2003 results. This framework extraction is derived from the attached letter rather than from external commentary.

---

## 1) What this extraction is trying to capture

This document does **not** summarize Berkshire’s 2003 events.

Instead, it extracts the **reusable analytical framework** embedded in the letter — the principles, filters, mental models, evaluation questions, and decision logic Warren Buffett uses to assess:

- a business,
- a stock,
- a management team,
- a capital allocation opportunity,
- a governance structure,
- and a financial risk profile.

The goal is to convert Buffett’s narrative into a **repeatable investment analysis framework** that can later be applied to other public companies.

---

## 2) Core meta-principles Buffett uses throughout the letter

## 2.1 Intrinsic value matters more than accounting optics
Buffett explicitly says that **per-share intrinsic value** is what counts, not book value. Book value can be a useful proxy in some settings, but it is still only a proxy.

### Reusable principle
When analyzing a company, always ask:

- What is the **economic value per share**?
- How much does accounting understate or overstate economic reality?
- Is the reported metric merely convenient, or is it actually decision-useful?

### Analytical implication
Do not anchor on:
- reported earnings alone,
- book value alone,
- GAAP presentation alone,
- or surface growth rates alone.

Focus on **economic earning power**, **capital allocation quality**, and **per-share intrinsic value growth**.

---

## 2.2 Measure performance on a per-share basis
Buffett consistently frames performance in **per-share** terms, not merely aggregate terms.

### Reusable principle
A business can grow total earnings while destroying owner value if:
- dilution is heavy,
- capital is misallocated,
- acquisitions are overpriced,
- or incremental returns are weak.

### Evaluation questions
- Has intrinsic value per share increased?
- Has earning power per share improved?
- Did capital allocation increase value per share, not just size?

---

## 2.3 Compare active management against the passive alternative
Buffett states that shareholders can buy the S&P 500 cheaply. Therefore Berkshire must outperform that passive option in intrinsic-value terms or it adds no value.

### Reusable principle
Every active investment decision should be judged against the **best simple alternative**.

### Evaluation questions
- Why own this company instead of an index fund or a clearly superior alternative?
- What specific edge justifies active selection?
- Is management actually creating value beyond what a passive option could deliver?

### Practical use
This becomes a hurdle-rate mindset:
- not “Is this acceptable?”
- but “Is this superior to the default alternative?”

---

## 2.4 Underutilized capital is better than stupid deployment
Buffett says underutilized capital is painful, but less painful than doing something stupid.

### Reusable principle
Capital allocation discipline includes the ability to **do nothing** when opportunities are unattractive.

### Evaluation questions
- Is management forcing capital into low-return uses just to stay active?
- Does the company exhibit patience when assets are overpriced?
- Is inactivity a sign of discipline or inability?

---

## 2.5 Flexibility in capital allocation is a structural edge
Buffett says Berkshire will buy:
- stocks when stocks are cheaper than businesses,
- bonds when bonds are attractive,
- businesses when businesses meet standards.

### Reusable principle
Superior capital allocators are **opportunity-set agnostic**. They allocate where risk-adjusted returns are best rather than where identity, tradition, or fashion tells them to allocate.

### Evaluation questions
- Is management free to move across asset classes or strategic uses of capital?
- Is the company locked into one capital allocation behavior because of culture, incentives, or investor pressure?
- Does management buy what is cheap, or what is conventional?

## 3) Business-quality framework

## 3.1 Buffett’s acquisition/business selection triad
Buffett gives a crisp three-part filter for businesses Berkshire wants to buy:

1. **Favorable and enduring economic characteristics**
2. **Talented and honest managers**
3. **Available at a sensible price**

This is one of the most reusable frameworks in the entire letter.

---

## 3.2 Favorable and enduring economic characteristics

### Reusable principle
A good business is not merely one with recent growth. It has economics that are favorable **and durable**.

### What Buffett is looking for
- durable profitability,
- resilience,
- staying power,
- ability to compound,
- economics that do not depend on temporary conditions,
- and competitive characteristics that can last.

### Evaluation questions
- What makes the economics favorable?
- What makes those advantages enduring rather than cyclical or temporary?
- How fragile is the business model?
- What could structurally erode margins, returns, or volume?
- Does growth come from real economics or from leverage, accounting, or favorable industry conditions?

### Translation into analytical criteria
Look for:
- durable pricing power or cost advantage,
- strong customer value proposition,
- efficient operations,
- sound unit economics,
- repeat demand,
- low need for promotional distortion,
- and low dependence on external financing for survival.

---

## 3.3 Talented and honest managers

### Reusable principle
Management quality is not just intelligence or charisma. Buffett repeatedly emphasizes:

- talent,
- discipline,
- integrity,
- straight-shooting behavior,
- and rational decision-making.

### What he rewards
- candor,
- accurate reserving,
- disciplined underwriting,
- shareholder orientation,
- willingness to avoid stupidity,
- ability to operate without accounting manipulation,
- and behavior that protects long-term economics.

### Evaluation questions
- Is management both able and honest?
- Does management communicate economically, not cosmetically?
- Does management resist pressure to optimize appearances?
- Does management admit mistakes clearly?
- Is capital deployment rational?
- Does management understand risk deeply, or merely report numbers confidently?

### Red flags
- promotional behavior,
- aggressive accounting,
- front-ended earnings,
- illusory profits,
- reserve games,
- growth without economic logic,
- and actions that enrich managers while weakening owners.

---

## 3.4 Sensible price

### Reusable principle
Even excellent businesses can be poor investments at the wrong price. Buffett says Berkshire owns excellent businesses whose prices already reflect that excellence.

### Evaluation questions
- Is the current price already discounting quality?
- What future outcome is embedded in today’s valuation?
- Is there a margin between business value and market price?
- Is this a wonderful business bought sensibly, or merely a wonderful story bought expensively?

### Performance logic
A stock can disappoint even while the underlying business performs well if:
- entry price was too high,
- expectations were unrealistic,
- or the market had already capitalized the quality.

---

## 4) Operating earnings and performance interpretation framework

## 4.1 Do not trust impressive growth rates without examining the base period
Buffett warns that growth-rate presentations can be distorted by conveniently chosen starting and ending dates.

### Reusable principle
Growth statistics are only meaningful when base-year and terminal-year selection are economically fair.

### Evaluation questions
- Is the company using an unusually depressed base year?
- Is the terminal year unusually strong?
- Is the reported CAGR economically representative?
- Does the time window flatter the narrative?

### Analytical rule
Always re-test growth from multiple base years.

---

## 4.2 Exclude distortions when measuring operating performance
Buffett highlights operating earnings excluding capital gains.

### Reusable principle
Separate:
- recurring operating performance,
- capital gains,
- accounting noise,
- and one-off items.

### Evaluation questions
- What portion of reported earnings is recurring?
- What portion is driven by asset sales, valuation changes, or unusual conditions?
- Is the company’s core engine actually improving?

---

## 4.3 Analyze the business as an unfolding movie, not a still photograph
Buffett explicitly says Berkshire should be viewed as an unfolding movie, not a still photograph.

### Reusable principle
Static financial snapshots often mislead when the business is changing in structure, mix, capital intensity, or earning composition.

### Evaluation questions
- What is changing beneath the surface?
- Is the business migrating toward better economics or worse economics?
- Are temporary accounting presentations masking long-term direction?
- What will this business likely look like in 3-10 years?

---

## 5) Capital allocation framework

## 5.1 Capital should flow to the cheapest high-quality opportunity
Buffett’s cross-asset flexibility implies a general rule:

### Reusable principle
Allocate capital to the opportunity with the best combination of:
- quality,
- expected return,
- downside protection,
- and scale.

### Evaluation questions
- Are stocks cheaper than whole businesses?
- Are bonds mispriced relative to risk?
- Is internal reinvestment superior to acquisitions?
- Is buyback activity economically justified?
- Is cash the right answer for now?

---

## 5.2 Size matters because opportunity sets shrink with scale
Buffett notes that as Berkshire grows, the number of opportunities large enough to matter declines sharply.

### Reusable principle
As capital base grows, investable opportunity set narrows.

### Evaluation questions
- Can this company continue compounding at its current size?
- Is management forced into lower-return uses of capital because the company is too large?
- Are returns on incremental capital likely to decline?

### Practical implication
Distinguish between:
- a great small compounding business,
- and a huge company facing reinvestment constraints.

---

## 5.3 Avoid forced action
Buffett values the freedom not to act under external pressure.

### Reusable principle
Institutional constraints can damage capital allocation:
- Wall Street pressure,
- legacy business identity,
- growth expectations,
- or investor demands for constant action.

### Evaluation questions
- Is management acting for owners or for quarterly optics?
- What constraints narrow their rational choices?
- Would they allocate capital differently if left alone?

---

## 6) Industry and business-model analysis framework

## 6.1 A good product does not guarantee a good industry
Buffett’s manufactured housing discussion shows that a product can offer genuine consumer value while the industry structure remains broken.

### Reusable principle
Separate:
- product usefulness,
- industry economics,
- distribution incentives,
- and financing model quality.

### Evaluation questions
- Is the customer proposition strong but the industry economics bad?
- Are intermediaries paid in ways that encourage destructive behavior?
- Does the financing/distribution model create future losses?
- Is growth being driven by unsound credit or unsound incentives?

---

## 6.2 Follow the incentive chain, not just the product
In manufactured housing, Buffett highlights how retailers and lenders profited upfront by generating loans destined to default.

### Reusable principle
Bad incentives can make a business appear healthy while planting future losses.

### Evaluation questions
- Who gets paid upfront?
- Who bears the eventual loss?
- Is any part of the chain rewarded for volume rather than sound economics?
- Are originators retaining risk or offloading it?

### Red flags
- originate-to-distribute models with weak underwriting,
- securitization that distances risk from decision-makers,
- compensation tied to volume rather than quality,
- and large current earnings paired with future credit pain.

---

## 6.3 Business model quality matters more than temporary market share
Buffett argues the industry needed a different model with meaningful down payments and shorter loans.

### Reusable principle
A smaller industry with sound economics is better than a larger industry fueled by bad practices.

### Evaluation questions
- Is current scale sustainable?
- Would the business still work under stricter standards?
- How much of present volume is real versus pulled forward by bad economics?

---

## 7) Management evaluation framework

## 7.1 Ability + discipline + integrity is the high bar
In insurance, Buffett says the critical variables are managerial brains, discipline, and integrity.

### Reusable principle
In commoditized or weak-moat industries, management quality may be the primary differentiator.

### Evaluation questions
- Is success here primarily a function of managerial excellence?
- Does management show discipline in pricing, reserving, underwriting, and growth?
- Is integrity observable in disclosures, decisions, and error acknowledgment?

---

## 7.2 Look for straight-shooters
Buffett praises Kevin Clayton for being “able and a straight-shooter.”

### Reusable principle
Candid, reality-based communicators are valuable because they reduce the risk of hidden problems.

### Evaluation questions
- Does management answer hard questions directly?
- Do disclosures clarify or obscure?
- Does management behave like a seller of stock, or an owner of a business?

---

## 7.3 Judge managers by how they behave when conditions are favorable
Buffett repeatedly criticizes CEOs who use favorable conditions to overreach, obscure, or enrich themselves.

### Reusable principle
Easy conditions reveal character:
- loose underwriting in booms,
- over-optimistic accounting,
- aggressive compensation,
- and risk transfer dressed up as innovation.

### Evaluation questions
- What did management do when money was easy?
- Did they tighten standards or loosen them?
- Did they preserve discipline while peers lost theirs?

---

## 8) Governance framework

## 8.1 Governance is tested most clearly in CEO pay
Buffett calls CEO pay the acid test of whether Corporate America is serious about reform.

### Reusable principle
Governance quality becomes visible where incentives and money are concentrated.

### Evaluation questions
- Is compensation fair relative to value delivered?
- Is pay negotiated like real money or play money?
- Are managers rewarded for genuine owner outcomes or for weakly measured activity?

---

## 8.2 Independence is not a formal label — it is economic reality plus backbone
Buffett argues statutory independence is often meaningless.

### Reusable principle
A director is not truly independent because rules say so. True independence requires:
- aligned economics,
- low dependence on board fees,
- business judgment,
- and willingness to challenge management.

### Evaluation questions
- How economically dependent are directors on the board role?
- Do directors have meaningful owner-like exposure?
- Do they have enough business savvy to challenge management?
- Is the board selected to monitor management or to ornament the company?

---

## 8.3 Ownership alignment matters
Buffett strongly favors directors who own significant stock purchased with their own money.

### Reusable principle
“Honest-to-God ownership” is better than option-based appearance alignment.

### Evaluation questions
- Do directors and executives own real stock bought with real money?
- Is upside proportional to ordinary shareholders’ upside?
- Is downside real?

---

## 8.4 Business savvy on boards is essential
Buffett says smart and admired people may still have no real understanding of business and therefore do not belong on corporate boards.

### Reusable principle
Prestige is not competence.

### Evaluation questions
- Can board members critically evaluate business proposals?
- Do they understand capital allocation, incentives, accounting, and strategic tradeoffs?
- Or are they effectively decorative?

---

## 9) Insurance and float framework

## 9.1 Float is valuable only if its cost is low or negative
Buffett describes float as wonderful if it does not come at a high price.

### Reusable principle
Low-cost liabilities can be a major competitive advantage.

### Analytical translation
For insurers, the key question is not just asset returns but:
- what does the funding base cost,
- how stable is it,
- and how safely can it be invested?

### Evaluation questions
- Is float growing?
- Is underwriting profitable or at least disciplined?
- Is the cost of float attractive through time?
- Is growth in float coming from good business or from underpriced risk?

---

## 9.2 Underwriting profit is a signal of franchise quality and discipline
Buffett values insurance operations that generate float while also earning underwriting profits.

### Reusable principle
The best insurers are paid to hold money.

### Evaluation questions
- Are underwriting results consistently rational?
- Does management chase premium volume at the expense of profitability?
- Are reserves likely adequate?
- Is reported profitability likely real?

---

## 9.3 Reserve adequacy is critical and easily abused
Buffett explicitly warns that long-tail insurance can report almost any earnings a CEO desires by misstating liabilities.

### Reusable principle
In businesses with estimation-heavy liabilities, accounting can be highly manipulable.

### Evaluation questions
- How much judgment exists in reserves or provisions?
- What incentives exist to understate liabilities?
- Does management discuss reserve uncertainty candidly?
- Have prior-year reserve deficiencies appeared repeatedly?

### Broader application beyond insurance
This logic applies to any company with:
- credit provisions,
- warranty reserves,
- litigation exposures,
- pension assumptions,
- or long-dated contingent liabilities.

---

## 9.4 Financial strength matters most when the system is stressed
Buffett emphasizes Gen Re/National Indemnity’s ability to pay without depending on others.

### Reusable principle
A financial company’s true strength is revealed when counterparties or the broader system are under stress.

### Evaluation questions
- Is the company independently strong or dependent on fragile counterparties?
- Can it honor commitments in a crisis without external rescue?
- Does it rely on reinsurance, wholesale funding, or short-term markets in ways that could fail simultaneously?

---

## 10) Balance-sheet and leverage framework

## 10.1 Debt is acceptable when matched to stable, durable economics
Buffett says Berkshire typically shuns debt, but accepts it at MidAmerican because the utility cash flows are stable and diverse.

### Reusable principle
Leverage is not judged abstractly. It is judged relative to:
- business stability,
- cash-flow predictability,
- asset-liability matching,
- and downside resilience.

### Evaluation questions
- Are the underlying cash flows stable enough to support debt through harsh conditions?
- Is debt housed where the economics justify it?
- Is there non-recourse or ring-fenced structure protecting the parent?
- Would the business remain comfortable under stress?

---

## 10.2 “Every tub on its own bottom”
Buffett says subsidiaries that lend money should pay an appropriate rate for funds and should not be subsidized by a rich parent.

### Reusable principle
Each business should bear its true economic cost of capital.

### Evaluation questions
- Is a business appearing attractive only because the parent subsidizes it?
- Are internal transfer prices realistic?
- Would the business still look good on a stand-alone basis?

### Analytical use
This is a powerful anti-distortion rule for conglomerates and internal-capital-market analysis.

---

## 10.3 Keep dry powder for truly attractive opportunities
Buffett notes that hidden or uncertain risks can force a company to hold more cash than ideal, which can create opportunity cost.

### Reusable principle
Liquidity is strategic optionality. But hidden risk can neutralize that optionality at the worst time.

### Evaluation questions
- Is liquidity genuinely available for deployment, or effectively reserved against hidden problems?
- Could one troubled business absorb capital exactly when better opportunities appear elsewhere?

---

## 11) Accounting-quality framework

## 11.1 Reported profits may be illusory
Buffett says Gen Re Securities’ reported profits struck him as illusory.

### Reusable principle
A business with opaque marks, model-based earnings, or complex contracts may report profits that are not economically reliable.

### Evaluation questions
- How much of earnings depends on model assumptions?
- Are mark-to-market gains reversible?
- Are reported profits cash-backed?
- Would an informed outsider be able to understand the true economics?

---

## 11.2 Complexity increases the odds that disclosure is insufficient
Buffett says even sophisticated readers cannot really learn from derivatives disclosures what risks lurk in positions.

### Reusable principle
When an activity is sufficiently complex, disclosure may not cure opacity.

### Evaluation questions
- Is the business understandable enough for reliable outside analysis?
- Are risks concentrated in instruments or structures that cannot be evaluated with confidence?
- Is the company’s confidence greater than the actual measurability of the risk?

---

## 11.3 Front-ended accounting should be treated with skepticism
In Clayton’s prior securitization model, Buffett notes the by-product was front-ending of income due to GAAP.

### Reusable principle
Accounting that accelerates earnings recognition can exaggerate economics.

### Evaluation questions
- Are profits being recognized long before cash economics are proven?
- Does the accounting favor current reported income over long-term realism?
- Would earnings look different if management chose to retain risk instead of selling it?

---

## 11.4 Consolidation can distort analytical interpretation
Buffett criticizes consolidation that brings in liabilities that are not economically Berkshire’s.

### Reusable principle
Accounting presentation may not match economic exposure.

### Evaluation questions
- What liabilities are truly recourse to shareholders?
- What part of the balance sheet is economically attributable to the parent?
- Does consolidation obscure rather than clarify?

---

## 12) Derivatives and financial-risk framework

## 12.1 Avoid risks that cannot be measured or bounded reliably
Buffett says Gen Re Securities carried sizable risks that could not effectively be measured or limited.

### Reusable principle
Do not rely on precision in areas where the underlying risk is structurally difficult to know.

### Evaluation questions
- Can downside truly be measured?
- Are stress scenarios credible?
- Is the distribution of outcomes fat-tailed or path-dependent?
- Could correlations rise exactly when capital is needed elsewhere?

---

## 12.2 Beware positions that demand capital at the worst possible time
Buffett notes derivatives problems could commandeer Berkshire’s capital and credit precisely when those resources would otherwise be most valuable.

### Reusable principle
A bad risk is one that becomes largest when opportunity cost is highest.

### Evaluation questions
- Could this business consume liquidity in a system-wide panic?
- Is its downside correlated with broader distress?
- Would it impair the ability to buy bargains during crises?

---

## 12.3 Low apparent risk is not the same as low real risk
Buffett’s criticism of derivatives-intensive accounting and Freddie Mac–style practices reflects a larger idea:

### Reusable principle
Smooth reported earnings in finance can conceal unstable underlying exposures.

### Evaluation questions
- Is the company generating stability from real resilience or from accounting convention?
- What has to remain benign for the model to work?
- What happens if counterparties, liquidity, or prices gap?

---

## 13) Investment portfolio framework

## 13.1 Own pieces of excellent businesses — but valuation still governs returns
Buffett says Berkshire owns excellent businesses, but their prices reflect their excellence.

### Reusable principle
Business quality and investment attractiveness are related but distinct.

### Evaluation questions
- Is the business excellent?
- Is the stock excellent at this price?
- What part of future success is already embedded?

---

## 13.2 Failure to sell can also be a capital allocation error
Buffett openly says he made a big mistake in not selling several large holdings during the bubble.

### Reusable principle
Capital allocation errors include both:
- bad buys,
- and failures to act when valuation becomes extreme.

### Evaluation questions
- Is there a reasonable case for trimming or exiting due to valuation?
- Is inaction rooted in rational tax considerations, or in inertia?
- Has price moved far beyond intrinsic value?

---

## 13.3 Macro forecasting is weak ground; default to security-level discipline
Buffett says the cemetery for seers has a huge section for macro forecasters.

### Reusable principle
Do not build an investment process that depends on frequent macro heroics.

### Evaluation questions
- Is the thesis based on company-level economics or macro prediction?
- If macro calls are involved, are they modest, protective, and secondary?
- Can the investment still work without precise macro timing?

---

## 13.4 Never reach for yield without adequate compensation
Buffett says Berkshire never reaches for a little more income by lowering credit standards or extending maturities.

### Reusable principle
Incremental yield is often purchased with hidden risk.

### Evaluation questions
- What exactly is being sacrificed for extra yield?
- Credit quality?
- Duration safety?
- Liquidity?
- Optionality?

---

## 14) Competitive advantage framework

## 14.1 In some industries, moats are weak and execution is everything
Buffett says insurers sell non-proprietary promises on non-proprietary paper, with little protection from brand, patents, or physical assets.

### Reusable principle
A company may lack classic structural moat characteristics yet still be superior because of managerial excellence and disciplined operations.

### Evaluation questions
- Is advantage structural, managerial, cultural, cost-based, regulatory, or distribution-based?
- If structural moats are weak, how repeatable is managerial edge?
- Does success depend on one extraordinary operator or an embedded culture/system?

---

## 14.2 Consumer trust, cost advantage, and service can form durable strengths
Examples in the letter imply several recurring advantage types:
- lowest-price reputation,
- strong brand,
- leading service,
- scale,
- financial strength,
- and customer trust in critical situations.

### Evaluation questions
- Why do customers choose this company?
- Is that advantage visible in share gains, retention, margins, or returns?
- Can competitors easily copy it?

---

## 15) Segment analysis framework

## 15.1 Analyze businesses by economic type, not just consolidated totals
Buffett says consolidated figures can become a jumble and make useful analysis almost impossible.

### Reusable principle
Break conglomerates or multi-segment businesses into economically coherent units.

### Evaluation questions
- Which segments are capital-intensive?
- Which segments earn high returns on tangible capital?
- Which segments depend on leverage, float, or regulation?
- Which segments drive intrinsic value versus accounting bulk?

---

## 15.2 Use segment economics to avoid misleading averages
A blended number can hide:
- excellent businesses inside weak groups,
- weak businesses inside large reported revenue,
- or capital-heavy segments overshadowing high-return units.

### Evaluation questions
- What are segment-level returns?
- What is the cash-generation profile by segment?
- Which units deserve valuation premiums or discounts?

---

## 16) Return-on-capital framework

## 16.1 Distinguish returns on tangible net worth from returns on carrying value
Buffett notes the manufacturing/service/retail group earned 20.7% on average tangible net worth, but only 9.2% on carrying value due to acquisition premiums.

### Reusable principle
Different capital bases answer different questions.

### Evaluation questions
- What return is the business earning on tangible operating capital?
- What return is the owner earning on total invested capital including goodwill?
- Are acquisitions being made at prices that dilute future returns?

### Analytical use
This is especially important when evaluating acquisitive companies.

---

## 17) Customer-value framework

## 17.1 A strong business often delivers obvious customer value
Across GEICO, NFM, NetJets, and Clayton, Buffett highlights businesses that solve customer problems clearly:
- lower cost,
- better service,
- reliability,
- quality,
- or special trust.

### Reusable principle
Durable economics often start with a genuine customer advantage.

### Evaluation questions
- What concrete value does the company create for the customer?
- Is the value strong enough to drive repeat behavior or share gains?
- Is the business winning because it helps customers, or because it exploits temporary market distortions?

---

## 18) Error-analysis framework

## 18.1 Study mistakes of commission and omission
Buffett repeatedly names both:
- mistakes of commission,
- and mistakes of omission.

### Reusable principle
A complete investment review process examines:
- bad actions taken,
- and good actions not taken.

### Evaluation questions
- What did management buy that it should not have bought?
- What did it fail to buy or sell when it should have acted?
- What patterns recur in those mistakes?

---

## 18.2 Candor about mistakes is a positive signal
Buffett openly admits:
- acquisition mistakes,
- failure to sell in the bubble,
- delay in shutting down derivatives,
- and reserve issues.

### Reusable principle
Management candor is diagnostic. Honest discussion of error often signals stronger stewardship than polished self-congratulation.

### Evaluation questions
- Does management discuss errors specifically?
- Are those admissions economically meaningful or merely cosmetic?
- Does behavior improve after the mistake?

## 19) Practical Buffett-style evaluation checklist derived from this letter

Use the questions below when analyzing a company or stock.

### A. Intrinsic value and performance
- What is likely intrinsic value per share?
- Are reported metrics understating or overstating economics?
- Is value growing per share, not just in total?
- Is management outperforming the passive alternative?

### B. Business quality
- Does the business have favorable economic characteristics?
- Are they enduring?
- Is the business model sound without heroic assumptions?
- Is the industry structure healthy or incentive-distorted?

### C. Management
- Are managers talented?
- Are they honest?
- Are they disciplined?
- Are they candid about errors and accounting judgments?
- Do they behave like stewards?

### D. Capital allocation
- Does management allocate flexibly across opportunities?
- Will it hold cash rather than force action?
- Does it understand opportunity cost?
- Is scale impairing future returns?
- Is inaction sometimes intelligently chosen?

### E. Balance sheet and risk
- Is leverage appropriate to the business model?
- Are liabilities stable and low-cost?
- Are risks understandable and measurable?
- Could trouble consume capital at the worst possible time?
- Are reserve/provision assumptions believable?

### F. Governance
- Are incentives aligned with owners?
- Do directors have real independence and business savvy?
- Is pay fair relative to performance?
- Is governance real or merely compliant?

### G. Valuation
- Is the business good?
- Is the stock cheap enough?
- How much excellence is already priced in?
- Is there a margin of safety?

## 20) Distilled Buffett framework from the 2003 letter

A Buffett-style investor, based on this letter, should prefer businesses and securities that satisfy most of the following:

1. **Intrinsic value is growing per share**
2. **Management is both able and trustworthy**
3. **Economic characteristics are favorable and durable**
4. **Price is sensible relative to value**
5. **Accounting does not obscure reality**
6. **Risk is understandable and does not explode under stress**
7. **Capital allocation is rational, flexible, and patient**
8. **Governance aligns decision-makers with owners**
9. **The business model is sound at the incentive level**
10. **Returns are judged against the best passive or default alternative**
11. **Leverage is matched to stability, not wishful thinking**
12. **Reported performance is separated from one-off gains and cosmetic growth**
13. **The company can be understood as an evolving economic system, not a static snapshot**
14. **Management avoids doing dumb things when attractive opportunities are scarce**
15. **The investor remains valuation-sensitive even when the business is excellent**

## 21) Reusable one-paragraph synthesis

The 2003 Berkshire letter suggests that Buffett evaluates investments by asking whether a company can grow **per-share intrinsic value** through a combination of **durable business economics**, **honest and talented management**, **disciplined capital allocation**, **sound incentives**, **transparent accounting**, **appropriately structured leverage**, and a **purchase price sensible enough** to leave room for attractive long-term returns. He is especially wary of growth-rate distortions, accounting that front-loads income, risks that cannot be measured, governance that is independent in name only, and business models whose apparent prosperity is driven by misaligned incentives rather than genuine economic strength.

## 23) Most reusable direct framework statements from this letter
