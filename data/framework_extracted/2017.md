# Buffett Reusable Analysis Framework Extraction — Berkshire Hathaway Shareholder Letter (2017)

**Source:** Berkshire Hathaway 2017 shareholder letter (dated February 24, 2018)  
**Extraction goal:** Isolate reusable investing and business-analysis logic that can be applied to evaluating public companies, private businesses, acquisitions, and portfolio decisions in a Buffett-like way.  
**Important:** This is **not** a year summary. It is a distilled framework derived from the letter’s underlying analytical principles.

---

## 1) Core lens: focus on economic reality, not accounting noise

### Principle
Reported earnings can be economically misleading. The analyst should prioritize **normalized per-share earning power** over GAAP figures distorted by unrealized gains/losses, tax one-offs, or other accounting artifacts.

### Reusable logic
- Separate **operating performance** from accounting volatility.
- Treat one-time gains, mark-to-market swings, and realized/unrealized investment noise as potential distortions.
- Ask whether reported earnings describe the business’s **ongoing economics** or merely the accounting period.
- Make per-share economics the unit of analysis, not absolute reported profit.

### Buffett-style evaluation questions
- What is the company’s normalized earning power per share?
- Which reported earnings components are repeatable, and which are incidental or accounting-driven?
- Is management helping owners distinguish operating reality from reporting noise?
- Are investors being misled by headline net income?

### Practical implication
A business can look stronger or weaker than it truly is if the analyst does not normalize earnings.

---

## 2) Primary scorecard: growth in normalized per-share earning power

### Principle
What counts most is not revenue growth, headline EPS, adjusted EBITDA, or reported net income in isolation. The key scorecard is **whether the business is increasing normalized per-share earning power over time**.

### Reusable logic
- Favor metrics tied to owner economics on a per-share basis.
- Judge management by whether they allocate capital in ways that increase long-term per-share earning power.
- Avoid being impressed by growth that comes mainly from leverage, acquisitions at high prices, or accounting conventions.

### Buffett-style evaluation questions
- Has per-share earning power improved over 3, 5, and 10 years?
- Did growth come from genuine business improvement or from financial engineering?
- Is share count helping or hurting the owner’s claim on earnings?

---

## 3) The four legitimate building blocks of value creation

Buffett describes four repeatable ways Berkshire adds value. These can be generalized into a business-analysis framework.

### The four building blocks
1. **Large standalone acquisitions**
2. **Bolt-on acquisitions** that fit existing businesses
3. **Internal sales growth and margin improvement**
4. **Investment income / returns on excess capital**

### Reusable logic
Any company’s long-term value creation can be decomposed into a small number of sources:
- organic growth,
- margin expansion,
- reinvestment returns,
- acquisitions,
- returns on surplus capital.

### Buffett-style evaluation questions
- Which of these value-building engines is actually driving results?
- Which engine is strongest, and which is weakest?
- Is management relying excessively on acquisitions because the core business is mediocre?
- Are investment gains masking weak operating performance?

## 4) Acquisition framework: the checklist Buffett explicitly states

### Acquisition criteria
For a standalone business, Buffett says the desired qualities are:
- **Durable competitive strengths**
- **Able and high-grade management**
- **Good returns on the net tangible assets required to operate the business**
- **Opportunities for internal growth at attractive returns**
- **A sensible purchase price**

### Reusable logic
This is one of the clearest Buffett acquisition filters:
- Quality of the franchise
- Quality of the people
- Capital efficiency
- Reinvestment runway
- Price discipline

### Buffett-style evaluation questions
- Does the business have a durable moat or merely temporary success?
- How much net tangible capital is required to generate current earnings?
- Can the business reinvest incremental capital at good rates?
- Is management both capable and trustworthy?
- Is the price sensible relative to quality and future earning power?

### Practical implication
A good business is not enough. Buffett requires both **quality** and **price discipline**.

---

## 5) Return on tangible capital matters more than abstract profitability

### Principle
Buffett specifically emphasizes **returns on net tangible assets required to operate the business**.

### Reusable logic
A business that appears profitable but requires large tangible capital to sustain operations may be less attractive than one that produces similar earnings with far less capital.

### Buffett-style evaluation questions
- How much incremental tangible capital is needed to produce incremental earnings?
- Are reported returns inflated by goodwill or accounting presentation?
- Does the business require constant reinvestment just to stand still?
- Is the business asset-light in a genuinely durable way?

### Analytical translation
Useful derived metrics:
- Earnings / net tangible operating assets
- Incremental earnings / incremental invested tangible capital
- Maintenance capex burden vs growth capex

---

## 6) Reinvestment runway is part of quality

### Principle
A business is more valuable when it can **internally grow at attractive returns**.

### Reusable logic
A strong current business without reinvestment opportunities may still be good, but a strong business with a long runway is better because retained earnings can compound.

### Buffett-style evaluation questions
- What can management do with retained earnings?
- Can the business reinvest internally at high returns?
- Is the runway long, short, or nearly exhausted?
- Does growth create value, or merely consume capital?

### Practical implication
Do not treat all earnings equally. The value of earnings depends heavily on the rate at which they can be reinvested.

---

## 7) Price discipline is non-negotiable

### Principle
Even decent businesses become unattractive when purchased at excessive prices.

### Reusable logic
- High prices can destroy future returns even when the business is sound.
- Market enthusiasm often weakens discipline.
- Valuation must be tied to future owner returns, not excitement around the asset.

### Buffett-style evaluation questions
- What owner return is implied by the purchase price?
- Am I paying for quality, or overpaying because quality is fashionable?
- What assumptions must be true for this price to work?
- Would the investment still make sense if financed without cheap debt?

---

## 8) In acquisition analysis, ignore leverage-assisted optics

### Principle
Debt financing can make an acquisition appear EPS-accretive even when the underlying economics are mediocre.

### Reusable logic
Buffett evaluates acquisitions on an **all-equity basis** to avoid being fooled by leverage.

### Buffett-style evaluation questions
- Would this deal still make sense if it were funded entirely with equity?
- Is EPS accretion coming from cheap debt rather than business quality?
- Does the transaction improve intrinsic value per share, or only accounting earnings per share?
- Is management using leverage to justify a high purchase price?

### Practical implication
A leveraged deal may look mathematically appealing while being economically weak.

---

## 9) Treat synergy claims with suspicion

### Principle
Buffett does not rely on synergy forecasts and notes that spreadsheets can always be made to justify a deal.

### Reusable logic
- Expected synergies are often a narrative bridge between a bad price and a desired deal.
- The more a deal depends on synergies, the less attractive the stand-alone economics may be.

### Buffett-style evaluation questions
- Does the deal work without synergies?
- Are the synergies concrete, controllable, and historically demonstrated?
- Is management buying growth or buying a story?

---

## 10) In markets full of imprudence, increase your own prudence

### Principle
Buffett’s rule: **the less prudence others show, the more prudence you should show**.

### Reusable logic
- Competitive environments often pressure investors and executives into bad decisions.
- The right response to euphoric pricing is not imitation but stricter selectivity.
- Discipline should be countercyclical.

### Buffett-style evaluation questions
- Is the market environment encouraging careless behavior?
- Am I lowering standards because others are?
- Should I be more conservative precisely because others are not?

---

## 11) Never risk what you need for what you do not need

### Principle
Buffett frames leverage aversion not as a style preference but as a moral and practical rule.

### Reusable logic
- Avoid strategies where a low-probability disaster can permanently impair capital.
- Accept slightly lower returns in exchange for survivability and peace of mind.
- Robustness is part of investment quality.

### Buffett-style evaluation questions
- What could permanently impair capital here?
- Is leverage creating fragility?
- What happens in a severe stress case, not just a base case?
- Am I reaching for incremental return at unacceptable downside risk?

---

## 12) Management quality is both skill and character

### Principle
Buffett repeatedly emphasizes **able and high-grade management** and also shows that sometimes “betting on people” is more certain than betting on assets.

### Reusable logic
Great managers matter most when:
- the business is complex,
- capital allocation matters,
- operations require judgment,
- or incentives could otherwise lead to empire-building.

### Buffett-style evaluation questions
- Is management competent in operations?
- Is management rational in capital allocation?
- Are they shareholder-oriented?
- Do they resist fashionable but foolish actions?
- Would you trust them to run the business if no one were watching?

### Signs Buffett implicitly values
- Candor
- Rationality
- Operational excellence
- Shareholder alignment
- Restraint when opportunities are unattractive

---

## 13) Stay inside the circle of competence

### Principle
Buffett openly admits where he does and does not understand operations.

### Reusable logic
- Analytical humility is an advantage.
- It is acceptable to defer to a proven operator inside a domain where that operator has demonstrated excellence.
- But such deference should rest on real evidence of talent and character.

### Buffett-style evaluation questions
- Do I truly understand this business?
- If not, is there a clearly exceptional operator whose competence is itself a source of confidence?
- Am I stretching beyond my circle of competence because the opportunity seems exciting?

---

## 14) Insurance framework: value the liability structure, not just reported earnings

### Principle
In insurance, the economics depend heavily on **float**, underwriting discipline, and balance-sheet strength.

### Reusable logic
Insurance can be extraordinary when:
- float is durable,
- the cost of float is low or negative,
- underwriting is disciplined,
- and liabilities are structured so the capital base is stable.

### Buffett-style evaluation questions
- How durable is the float or equivalent liability funding?
- What is the cost of that float over time?
- Is underwriting consistently disciplined?
- What catastrophe or reserve risks are embedded in the balance sheet?
- Can liabilities run suddenly, or are they structurally sticky?

### Generalized insight beyond insurance
The same logic applies to any business funded by customer deposits, deferred revenue, prepaid balances, or other liabilities:
- How stable is the funding?
- What does it cost?
- How likely is a run or sudden withdrawal?

---

## 15) The best funding is stable, low-cost, and non-runnable

### Principle
Buffett values p/c float partly because it cannot be withdrawn in a bank-run-like fashion.

### Reusable logic
A funding source is superior when it is:
- low cost,
- durable,
- operationally tied to the business,
- and not vulnerable to sudden liquidity stress.

### Buffett-style evaluation questions
- What portion of the capital structure is fragile under stress?
- Does the company depend on fickle external funding?
- Could a liquidity freeze threaten survival?
- Is the company built to withstand market closure or funding stress?

---

## 16) Build for discontinuities, not normal times

### Principle
Berkshire is intentionally built to withstand extreme economic discontinuities, including market closures and mega-catastrophes.

### Reusable logic
- Financial strength should be evaluated under discontinuity, not just smooth conditions.
- Liquidity and excess strength may look inefficient in calm periods but become decisive in crises.

### Buffett-style evaluation questions
- Could this business survive a severe stress scenario without outside rescue?
- How much liquidity is truly available in a crisis?
- Is the firm reliant on kindness from lenders, capital markets, or counterparties?
- What would happen if markets closed or refinancing disappeared?

---

## 17) Good risk analysis includes severity, not just probability

### Principle
Buffett discusses mega-catastrophe probabilities while also emphasizing that no one knows the exact odds and that exposures grow over time.

### Reusable logic
- Risk is partly unknowable.
- Do not rely on false precision.
- Focus on survivability under severe outcomes.
- Track how exposure changes structurally over time.

### Buffett-style evaluation questions
- What are the severe-but-plausible loss scenarios?
- Even if probability estimates are wrong, can the company survive?
- Is exposure trend increasing because the environment itself is changing?

---

## 18) In some businesses, what counts are brains and capital

### Principle
Buffett says certain businesses lack trade secrets, patents, or locational advantages; what matters there is **brains and capital**.

### Reusable logic
When structural moat is thin, advantage may instead come from:
- superior underwriting or decision-making,
- disciplined culture,
- superior capital backing,
- or better incentives.

### Buffett-style evaluation questions
- Is advantage coming from a product moat, network effect, brand, regulation, or superior decision-making?
- If no moat exists, is management skill enough to sustain superior results?
- Is capital scale itself a strategic advantage?

---

## 19) For operating businesses, compare capex to depreciation carefully

### Principle
Buffett notes that depreciation can either overstate or understate economic reality depending on the business.

### Reusable logic
- Accounting depreciation is not automatically equal to economic maintenance capex.
- Some amortization may not reflect real recurring cost.
- Some depreciation may materially understate true asset upkeep requirements.

### Buffett-style evaluation questions
- What is true maintenance capex?
- Is depreciation overstating or understating economic wear and tear?
- How much capital must be reinvested just to preserve current earning power?
- Are free-cash-flow metrics realistic or cosmetically inflated?

---

## 20) Idle liquidity has opportunity cost, but strategic liquidity has option value

### Principle
Buffett dislikes excessive idle cash because it earns little, yet he values extraordinary liquidity for resilience and future opportunity.

### Reusable logic
Cash should be assessed in two ways:
- **drag on returns** during calm periods,
- **optionality and protection** during stress or when opportunities appear.

### Buffett-style evaluation questions
- Is cash excessive relative to operating need and risk?
- Is management hoarding cash without a rational purpose?
- Does liquidity create a genuine option to act decisively in crises?

---

## 21) View stocks as partial business ownership, not trading symbols

### Principle
Buffett treats marketable common stocks as **interests in businesses**, not as chart objects or media narratives.

### Reusable logic
- Security analysis should begin with business analysis.
- Market quotations are secondary to the operating success of the underlying enterprise.
- A minority position should be thought of as fractional ownership in a business.

### Buffett-style evaluation questions
- If the market closed for years, would I still want to own this business?
- What is the business economics of the investee?
- Is my thesis based on business performance or on expected price movement?

---

## 22) Dividends alone understate owner economics

### Principle
Reported dividend income from investments can materially understate true economic earnings when good businesses retain earnings productively.

### Reusable logic
- Evaluate both **distributed** and **retained** earnings.
- Retained earnings matter if they are reinvested at good rates or used for intelligent repurchases.
- Look through accounting receipt to underlying business value creation.

### Buffett-style evaluation questions
- How much earnings power is being retained on my behalf?
- Is retained capital being reinvested productively?
- Do capital gains over time plausibly reflect retained earnings compounded inside the business?

---

## 23) Over time, retained earnings should show up in value

### Principle
Buffett expects undistributed earnings, viewed across a group of successful investees, to translate into commensurate capital gains over time.

### Reusable logic
A business can create tremendous owner value without paying out most of its profits, provided retained earnings are deployed intelligently.

### Buffett-style evaluation questions
- What has management done historically with retained earnings?
- Has intrinsic value per share grown in line with retained capital?
- Are repurchases value-creating or value-destructive?

---

## 24) Short-term market prices are noisy; long-term prices weigh business results

### Principle
In the short run, markets vote; in the long run, they weigh.

### Reusable logic
- Price and value can diverge for long stretches.
- Short-term volatility does not disprove long-term compounding.
- The analyst must tolerate temporary mispricing.

### Buffett-style evaluation questions
- Is the market currently voting emotionally rather than weighing business results?
- What business progress is being obscured by price volatility?
- Am I letting price action override business facts?

---

## 25) Reinvestment plus time is a major value engine

### Principle
Long-term value builds when earnings are reinvested and compound interest is allowed to work.

### Reusable logic
- Compounding is a function of return on retained capital and duration.
- The analyst should focus on whether the business can continue compounding at satisfactory rates.

### Buffett-style evaluation questions
- What is the expected rate of compounding from retained earnings?
- How durable is that rate?
- What might interrupt the compounding process?

---

## 26) Avoid borrowed money in equities

### Principle
Buffett uses Berkshire’s own historical drawdowns to argue strongly against leverage in stock ownership.

### Reusable logic
- Even superior businesses and portfolios can suffer deep temporary price declines.
- Leverage turns volatility into forced decision-making and potential permanent loss.

### Buffett-style evaluation questions
- Could a temporary market decline force liquidation?
- Would leverage impair my judgment during panic?
- Is this structure robust enough to survive a 50% price drawdown?

---

## 27) Market declines are opportunities for the unleveraged and emotionally steady

### Principle
Major declines offer extraordinary opportunity to those not handicapped by debt.

### Reusable logic
- Crises reward liquidity, patience, and emotional stability.
- Opportunity quality often improves when sentiment collapses.

### Buffett-style evaluation questions
- If prices fell sharply, would I be a forced seller or an opportunistic buyer?
- Do I have liquidity and temperament for panic environments?
- Which watchlist businesses would become compelling at lower prices?

---

## 28) Investment risk should be defined as failure to meet the objective

### Principle
Buffett defines risk not as price volatility alone but as the possibility that postponed consumption fails to become greater future consumption.

### Reusable logic
- True risk is objective-relative.
- Assets that appear stable can be risky if they guarantee poor real returns.
- Long-duration purchasing-power erosion is genuine risk.

### Buffett-style evaluation questions
- What is my actual objective: nominal stability or real wealth growth?
- Does this asset preserve purchasing power after inflation and tax?
- Is apparent safety hiding long-term economic risk?

---

## 29) Long-term bonds can be riskier than equities

### Principle
Buffett argues that so-called risk-free long-term bonds may be riskier than diversified equities over long horizons when bought at poor yields.

### Reusable logic
- Low nominal volatility does not equal low long-term economic risk.
- Duration risk plus inflation risk can make bonds poor long-term stores of purchasing power.
- Relative valuation matters: stock attractiveness depends partly on earnings yield versus interest rates.

### Buffett-style evaluation questions
- What real return is locked in by current bond yields?
- Are equities available at sensible multiples relative to interest rates?
- Over the relevant time horizon, which asset better protects and compounds purchasing power?

---

## 30) Risk declines with horizon for sensible equity ownership

### Principle
Buffett argues that for long-term investors, diversified equities bought at sensible valuations become less risky than bonds as the horizon lengthens.

### Reusable logic
- Time horizon changes the meaning of risk.
- Volatility matters most to short-horizon or leveraged investors.
- Valuation discipline remains essential.

### Buffett-style evaluation questions
- What is the investor’s actual horizon?
- Is the equity portfolio diversified enough?
- Were the equities bought at a sensible multiple relative to prevailing rates?

---

## 31) High-grade bonds can increase portfolio risk

### Principle
Buffett rejects the simplistic idea that more bonds automatically means less risk for long-term investors.

### Reusable logic
Asset allocation should not substitute for real risk analysis. Sometimes bonds reduce mark-to-market fluctuation while increasing long-run failure risk.

### Buffett-style evaluation questions
- Is this portfolio optimized for optics or for long-term outcomes?
- Are fixed-income allocations preserving capital or locking in weak real returns?

---

## 32) Costs matter enormously; fees are a persistent drag

### Principle
The hedge fund bet reinforces Buffett’s long-running point that fees compound against investors and often overwhelm value added.

### Reusable logic
- High-cost intermediation demands a very high performance hurdle.
- Even skilled managers can leave clients with poor net returns after fees.
- Complexity often enriches intermediaries more reliably than owners.

### Buffett-style evaluation questions
- What is the full fee load, direct and indirect?
- How much gross alpha is required just to match a low-cost alternative?
- Who is most likely to get rich here: the investor or the manager?

---

## 33) Markets are usually rational, but occasionally crazy

### Principle
Exceptional opportunities arise when markets behave irrationally.

### Reusable logic
- Rare valuation mismatches can be obvious enough that brilliance is unnecessary.
- Success during such moments requires temperament and simplicity more than complexity.

### Buffett-style evaluation questions
- Is this an unusual valuation mismatch or just a normal fluctuation?
- What simple fundamentals contradict current market pricing?
- Can I act independently of crowd emotion?

---

## 34) Big, easy decisions are often better than constant activity

### Principle
Buffett favors a few large, understandable decisions over thousands of marginal ones.

### Reusable logic
- Decision quality often beats decision quantity.
- Frequent action can create cost, noise, and overconfidence.
- A clearly superior alternative should not require elaborate sophistication.

### Buffett-style evaluation questions
- Is this one of the rare obvious decisions?
- Am I acting because the decision is good or because activity feels productive?
- Can the thesis be explained simply and clearly?

---

## 35) Simplicity is a feature, not a weakness

### Principle
Buffett emphasizes that major successful decisions may come from “kindergarten-like” analysis rather than complex models.

### Reusable logic
A thesis that depends on fragile complexity may be inferior to one grounded in a few durable facts:
- clear relative valuation,
- solid business quality,
- long runway,
- strong balance sheet.

### Buffett-style evaluation questions
- What are the few essential facts driving the decision?
- Does the thesis still hold if I remove complex assumptions?
- Is the simplicity revealing strength or hiding ignorance?

---

## 36) A sensible multiple relative to interest rates still matters

### Principle
Buffett explicitly conditions the attractiveness of equities on their being purchased at a **sensible multiple of earnings relative to prevailing interest rates**.

### Reusable logic
- Valuation is not absolute; it is partly relative to opportunity cost.
- Low interest rates can justify somewhat higher equity multiples, but not unlimited ones.
- The key is still future owner return.

### Buffett-style evaluation questions
- What earnings yield am I buying?
- How does that compare with bond yields and inflation?
- Is the spread sufficient given business quality and risk?

---

## 37) Share repurchases create value only if done intelligently

### Principle
Buffett notes that retained earnings can raise EPS through expansion or repurchases.

### Reusable logic
Repurchases are not inherently good. They are beneficial when:
- shares are repurchased below intrinsic value,
- the business does not sacrifice better reinvestment options,
- and balance-sheet strength is preserved.

### Buffett-style evaluation questions
- Is management repurchasing shares because they are cheap or because it is fashionable?
- Would retained cash earn better returns elsewhere?
- Is the buyback enhancing per-share intrinsic value?

---

## 38) Shareholder communication is part of governance quality

### Principle
Buffett tries to communicate in a way that gives all shareholders time and equal access to information.

### Reusable logic
- Honest communication is an analytical clue about management quality.
- Management that explains distortions, timing, and real economics may be more trustworthy.
- Equal treatment of large and small shareholders signals owner orientation.

### Buffett-style evaluation questions
- Is management candid about what matters and what does not?
- Do they explain accounting distortions clearly?
- Do they appear to favor select institutions over ordinary owners?

---

## 39) Owner alignment means sharing downside, not just upside

### Principle
Buffett highlights that Berkshire managers and directors who own stock got it through open-market purchases or business sales, and bear downside as well as upside.

### Reusable logic
Alignment is strongest when insiders actually risk capital, not merely receive option-like rewards.

### Buffett-style evaluation questions
- Do insiders have real skin in the game?
- Is their ownership exposed to downside?
- Are incentives encouraging durable value creation or short-term optics?

---

## 40) A compact Buffett-style analysis template derived from this letter

Use the following checklist when analyzing any company or stock.

### A. Economic reality
- What is normalized per-share earning power?
- What accounting items distort the picture?
- What is recurring vs one-time?

### B. Business quality
- Does the company have durable competitive strength?
- Are returns on required tangible capital attractive?
- Is there a long runway for reinvestment at good returns?

### C. Management quality
- Are managers able, candid, disciplined, and shareholder-oriented?
- Do they allocate capital rationally?
- Are they resisting leverage, empire-building, and story-driven deals?

### D. Capital allocation
- Is value creation coming from organic growth, acquisitions, margins, or investment returns?
- Are retained earnings producing higher intrinsic value per share?
- Are repurchases intelligent?

### E. Balance-sheet resilience
- Could the company survive discontinuity, liquidity stress, or severe industry loss?
- Is funding stable and non-runnable?
- Is leverage creating hidden fragility?

### F. Valuation
- Is the purchase price sensible?
- What owner return is implied?
- Does the thesis still work on an all-equity basis?
- Does it work without synergies?

### G. Investor behavior
- Am I thinking like an owner of a business or like a trader of a symbol?
- Am I relying on market mood, expert activity, or fee-heavy complexity?
- Is this a big, easy decision or unnecessary motion?

11. Think of stocks as businesses.

14. Market prices can be irrational for long stretches; business value matters more.

## 43) Bottom line extraction

The deepest reusable lesson from the 2017 letter is that Buffett evaluates businesses and investments through an integrated lens:

- **economic reality over accounting presentation**,
- **per-share compounding over headline growth**,
- **quality plus price discipline over excitement**,
- **balance-sheet resilience over leverage-assisted returns**,
- **retained-earnings compounding over short-term market fluctuations**,
- and **simple, high-quality decisions over complex, fee-laden activity**.

That combination is the repeatable framework.
