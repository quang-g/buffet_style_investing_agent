# Berkshire Hathaway 1987 Letter — Reusable Buffett-Style Investment / Business Analysis Framework

Source letter: Berkshire Hathaway Chairman's Letter (1987) fileciteturn0file0

## Purpose of this extraction

This document extracts **reusable stock/business analysis logic** from Warren Buffett’s 1987 Berkshire Hathaway shareholder letter. It excludes year-specific narration except where a concrete example reveals a general investing principle.

---

## 1) Core valuation logic

### 1.1 Focus on business value, not accounting book value

**Framework principle**
- The investor should judge performance by the change in **per-share business value / intrinsic value**, not by book value alone.
- Book value can be a poor proxy for economic value. Sometimes it understates franchise value; sometimes it overstates reality.
- Book value is useful only when it roughly tracks business value. It must never be treated as truth by default.

**Reusable evaluation questions**
- Is book value a reasonable proxy for intrinsic value in this business, or are they materially disconnected?
- What assets or economics make book value understate value (brand, franchise, pricing power, low capital intensity, intangible strength)?
- What accounting values may overstate economic value (distressed assets, weak earning power, inflated asset carrying values)?
- Is per-share intrinsic value compounding faster or slower than book value?

**Performance logic**
- Rising book value is not enough. The real test is whether underlying earning power and intrinsic value per share are increasing.

---

### 1.2 Evaluate returns relative to the capital actually required

**Framework principle**
- Earnings alone say little. Economic quality is revealed by the amount of **capital required** to produce those earnings.
- Buffett emphasizes returns on **equity capital employed** and, more broadly, returns on the underlying assets needed to run the business.
- Great businesses often produce high earnings while requiring little incremental capital.

**Reusable evaluation questions**
- How much equity capital is actually needed to produce current earnings?
- What is the after-tax earning power relative to tangible equity employed?
- Is leverage creating the return, or is the business itself inherently productive?
- Would returns still look exceptional with nominal leverage?

**Performance logic**
- A high return generated with little debt and modest capital needs signals business strength.
- A business that requires little capital can send excess cash to owners or redeploy it into new opportunities.

**Practical metric ideas**
- Return on equity
- Return on incremental capital
- Pre-tax earnings / equity employed
- Debt dependence versus operating earning power

---

### 1.3 Separate manager performance from acquisition price

**Framework principle**
- A manager should be judged on the return earned on the **underlying operating assets**, not on the price a parent company paid to acquire the business.
- Purchase price matters greatly for the investor’s return, but it does not change the capital the operator must manage.

**Reusable evaluation questions**
- Is weak reported return due to mediocre operations, or due to an expensive acquisition price?
- What is the operating return on the business’s real asset base independent of purchase accounting?
- Am I evaluating management quality separately from investment-entry quality?

**Performance logic**
- Two judgments must be kept distinct:
  1. Is this an excellent business run well?
  2. Did I pay a sensible price for it?

---

## 2) Business quality: what makes a company exceptional

### 2.1 Favor businesses with durable, fortress-like franchises

**Framework principle**
- Sustained high returns usually come from a **strong franchise** that competitors struggle to erode.
- Violent change makes it hard to build a fortress-like position.
- The best businesses are often not glamorous; they are frequently mundane businesses with durable advantages.

**Reusable evaluation questions**
- Does this business have a franchise that resists competition?
- Can competitors easily copy the product, underprice it, or take share?
- Is the customer relationship habitual, local, trusted, or otherwise sticky?
- Is industry change so rapid that any advantage is fragile?
- Can the business still look recognizable 5–10 years from now?

**Performance logic**
- Stability plus a strong franchise is often a better recipe for superior economics than excitement plus disruption.

---

### 2.2 Prefer simplicity and understandability

**Framework principle**
- Buffett wants businesses that are simple enough to understand and evaluate.
- Complexity, especially technological or structural complexity, increases the odds of error.

**Reusable evaluation questions**
- Can I explain how this company makes money in plain language?
- What variables truly drive value here?
- Is the business understandable enough that I can estimate future economics with reasonable confidence?
- Is complexity disguising weak economics?

**Performance logic**
- Good investing requires understanding. If the business model is opaque, valuation confidence is low and mistakes become more likely.

---

### 2.3 Good businesses usually do ordinary things exceptionally well

**Framework principle**
- Exceptional economics often come not from novelty but from disciplined execution in a sound business.
- Strong businesses improve service, product line, distribution, and operations, but they do not abandon their core strengths for fashion.

**Reusable evaluation questions**
- Is management building on existing strengths or chasing distractions?
- Are gains driven by better execution rather than promotional storytelling?
- Does the company protect and deepen its winning formula?

**Performance logic**
- Long-term winners often avoid strategic drift. They improve the business they already know how to run.

---

### 2.4 Customer value is part of franchise strength

**Framework principle**
- Businesses that offer clear customer value, honesty, relevance, freshness, or service can build unusually durable franchises.
- Customer affection can be an economic asset.

**Reusable evaluation questions**
- Why do customers prefer this business beyond price alone?
- Does the company behave in a way that earns trust and repeat business?
- Is the product genuinely indispensable or simply available?
- Does management think first about customer value and only then about accounting optics?

**Performance logic**
- A business that is loved, trusted, or indispensable often enjoys pricing power, repeat demand, and resilience.

---

### 2.5 Great businesses often require little debt

**Framework principle**
- Really good businesses usually do not need much borrowing.
- If a company requires heavy leverage to produce attractive returns, its economics may be weaker than they appear.

**Reusable evaluation questions**
- How much debt is necessary for the company to generate its returns?
- Are returns robust without leverage?
- Is the balance sheet conservative because the business is strong, or merely because management is cautious?

**Performance logic**
- True business quality reveals itself in strong returns with modest leverage.

---

## 3) Management assessment framework

### 3.1 Management quality is a primary investment variable

**Framework principle**
- Buffett repeatedly evaluates businesses through the lens of the people running them.
- Desired traits include: talent, energy, integrity, rationality, cost discipline, customer orientation, focus, and character.

**Reusable evaluation questions**
- Are the managers both competent and honest?
- Do they understand the details of the business deeply?
- Do they protect the franchise rather than exploit it?
- Do they allocate costs and capital rationally?
- Do they enjoy the business and act like owners?

**Performance logic**
- A strong business can be damaged by weak managers; a good manager can widen the gap between a strong franchise and weaker peers.

---

### 3.2 Measure managers by operating excellence, not bureaucracy

**Framework principle**
- Buffett admires managers who deliver results without dependence on corporate ritual, centralized control, or administrative theater.
- He values autonomy when matched with exceptional operating judgment.

**Reusable evaluation questions**
- Does performance come from real operating excellence or from system-heavy bureaucracy?
- Are managers self-directed and owner-minded?
- Would more interference from headquarters improve results, or damage them?

**Performance logic**
- Great operators often need trust, not supervision.

---

### 3.3 Look for managers who stay within their strengths and do not get diverted

**Framework principle**
- Buffett praises managers who protect franchises, control costs, search for adjacent growth, and do not wander into unrelated ventures.

**Reusable evaluation questions**
- Is management expanding from strengths or diversifying from insecurity?
- Do new products and markets reinforce the existing franchise?
- Are there signs of empire-building or fashionable distraction?

**Performance logic**
- Incremental growth built on strengths is safer and usually more valuable than unfocused expansion.

---

### 3.4 High-grade people matter even in non-controlled holdings

**Framework principle**
- Buffett wants not just good businesses, but businesses run by high-grade, talented, likeable, trustworthy people.
- He explicitly values the quality of long-term association.

**Reusable evaluation questions**
- Would I want to be partnered with these people for many years?
- Do I trust them in situations where short-term incentives tempt bad behavior?
- Have they demonstrated service-above-self behavior?

**Performance logic**
- Character matters economically. It shapes capital allocation, disclosure quality, treatment of stakeholders, and long-term trust.

---

## 4) Capital intensity, cash generation, and reinvestment logic

### 4.1 The best businesses can grow while releasing cash

**Framework principle**
- An exceptional business can both grow and make most of its earnings available for redeployment.
- Low capital intensity magnifies owner value because retained earnings are not trapped in maintenance needs.

**Reusable evaluation questions**
- How much of earnings must be reinvested just to maintain competitive position?
- Can the business grow without proportionate capital additions?
- Does growth consume cash or release cash?
- What portion of earnings is truly available for owners or redeployment?

**Performance logic**
- A business with high returns and low reinvestment needs creates a powerful compounding engine.

---

### 4.2 Distinguish reported earnings from economic earnings

**Framework principle**
- Buffett repeatedly adjusts mentally for accounting conventions that obscure economic reality.
- Purchase accounting, goodwill amortization, dividend-only recognition for minority holdings, and market-value balance-sheet marks can all distort economic understanding.

**Reusable evaluation questions**
- What accounting treatments are masking the true economics?
- What are the normalized earnings of the business absent acquisition accounting noise?
- Am I focusing on reported earnings because they are available, or on economic earning power because it matters?

**Performance logic**
- The serious investor must build an independent view of earning power rather than inherit GAAP at face value.

---

### 4.3 Capital allocation is one of the most important management tests

**Framework principle**
- CEOs often rise through operating excellence but are later required to allocate capital, a different skill entirely.
- Poor capital allocation can destroy value even inside a good business.

**Reusable evaluation questions**
- Is management skilled at allocating retained earnings?
- How have past retained dollars translated into per-share value?
- Does management reinvest where returns are high, repurchase intelligently, acquire rationally, or waste capital?
- Are advisors, bankers, or consultants being used as substitutes for managerial judgment?

**Performance logic**
- Over time, retained earnings become a large share of total capital employed. Capital allocation therefore becomes central to long-run shareholder outcomes.

---

## 5) Industry economics framework

### 5.1 Start with industry structure, not just company narrative

**Framework principle**
- Some industries are structurally unattractive regardless of management quality.
- Buffett highlights commodity-like industries as especially difficult: many competitors, ease of entry, no meaningful product differentiation.

**Reusable evaluation questions**
- How many competitors are there?
- How easy is new entry?
- Is the product truly differentiated in ways customers value?
- Does the industry normally support excess returns, or only during temporary shortage/supply imbalances?

**Performance logic**
- In weak industry structures, only the low-cost producer or a protected niche operator can sustain strong profitability.

---

### 5.2 In commodity businesses, conditions can look good temporarily but not durably

**Framework principle**
- Commodity businesses can enjoy excellent periods when supply is tight, but such prosperity often invites expansion that destroys future returns.
- Temporary shortage conditions should not be confused with durable franchise economics.

**Reusable evaluation questions**
- Are current high returns due to structural advantage or temporary scarcity?
- Will strong current profitability attract new capacity?
- Are industry participants behaving rationally, or are they likely to over-expand?

**Performance logic**
- Temporary tailwinds can produce misleading numbers. The investor must separate cyclical good fortune from durable advantage.

---

### 5.3 Use industry-specific metrics, but interpret them economically

**Framework principle**
- Buffett uses insurance combined ratios, float dynamics, premium growth, reserve behavior, and tax effects as economic rather than purely accounting tools.
- Domain metrics matter only insofar as they explain future economics.

**Reusable evaluation questions**
- What are the critical operating metrics that actually determine value in this industry?
- What threshold separates good economics from bad economics?
- Are reported metrics stable, lagged, distorted, or easy to manipulate?

**Performance logic**
- Industry metrics are valuable only when linked to real earning power and risk.

---

## 6) Insurance-specific framework extracted from the letter

### 6.1 Insurance must be judged as a combined underwriting-plus-investment business

**Framework principle**
- Underwriting results and investment income from float must be assessed together.
- A combined ratio modestly above 100 can still produce acceptable overall economics if float is large and low-cost.

**Reusable evaluation questions**
- Is underwriting profitable or at least acceptably priced relative to float economics?
- What is the cost of float?
- Is float growing, stable, or shrinking?
- Is the insurer earning money because underwriting is sound, because float is cheap, or because accounting is flattering results?

**Performance logic**
- Insurance value is driven by the quality, cost, scale, and durability of float combined with underwriting discipline.

---

### 6.2 Reserve-driven earnings demand skepticism

**Framework principle**
- Casualty insurance earnings can be highly unreliable because reserves depend on uncertain future claims, especially in long-tail lines.
- Reported earnings in such businesses should be viewed with suspicion, including one’s own.

**Reusable evaluation questions**
- How much of current earnings depend on reserve estimates?
- Is the company writing long-tail business where error bands are large?
- Has reserve development historically validated management’s estimates?
- Are auditors and investors treating uncertain figures with unjustified confidence?

**Performance logic**
- When earnings can be changed materially by assumptions, analytical skepticism must rise.

---

### 6.3 In unattractive industries, differentiate through structural advantage or disciplined behavior

**Framework principle**
- Berkshire attempts to escape commodity economics in insurance by differentiating through:
  1. extraordinary financial strength, and
  2. indifference to volume when pricing is unattractive.

**Reusable evaluation questions**
- What truly differentiates this insurer from commodity competitors?
- Does the firm have a balance sheet advantage customers value in stress periods?
- Can management reduce volume without damaging long-term franchise position?
- Is market share being pursued irrationally?

**Performance logic**
- The willingness to walk away from underpriced business is itself a competitive advantage.

---

### 6.4 Price based on exposure, not competition

**Framework principle**
- Insurance should be priced according to risk exposure and expected economics, not according to competitor prices.

**Reusable evaluation questions**
- Is pricing driven by exposure and expected loss economics, or by market-share motives?
- Will the business shrink volume when prices are inadequate?
- Is current growth evidence of discipline or evidence of recklessness?

**Performance logic**
- In insurance, refusing underpriced business protects long-term value even when it hurts short-term volume.

---

## 7) Public-equity investing framework

### 7.1 Treat stock purchases as purchases of businesses

**Framework principle**
- When buying common stocks, Buffett approaches the decision as if buying into a private business.
- The key variables are:
  1. economic prospects,
  2. people in charge,
  3. price paid.

**Reusable evaluation questions**
- If this stock were a private business offered to me, would I want to own it?
- What are the business’s long-term economics?
- Who is running it, and do I trust them?
- Is the current price sensible relative to intrinsic value?

**Performance logic**
- The investor’s economic fate follows the business’s fate, whether ownership is total or partial.

---

### 7.2 Market quotations are an opportunity set, not instruction

**Framework principle**
- Mr. Market exists to serve, not to guide.
- Price volatility is useful when it offers bargains; it is dangerous when it influences judgment.

**Reusable evaluation questions**
- Do I understand the business well enough to judge value independently of the market?
- Am I reacting to price movement, or to business value?
- Is today’s market quote offering me an opportunity or trying to manipulate my mood?

**Performance logic**
- A volatile market benefits the investor who is psychologically independent and valuation-driven.

---

### 7.3 Insulate thought from crowd emotion

**Framework principle**
- Investment success depends on combining sound business judgment with emotional insulation from market contagion.
- Buffett explicitly rejects reliance on formulae, market signals, and price behavior as primary guides.

**Reusable evaluation questions**
- What is my estimate of intrinsic value independent of price action?
- Am I feeling pressure because others are euphoric or fearful?
- Would I still make this decision if quotes were unavailable for years?

**Performance logic**
- Independent judgment plus emotional stability is a core edge.

---

### 7.4 Let operating results, not quotations, tell you whether you were right

**Framework principle**
- The success of an investment should be judged by business performance over time, not by daily or yearly stock movements.
- Market recognition may be delayed, and that delay can be beneficial if it allows additional purchases at good prices.

**Reusable evaluation questions**
- Is the business compounding intrinsic value satisfactorily?
- Has market disappointment created a better reinvestment opportunity?
- Am I demanding quick recognition instead of correct analysis?

**Performance logic**
- In the long run, business performance drives value realization even if the market is slow to notice.

---

### 7.5 Sell for valuation, better opportunity, or changed understanding — not merely because of gain

**Framework principle**
- Buffett does not sell simply because a stock has risen or because he has held it for a long time.
- Valid reasons to sell include:
  - clear overvaluation,
  - a more attractive opportunity,
  - or recognition that the business is less understandable or less attractive than first believed.

**Reusable evaluation questions**
- Is the market price now materially above intrinsic value?
- Do I need capital for a clearly better-understood or more undervalued investment?
- Has my assessment of business quality or management changed?
- Am I tempted to sell just because I have a profit?

**Performance logic**
- “Taking a profit” is not, by itself, an intelligent investment reason.

---

### 7.6 Prefer outstanding businesses at sensible prices over mediocre businesses at bargain prices

**Framework principle**
- Buffett explicitly states the preference for buying an outstanding business at a sensible price rather than a mediocre business at a bargain price.
- Cheapness alone is not enough; quality matters.

**Reusable evaluation questions**
- Is this a genuinely outstanding business, or merely statistically cheap?
- If the business is mediocre, what prevents mean reversion from failing to occur?
- Am I buying strength at a fair price or weakness at a tempting price?

**Performance logic**
- Quality compounds. Mediocrity often consumes time, attention, and capital.

---

### 7.7 Stay within areas you understand

**Framework principle**
- Buffett compares categories continuously for the highest after-tax expected return, but only among alternatives he thinks he understands.

**Reusable evaluation questions**
- Do I understand this asset class or security well enough to estimate expected value?
- Is my perceived edge real, or is it borrowed confidence?
- Am I choosing among understandable alternatives, or reaching beyond competence because opportunity seems exciting?

**Performance logic**
- Opportunity set breadth matters less than disciplined selectivity inside one’s competence.

---

## 8) Portfolio construction and opportunity selection

### 8.1 Compare all capital uses on an after-tax expected-value basis

**Framework principle**
- Buffett compares common stocks, bonds, cash equivalents, arbitrage, and special situations by their highest **after-tax return as measured by mathematical expectation**.
- He does not optimize for accounting appearance or immediate reported earnings.

**Reusable evaluation questions**
- Among available uses of capital, which offers the highest after-tax expected value?
- What is the probability-weighted outcome distribution?
- Am I ignoring taxes, optionality, or downside asymmetry?
- Am I choosing an asset because it looks good in reported earnings rather than because it maximizes net worth?

**Performance logic**
- Rational capital allocation compares opportunities across categories, not within silos.

---

### 8.2 Keep liquidity and flexibility for rare great opportunities

**Framework principle**
- Cash or low-commitment holdings can be worthwhile when they preserve the ability to act decisively on rare major opportunities.
- Buffett is willing to accept short-term earnings drag to remain prepared.

**Reusable evaluation questions**
- What optionality does current liquidity provide?
- Am I overreaching for current yield and sacrificing future flexibility?
- Is this parking place temporary and rational, or lazy capital allocation?

**Performance logic**
- Preparedness has option value. The ability to act when bargains appear can outweigh interim carry costs.

---

### 8.3 Arbitrage is acceptable only when structured, understandable, and sized prudently

**Framework principle**
- Buffett participates selectively in publicly announced arbitrage situations, avoids speculative “in play” dynamics, and recognizes that a few bad outcomes can erase a strong record.

**Reusable evaluation questions**
- Is the event clearly announced and understandable?
- What breaks the deal?
- What is the downside on failure versus upside on success?
- Is position size consistent with the tail-risk profile?

**Performance logic**
- Event-driven investing can be attractive when probabilities, spreads, and failure scenarios are understood conservatively.

---

### 8.4 Security structure should match business predictability

**Framework principle**
- When industry economics are less predictable, Buffett may prefer a structure such as convertible preferred rather than straight common stock.
- Instrument choice can be a way to shape risk-reward when the business is acceptable but uncertain.

**Reusable evaluation questions**
- Is the common equity the best expression of the opportunity?
- Would preferred, convertible, or bond-like structures better protect downside while preserving upside?
- Is uncertainty about industry economics high enough to justify a more defensive security form?

**Performance logic**
- Security selection is part of investment analysis, not an afterthought.

---

## 9) Accounting, reporting, and analytical skepticism

### 9.1 Ignore accounting schizophrenia; focus on future earning power

**Framework principle**
- GAAP can reflect current values without current earnings in one case, and current earnings without current values in another.
- The investor should form an independent estimate of business value based on future earning power.

**Reusable evaluation questions**
- What does accounting recognize too early, too late, or not at all?
- What would owner economics look like if I ignored the accounting presentation?
- Which accounting number is likely to mislead less-sophisticated investors?

**Performance logic**
- Independent valuation requires stepping outside reported figures when they distort economic reality.

---

### 9.2 Be especially skeptical where earnings can be manufactured by assumption

**Framework principle**
- Where accounting depends heavily on judgment, the risk of innocent error and deliberate manipulation rises.

**Reusable evaluation questions**
- Which earnings components are assumption-heavy?
- How easy would it be for management to flatter results without obvious rule-breaking?
- Is the business one where “earnings” can be created by a stroke of the pen?

**Performance logic**
- Analytical skepticism should scale up in proportion to estimate intensity and opacity.

---

## 10) Debt, balance-sheet policy, and risk framework

### 10.1 Never risk what is important for returns that are relatively unimportant

**Framework principle**
- Buffett rejects leverage that could threaten survival, reputation, policyholders, employees, or permanent capital.
- He prefers acceptable long-term results under severe adversity over optimized results under normal conditions.

**Reusable evaluation questions**
- Under a truly adverse scenario, could this balance sheet still survive comfortably?
- Is leverage being used to enhance already-good economics or to compensate for weak economics?
- What important things are being risked for incremental return?

**Performance logic**
- Resilience matters more than maximizing near-term ROE.

---

### 10.2 Borrow only at levels that are safe on a worst-case basis

**Framework principle**
- Debt is acceptable when, under conservative assumptions, it does not threaten the enterprise.
- Safety analysis should consider business diversity, capital intensity, liquidity, and debt structure.

**Reusable evaluation questions**
- What does the downside case look like, not the base case?
- Are earnings diversified and entrenched?
- Are major liquid assets available?
- Is debt maturity structured sensibly?

**Performance logic**
- Debt policy should be judged by survival capability in bad environments, not elegance in spreadsheets.

---

### 10.3 Finance in anticipation of need, not only in reaction to it

**Framework principle**
- Intelligent liability management may require raising funds before opportunities appear.
- Good acquisition opportunities often emerge when money is tight, so preparedness matters.

**Reusable evaluation questions**
- Is the company raising capital opportunistically while terms are reasonable?
- Will it be able to act when rare attractive assets become available?
- Is management stretching for yield because carry cost is psychologically irritating?

**Performance logic**
- Funding readiness is strategic optionality.

---

## 11) Acquisition framework explicitly stated in the letter

Buffett provides a direct acquisition checklist that is highly reusable as a stock/business filter.

### 11.1 Buffett-style acquisition / investment filter

**Target characteristics**
1. Large earnings base
2. Demonstrated consistent earning power
3. Good returns on equity with little or no debt
4. Management already in place
5. Simple business
6. Seller provides a price

**Expanded reusable screening questions**
- Does the business already earn meaningful after-tax profits?
- Is earning power demonstrated, not merely projected?
- Is the opportunity a true operating business rather than a turnaround hope story?
- Does it earn good returns on equity without financial engineering?
- Is the management team already competent and in place?
- Is the business simple enough to understand?
- Is price clear enough for rational decision-making?

**Embedded performance logic**
- Buffett is screening for businesses that are already economically good, understandable, and operable without needing rescue.

## 12) Reusable mental models from the letter

### 12.1 Intrinsic value over accounting convention
- The map is not the territory; GAAP is not business value.

### 12.2 Return on capital reveals economic quality
- Earnings must always be tied to the capital required to produce them.

### 12.3 Stability is often a feature, not a bug
- Violent change is enemy territory for fortress franchises.

### 12.4 Market as servant, not teacher
- Quotes are offers, not instructions.

### 12.5 Temperament is an investing edge
- Emotional independence is as important as analytical ability.

### 12.6 Industry structure sets the outer bound of returns
- Great managers cannot repeal commodity economics.

### 12.7 Capital allocation is management’s hidden report card
- Retained earnings reveal whether CEOs can think like owners.

### 12.8 Optionality has value
- Liquidity and pre-arranged financing enable action when rare bargains appear.

### 12.9 Risk is survival-based, not volatility-based
- The real question is not price fluctuation but permanent impairment or institutional endangerment.

### 12.10 Quality plus price beats cheapness alone
- The ideal is outstanding economics at a sensible price.

## 13) Buffett-style analysis checklist derived from this letter

### Business economics
- What is the gap between book value and intrinsic value?
- What is the return on equity / capital employed?
- How much capital is needed to sustain and grow earnings?
- How much debt is required to make returns look good?
- Does the business have a fortress-like franchise?
- Is the business simple and understandable?
- Is the industry stable enough to support durable economics?

### Management
- Are management competent, honest, and rational?
- Do they protect the franchise and control costs?
- Do they stay focused on strengths?
- Are they customer-oriented in a way that strengthens economics?
- Are they strong capital allocators?

### Industry structure
- Is the business in a commodity-like industry?
- How easy is entry?
- What truly differentiates the company?
- Are good current returns cyclical, shortage-driven, or durable?

### Financial quality
- Are reported earnings trustworthy?
- What accounting adjustments are obscuring economics?
- How much of earnings is assumption-based?
- What does normalized earning power look like?

### Investment decision
- If this were a private business, would I want to own it?
- Is the price sensible relative to intrinsic value?
- Is this an outstanding business at a fair price, or a mediocre one at a cheap price?
- Am I acting from business judgment or from market emotion?
- Is there a better after-tax expected-value use of capital available?

### Risk
- What happens under a severe downside scenario?
- Is debt unquestionably manageable?
- Am I risking permanent capital or franchise safety for incremental return?
- Do I have enough liquidity and flexibility to act when better opportunities arise?

## 14) Most important framework statements from the 1987 letter

1. **Business value matters more than book value.**
2. **Economic performance must be judged against the capital required.**
3. **Outstanding businesses often combine high returns, low leverage, and low capital needs.**
4. **Strong franchises are easier to build in stable, understandable businesses than in rapidly changing ones.**
5. **Management quality, especially capital allocation skill and integrity, is central.**
6. **Stocks should be bought as businesses, not as price tokens.**
7. **Market volatility is useful only to the investor who can value independently.**
8. **Accounting numbers often obscure economic truth; future earning power is the anchor.**
9. **In structurally poor industries, discipline and differentiation are essential.**
10. **Never risk survival or important stakeholder obligations for a little extra return.**
11. **Prefer outstanding businesses at sensible prices to mediocre businesses at bargain prices.**
12. **Keep liquidity and financing flexibility for rare, high-value opportunities.**

## 15) How this letter updates the broader Buffett framework

Relative to Buffett’s broader body of work, the 1987 letter especially reinforces these themes:
- intrinsic value over book value,
- return-on-capital analysis,
- durable franchise preference,
- exceptional management as a compounding variable,
- skepticism toward reported earnings in estimate-heavy industries,
- treating public stocks as private-business purchases,
- Mr. Market as a behavioral advantage source,
- quality-over-cheapness,
- capital allocation as a core CEO skill,
- and conservative balance-sheet design centered on survival and optionality.
