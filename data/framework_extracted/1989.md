# Berkshire Hathaway 1989 Shareholder Letter — Reusable Buffett Analysis Framework

Source: Berkshire Hathaway Chairman's Letter, 1989.

## Purpose of this extraction

This document extracts the **reusable stock/business analysis framework** embedded in Warren Buffett’s 1989 Berkshire Hathaway shareholder letter. It is **not** a year summary. It isolates the underlying **principles, criteria, mental models, evaluation questions, and performance logic** Buffett uses to assess businesses, securities, managers, capital allocation, accounting figures, and risk.

---

# 1) Foundational Valuation Logic

## 1.1 Intrinsic value matters more than book value

### Principle
The economically relevant figure is **intrinsic value**, not accounting net worth. Book value can understate or overstate business worth depending on the economics of the assets carried.

### Buffett logic
A business is worth the present value of all future cash flows, discounted at prevailing interest rates. Book value is only a rough proxy and can become misleading when accounting carrying values diverge from true business economics.

### Reusable implication
Never treat book value, GAAP equity, or market price as the same thing as business value.

### Evaluation questions
- What is the business rationally worth based on future cash it can produce?
- Does accounting capital understate or overstate real earning power?
- Is book value still a useful proxy here, or has it become detached from intrinsic value?

### Performance logic
If intrinsic value compounds faster than book value, reported equity growth understates real progress. If market price moves closer to intrinsic value, investors can enjoy a second source of return beyond business performance.

---

## 1.2 Separate business performance from valuation re-rating

### Principle
Investment returns can come from two distinct engines:
1. growth in intrinsic business value, and
2. a market revaluation that closes the gap between price and value.

### Buffett logic
Past performance may look exceptional because both engines operated at once. Future performance may be weaker if the valuation gap has already closed, even if the underlying businesses remain excellent.

### Reusable implication
When projecting returns, do not extrapolate historic results without decomposing them into:
- underlying business compounding, and
- multiple expansion / discount closing.

### Evaluation questions
- How much of past return came from business improvement?
- How much came from valuation re-rating?
- Has the “catch-up” already happened?

### Performance logic
Once a stock is no longer underpriced, future returns depend much more heavily on internal business performance.

---

## 1.3 Size is an anchor on future growth

### Principle
High growth rates are harder to sustain as the capital base becomes large.

### Buffett logic
Compounding math becomes more difficult at scale. A tiny base can grow rapidly for a long time; a very large base eventually encounters scarcity of opportunities.

### Reusable implication
Judge expected growth relative to the size of the business, the addressable market, and the amount of capital needing deployment.

### Evaluation questions
- How large is the current capital base?
- What reinvestment opportunities exist that are both large enough and attractive enough?
- Is the growth expectation realistic given scale?

---

# 2) Accounting vs Economic Reality

## 2.1 Treat deferred taxes as a real but unusual liability

### Principle
Deferred tax liabilities are neither equivalent to ordinary near-term payables nor meaningless accounting fiction.

### Buffett logic
Deferred taxes on appreciated securities economically resemble an **interest-free loan from the government** that becomes payable upon realization. This liability has real value, but its timing and cost are structurally different from trade debt.

### Reusable implication
When valuing a company, classify liabilities by economic character, not just accounting label.

### Evaluation questions
- Is this liability immediate, fixed, and unavoidable?
- Or is it elective, long-dated, and linked to asset turnover?
- What financing benefit does the company receive from the deferral?

### Performance logic
A deferred tax liability can behave like low-cost leverage and improve compounding if management can defer realization for long periods.

---

## 2.2 Reported earnings can understate real earning power

### Principle
GAAP earnings may omit economically relevant earnings from investees.

### Buffett logic
For minority stakes, accounting rules often record only dividends received, not Berkshire’s share of retained earnings. But if retained earnings are intelligently reinvested by capable managers, those earnings still belong economically to the owner.

### Reusable implication
Use **look-through earnings**, not just reported earnings, when analyzing owner economics.

### Evaluation questions
- What earnings power is hidden by accounting treatment?
- How much of investee earnings is being retained rather than distributed?
- Are those retained earnings being deployed intelligently?

### Performance logic
A company’s intrinsic value grows through both distributed cash and undistributed earnings that are reinvested at good returns.

---

## 2.3 Economic presentation can be more useful than GAAP presentation

### Principle
Financial statements should be reorganized in a way that matches how the economics of the business actually work.

### Buffett logic
Buffett groups Berkshire into segments that help estimate intrinsic value rather than simply accepting GAAP presentation at face value.

### Reusable implication
Analytical framing matters. Recast financials by economic function when necessary.

### Evaluation questions
- What business segments drive intrinsic value?
- Which assets are operating and which are non-operating?
- Which accounting adjustments obscure real business performance?

---

# 3) Earnings Quality and Owner Economics

## 3.1 Look-through earnings are central

### Principle
Fundamental earning power is best measured by combining:
- reported operating earnings, and
- the owner’s share of retained operating earnings of investees,
while excluding capital gains.

### Buffett logic
Capital gains are episodic and valuation-dependent; operating earnings better reflect business economics. Retained earnings of strong investees are economically valuable if reinvested well.

### Reusable implication
Anchor analysis on recurring earning power that can plausibly compound over time.

### Evaluation questions
- What are normalized operating earnings?
- How much retained earnings belong economically to the owner?
- Are capital gains disguising weak operating economics?

---

## 3.2 Retained earnings are valuable only if management allocates them well

### Principle
Undistributed earnings deserve near-full value only when they are controlled by talented, owner-oriented managers with attractive reinvestment opportunities.

### Buffett logic
A dollar retained by a great manager may be worth at least a dollar to shareholders, and potentially more. That is not true for most management teams.

### Reusable implication
Do not automatically credit retained earnings as value creation. First test managerial capital allocation skill.

### Evaluation questions
- Does management create more than $1 of value per $1 retained?
- Is retained capital earning high incremental returns?
- Is the manager owner-oriented, skilled, and rational?

---

# 4) Business Quality Framework

## 4.1 Prefer wonderful businesses over statistically cheap mediocre ones

### Principle
It is better to buy a **wonderful business at a fair price** than a **fair business at a wonderful price**.

### Buffett logic
The “cigar butt” strategy can occasionally produce a final puff of value, but mediocre businesses erode investor advantage over time because low returns on capital consume the benefit of a cheap purchase price.

### Reusable implication
Cheapness alone is insufficient. Business quality determines whether time helps or hurts the investor.

### Evaluation questions
- Is the business merely cheap, or actually good?
- Will time amplify value creation or expose structural weakness?
- Is current undervaluation compensating for poor economics, or is it a genuine bargain in a strong business?

### Performance logic
Time is the friend of the wonderful business and the enemy of the mediocre one.

---

## 4.2 Good management cannot rescue bad economics

### Principle
When capable managers operate in structurally bad businesses, business economics usually dominate managerial talent.

### Buffett logic
A brilliant manager on a broken-down business rarely changes the long-run outcome. The reputation of the business remains intact.

### Reusable implication
Analyze the economics of the horse before admiring the jockey.

### Evaluation questions
- Does the industry structure permit attractive returns?
- Is management excellence being mistaken for business quality?
- Would even an average operator do well in this business? Or would even a great one struggle?

---

## 4.3 Seek simple, understandable businesses

### Principle
Invest only in businesses whose economics can be understood well enough to make a reasonably intelligent forecast.

### Buffett logic
Buffett explicitly avoids businesses with too much technology or economics outside his circle of competence.

### Reusable implication
Understanding is a hard filter, not a soft preference.

### Evaluation questions
- Can I explain how this business makes money in plain language?
- Can I make a reasonably intelligent estimate of future economics?
- Is complexity hiding fragility or merely making the story sound impressive?

---

## 4.4 Look for durable economic advantages expressed in operating structure

### Principle
Some outstanding businesses possess structural advantages that reinforce themselves.

### Buffett logic from examples in the letter
- huge selection + low costs + buying power + low prices + high traffic can create a reinforcing loop;
- high penetration in a local newspaper increases advertiser value, which supports profits;
- brand strength plus effective advertising can lift volume and economics.

### Reusable implication
Study the **mechanism** by which an advantage compounds.

### Evaluation questions
- What feedback loop strengthens this business over time?
- Does scale lower cost, improve selection, strengthen distribution, or raise customer utility?
- Does customer preference translate into pricing power, volume advantage, or both?

---

## 4.5 Use return on equity and return on invested capital, but interpret them correctly

### Principle
High returns on equity and invested capital are strong evidence of business quality, especially when achieved without leverage.

### Buffett logic
Berkshire admires businesses earning good returns on equity while using little or no debt. High returns generated by leverage are less impressive.

### Reusable implication
Focus on **unlevered business quality**, not cosmetically boosted returns.

### Evaluation questions
- What is return on equity?
- What is return on invested capital?
- How much of that return depends on leverage?
- Are cash balances masking or distorting operating returns?

---

# 5) Management Assessment Framework

## 5.1 Back first-class businesses with first-class management

### Principle
Business quality and management quality should be evaluated together.

### Buffett logic
Buffett wants “first-class businesses accompanied by first-class managements.” He repeatedly highlights managers who are operationally excellent, owner-oriented, trustworthy, and deeply knowledgeable.

### Reusable implication
Assess both:
- the economics of the business, and
- the character and capital allocation skill of the people running it.

### Evaluation questions
- Is management rational, skilled, and operationally excellent?
- Are they owner-oriented?
- Do they think like long-term partners rather than hired optimizers of short-term optics?

---

## 5.2 Integrity is non-negotiable

### Principle
Never partner with people lacking admirable qualities, regardless of business attractiveness.

### Buffett logic
Buffett prefers to go into business only with people he likes, trusts, and admires. He explicitly says he has never succeeded in making a good deal with a bad person.

### Reusable implication
Character is part of intrinsic value because it affects capital allocation, reporting honesty, incentives, and downside risk.

### Evaluation questions
- Do I trust management’s representations?
- Do they treat shareholders as partners?
- Would I still want to own this business if the numbers looked the same but management changed?

---

## 5.3 Owner orientation increases the value of retained earnings

### Principle
Managers who think like owners are more likely to convert retained earnings into per-share value.

### Buffett logic
Buffett praises managers who allocate capital carefully, operate efficiently, and focus on shareholder outcomes rather than institutional habits.

### Reusable implication
Owner orientation is not a slogan; it is visible in capital allocation, conservatism, candor, and long-term behavior.

### Evaluation questions
- How have managers used retained earnings historically?
- Do they chase size, prestige, and activity, or value per share?
- Do they act differently from peers when economics warrant it?

---

## 5.4 Great managers often excel in details

### Principle
Superior managers often have unusually precise command of operations, customers, cost structures, and competitive conditions.

### Buffett logic
He repeatedly praises managers who know every “nut and bolt” of their businesses and whose excellence shows up in margins, penetration, and returns.

### Reusable implication
Operational mastery is a real edge, especially in businesses where execution compounds.

### Evaluation questions
- Does management show specific operating understanding or only generic strategic language?
- Can they explain the drivers of margins, customer behavior, and competitive position concretely?

---

# 6) Capital Allocation Framework

## 6.1 Maximize eventual net worth, not immediate reportable earnings

### Principle
Capital should be allocated to the highest **after-tax expected return** opportunities within the circle of competence, not to whatever flatters near-term accounting earnings.

### Buffett logic
Buffett searches continuously across common stocks, fixed income, cash equivalents, and arbitrage for the best after-tax mathematical expectation, while ignoring reported earnings optics.

### Reusable implication
Capital allocation should be judged by long-run owner wealth creation, not accounting cosmetics.

### Evaluation questions
- What is the after-tax expected return of each available use of capital?
- What is the opportunity cost of choosing one use over another?
- Is management optimizing optics or economics?

---

## 6.2 Capital allocation is opportunity-cost driven

### Principle
Selling an asset is rational when a better opportunity appears, regardless of whether the existing asset is currently at a gain or loss.

### Buffett logic
Buffett sold tax-exempt bonds not because they had become bad in isolation, but because a better deployment appeared.

### Reusable implication
Always compare an asset to the best currently available alternative.

### Evaluation questions
- What is the best available next use of capital?
- Am I evaluating this asset on an absolute basis instead of a relative opportunity-cost basis?

---

## 6.3 Structure the instrument to fit the quality and knowability of the business

### Principle
When business economics are uncertain or outside one’s predictive comfort, investment structure should provide downside protection while retaining upside.

### Buffett logic
For industries like airlines, paper, or investment banking—where long-term economics were less predictable—Buffett used convertible preferreds rather than common stock.

### Reusable implication
Security selection is part of business analysis. The right answer may be debt, preferred, common, or no investment at all.

### Evaluation questions
- Do I truly understand the industry economics?
- If not, can I structure a claim that protects principal while preserving acceptable upside?
- Is the instrument matched to my confidence level?

---

## 6.4 Flexibility has value

### Principle
Capital commitments should be judged not only by stated return, but also by what flexibility they consume.

### Buffett logic
A mediocre fixed return can be disappointing if it ties up capital and causes the investor to miss future, better opportunities.

### Reusable implication
Illiquidity and optionality cost should be part of the hurdle rate.

### Evaluation questions
- What opportunities am I giving up by locking capital here?
- Does the expected return adequately compensate for lost flexibility?

---

# 7) Tax-Aware Compounding

## 7.1 Deferral materially improves compounding

### Principle
When equivalent pre-tax returns are compared, the timing of tax payments can produce enormous differences in after-tax outcomes.

### Buffett logic
Repeated realization and taxation interrupt compounding. Long holding periods allow gains to compound untaxed for longer.

### Reusable implication
Turnover is not costless. Tax friction is a major determinant of long-term owner returns.

### Evaluation questions
- How often does this strategy force taxable realization?
- What is the after-tax, not pre-tax, compounding path?
- Does patience create a structural edge here?

---

## 7.2 Long holding periods require both economics and trust

### Principle
A “Rip Van Winkle” approach works only when the underlying businesses are strong and management is worthy of long-term confidence.

### Buffett logic
Buffett prefers staying with rare, excellent business relationships rather than optimizing every last increment of return through constant switching.

### Reusable implication
Long duration is earned by quality, not by passivity.

### Evaluation questions
- Is this business good enough to deserve long holding periods?
- Are management quality and economics strong enough to justify low turnover?

---

# 8) Risk Framework

## 8.1 Risk is not price volatility alone

### Principle
A temporary mark-to-market decline does not equal fundamental impairment.

### Buffett logic
A general market decline could reduce Berkshire’s net worth for a year without disturbing intrinsic value or long-term economics.

### Reusable implication
Distinguish:
- quote risk,
- earnings risk,
- balance sheet risk,
- permanent capital loss risk.

### Evaluation questions
- Is the problem a quoted price change or a deterioration in business value?
- Would lower market prices harm or help a long-term owner?

---

## 8.2 Avoid situations with a small chance of ruin, even if upside odds are favorable

### Principle
A small probability of distress or disgrace is unacceptable even when the expected return appears attractive.

### Buffett logic
Buffett rejects leverage levels that could generate better average returns at the cost of even a small chance of serious trouble.

### Reusable implication
Do not trade survivability for incremental return.

### Evaluation questions
- Does this decision introduce non-trivial ruin risk?
- Am I reaching for return in a way that could permanently impair capital or reputation?

---

## 8.3 Underwrite decisions, not quarterly appearances

### Principle
It is acceptable to look foolish in the short run if the underlying decision process is sound.

### Buffett logic
Berkshire is willing to accept lumpy reported results if the expected value of the underwriting or investment decision is favorable.

### Reusable implication
Short-term embarrassment is not the same as bad risk-taking. Evaluate decisions by expected value and prudence, not by smoothness of reported earnings.

### Evaluation questions
- Is management optimizing for appearance or for expected value?
- Would a one-quarter loss reveal imprudence or simply honest exposure to a favorable bet?

---

# 9) Insurance-Specific Framework

## 9.1 Insurance is a commodity-like business except when capacity is scarce

### Principle
Property-casualty insurance tends toward subnormal profitability unless there is a shortage of usable industry capacity.

### Buffett logic
With many participants selling a commodity-like product at independently set prices, strong average profitability appears only when fear temporarily constrains supply.

### Reusable implication
In cyclical commodity-like industries, profits depend heavily on supply discipline, not managerial aspiration.

### Evaluation questions
- Is industry capacity tight or abundant?
- Are competitors disciplined because they are scared, or aggressive because they are eager?
- Is the current pricing environment driven by real scarcity or by wishful thinking?

---

## 9.2 Ignore industry “needs”; study economic fundamentals

### Principle
An industry’s desire for higher prices does not cause higher profitability. Economics do.

### Buffett logic
Insurance may “need” higher prices, but profitability will improve only when actual capacity shortages force the market to turn away business despite higher prices.

### Reusable implication
Never confuse management desire, sell-side narrative, or industry lobbying with actual economic power.

### Evaluation questions
- What concrete mechanism will change industry economics?
- Are price increases supported by scarcity, or merely hoped for?

---

## 9.3 Underwriting discipline outranks volume growth

### Principle
Do not write business with negative expected value merely to maintain volume.

### Buffett logic
Berkshire is content to let premium volume shrink rather than write insurance at rates that imply loss.

### Reusable implication
In any business, walk away from unprofitable growth.

### Evaluation questions
- Is management pursuing volume at inadequate pricing?
- Are current margins mathematically attractive, or is growth being purchased?

---

## 9.4 Float is valuable only when underwriting losses are tolerable

### Principle
Insurance float is attractive funding only if underwriting losses are low enough that the cost of float remains acceptable.

### Buffett logic
Large float plus tolerable underwriting losses can produce highly favorable economics through investment income on policyholder funds.

### Reusable implication
Evaluate float as a financing source with a cost.

### Evaluation questions
- How large is float relative to premium volume?
- What is the cost of float implied by underwriting results?
- Is float growing, stable, or running off?

---

## 9.5 Supply capital when others cannot

### Principle
Extraordinary returns often appear when fear creates temporary shortages and one has both capital and emotional steadiness.

### Buffett logic
After catastrophe events created a shortage of reinsurance capacity, Berkshire moved instantly when prices became attractive.

### Reusable implication
Preparedness plus courage matters. Keep resources and decision speed ready for stressed markets.

### Evaluation questions
- Is there temporary market dislocation creating favorable pricing?
- Do I have liquidity and balance sheet strength to act?
- Can I retain the risk rather than relying on fragile onward distribution?

---

# 10) Marketable Securities Framework

## 10.1 Choose among asset categories by after-tax expected value

### Principle
There is no permanent bias toward stocks, bonds, cash, or arbitrage. The right choice is the one with the best after-tax mathematical expectation within one’s understanding.

### Buffett logic
Buffett treats asset classes as competing uses of capital rather than ideological identities.

### Reusable implication
Be valuation-driven, not category-driven.

### Evaluation questions
- Which available asset class offers the highest after-tax expected return now?
- Am I being driven by habit or by opportunity?

---

## 10.2 Stay inside the circle of competence

### Principle
Only invest in alternatives whose economics you believe you understand.

### Buffett logic
Buffett will not forecast industries he cannot understand and therefore structures exposure differently or avoids it.

### Reusable implication
Understanding is part of margin of safety.

---

## 10.3 Arbitrage must be economically sensible, not “greater fool” dependent

### Principle
Special situations should be pursued only when the odds are attractive and the transaction itself makes economic sense.

### Buffett logic
Buffett avoided many arbitrage opportunities because the underlying transactions seemed irrational.

### Reusable implication
Avoid situations where payoff depends more on crowd behavior than on sound economics.

### Evaluation questions
- Do I actually like the odds?
- Does the deal make economic sense?
- Is the spread compensation for analyzable risk, or am I relying on a greater fool?

---

# 11) Capital Structure and Financing Framework

## 11.1 A base business cannot be made excellent by clever financing

### Principle
Accounting or capital-structure engineering cannot convert a weak business into a strong one.

### Buffett logic
Zero-coupon and PIK financing can postpone pain and create the illusion of adequacy, but they do not improve business earning power.

### Reusable implication
Always analyze the economics of the enterprise before admiring the financing structure.

### Evaluation questions
- Is capital structure disguising weak operating economics?
- Would the business still be attractive under a plain-vanilla financing structure?

---

## 11.2 Interest must be covered from real current cash flow after real capital needs

### Principle
True debt service capacity must be measured after allowing for capital expenditures needed to sustain the business.

### Buffett logic
Buffett rejects EBDIT-style reasoning that ignores depreciation and accrued interest. Maintenance capex is economically real.

### Reusable implication
For leveraged businesses, test cash coverage conservatively.

### Evaluation questions
- Does current cash flow comfortably cover all interest, both cash and accrued?
- Are maintenance capital expenditures being honestly included?
- Is depreciation a fair rough proxy for long-run capital needs?

---

## 11.3 Be skeptical of metrics designed to justify bad deals

### Principle
When promoters invent flattering metrics to rationalize obviously stretched transactions, danger is high.

### Buffett logic
He criticizes EBDIT and similar measures because they ignore real economic costs.

### Reusable implication
Whenever a metric systematically excludes essential expenses, treat it as a warning sign.

### Evaluation questions
- What real economic costs are being excluded?
- Is the metric illuminating economics or hiding them?
- Who benefits from using this metric?

---

## 11.4 Gatekeepers matter

### Principle
Intermediaries should protect capital providers from excess rather than merely facilitate transactions.

### Buffett logic
Investment bankers often failed as gatekeepers by promoting unsound deals.

### Reusable implication
Analyze incentive structures around the company, not just the company itself.

### Evaluation questions
- Who is being paid regardless of long-term outcome?
- Are advisors aligned with investors or with transaction volume?

---

# 12) Anti-Patterns and Mistakes Buffett Explicitly Flags

## 12.1 The “cigar butt” trap

Cheap bad businesses may produce a final puff of value, but they usually do not compound well.

## 12.2 Overestimating managerial heroics

A brilliant manager rarely overcomes structurally poor business economics.

## 12.3 Tackling seven-foot hurdles

It is usually better to find obvious, solvable opportunities than to prove brilliance on difficult problems.

## 12.4 Thumb-sucking / omission errors

Big missed opportunities inside one’s circle of competence can be as costly as visible mistakes.

## 12.5 Institutional imperative

Organizations drift into irrationality through inertia, empire building, budget absorption, internal justification, and peer imitation.

### Reusable checks for institutional imperative
- Is management resisting needed change because it is already in motion?
- Are projects appearing mainly because capital is available?
- Are strategy decks rationalizing what leadership already wants to do?
- Is peer behavior being copied without independent reasoning?

## 12.6 Excess leverage for incremental return

A strategy that slightly raises expected returns but creates even a small chance of serious trouble is unattractive.

# 13) Acquisition Framework Explicitly Stated in the Letter

Buffett directly lists the qualities Berkshire seeks in acquisitions. This is one of the clearest reusable checklists in the letter.

## Acquisition criteria
1. **Large purchase size** — enough earnings to matter.
2. **Demonstrated consistent earning power** — not projections, stories, or turnarounds.
3. **Good returns on equity with little or no debt** — quality without financial crutches.
4. **Management already in place** — the business must run itself under existing leaders.
5. **Simple business** — understandable economics.
6. **Seller names a price** — avoid time-wasting auctions and vague processes.

## Additional implied acquisition preferences
- Friendly transactions only.
- Confidentiality and speed matter.
- Cash is preferred unless stock is exchanged at favorable intrinsic-value terms.
- Ideal sellers are owner-managers who want liquidity but also want to remain involved.

# 14) Reusable Buffett-Style Analysis Questions

## Business economics
- Is this a wonderful business or merely a cheap one?
- What structural advantages protect returns on capital?
- Does time help or hurt this business?
- Are returns on equity/invested capital high without leverage?

## Management
- Do I trust, admire, and like management?
- Are they owner-oriented?
- How intelligently have they allocated retained earnings?
- Do they know the details of the business deeply?

## Valuation
- What is intrinsic value based on future cash flows?
- How different is intrinsic value from book value or market price?
- How much of expected return depends on business compounding vs re-rating?

## Earnings quality
- What are normalized operating earnings?
- What hidden earning power exists through retained earnings of investees?
- Are accounting figures obscuring economic reality?

## Capital allocation
- What is management optimizing: reported earnings or eventual net worth?
- Are there better alternative uses of capital?
- Is the investment structure appropriate for the certainty level of the economics?

## Risk
- What could permanently impair capital?
- Is leverage introducing any meaningful chance of distress?
- Is apparent smoothness just hiding latent risk?

## Industry structure
- Is this a commodity business or one with durable advantages?
- Does profitability depend on temporary capacity shortages?
- Are favorable conditions based on hope or on actual supply-demand discipline?

## Tax and time
- How much turnover-induced tax friction exists?
- Does deferral create a compounding edge?
- Is this business worthy of long holding periods?

# 17) One-Paragraph Synthesis

The 1989 letter shows Buffett analyzing businesses through an **economic-owner lens**: estimate intrinsic value from future cash flows; adjust accounting numbers to reflect real earning power; prefer simple, high-return, low-debt businesses run by trustworthy, owner-oriented managers; treat retained earnings as valuable only when capital allocation is excellent; allocate capital by after-tax expected return and opportunity cost; avoid leverage and promotional metrics that can create even a small chance of ruin; and recognize that exceptional long-term returns come from combining business quality, disciplined valuation, tax-efficient compounding, and the temperament to act aggressively only when odds are decisively favorable.
