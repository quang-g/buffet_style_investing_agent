# Berkshire Hathaway 2004 Shareholder Letter — Reusable Buffett Analysis Framework

**Source letter:** Berkshire Hathaway Shareholder Letter dated February 28, 2005 (covering 2004).  
**Extraction goal:** isolate reusable stock/business analysis logic, not year-specific events.

---

## 1) Core lens: think in intrinsic value, not accounting labels

### Principle
Buffett’s true scorecard is **per-share intrinsic value**, not book value, reported earnings, revenue growth, or realized gains.

### Reusable logic
- A metric is useful only if it helps estimate **intrinsic value per share**.
- Book value can be a rough proxy in some businesses, but it may become less relevant as the business mix changes.
- Reported accounting results can be directionally useful while still being economically incomplete or distorted.
- Performance should be assessed **per share**, not in aggregate.

### Evaluation questions
- What is the best estimate of **intrinsic value per share**?
- Is reported book value a decent proxy here, or does it materially understate/overstate economic value?
- Are management actions increasing **per-share** value, or merely increasing size?
- Which reported metrics are economically meaningful, and which are mostly accounting noise?

### Performance logic
- A business or portfolio should be judged by whether it compounds **per-share intrinsic value** over time.
- Aggregate growth that requires dilution or poor capital allocation may destroy owner value even if headline numbers rise.

---

## 2) Use the correct benchmark: opportunity cost matters

### Principle
Management must beat the owner’s realistic alternative. For Buffett, that baseline is a **low-cost S&P 500 index fund**.

### Reusable logic
- Capital allocation skill should be compared against a simple passive alternative available to shareholders.
- If active decisions do not exceed a low-cost benchmark over time, management is not adding value.
- Short-term relative results can be noisy; long-term outperformance is what matters.

### Evaluation questions
- What can owners achieve on their own with a passive alternative?
- Does this company’s capital allocation justify active control over shareholder capital?
- Are underperformance/excess returns structural or just timing noise?

### Performance logic
- Long-term owner value depends on whether management beats the default alternative **after costs, taxes, and risk**.

---

## 3) Treat stocks as businesses, not tickers

### Principle
Equities are **fractional ownership interests in businesses**.

### Reusable logic
- Ignore chart patterns, market tips, and near-term opinion noise.
- Focus on the business: earnings power, cash generation, returns on capital, resilience, and reinvestment prospects.
- Market prices matter primarily at entry/exit; business performance drives long-term value.

### Evaluation questions
- If the stock market closed for years, would I still want to own this business?
- What is my share of the business’s normalized earnings?
- How has my share of earnings and cash distributions grown over time?
- Is the market price asking me to pay for business reality or market fantasy?

### Performance logic
- Sound investing comes from business analysis first, market quotation second.

---

## 4) Demand discipline over activity

### Principle
It is better to do nothing than to do something stupid. Cash is uncomfortable, but forced deployment is worse.

### Reusable logic
- Uninvested cash is a symptom of opportunity scarcity, not necessarily managerial failure.
- A disciplined investor resists buying mediocre assets merely to stay busy.
- The hurdle is not “find something,” but “find something that offers a reasonable return.”

### Evaluation questions
- Is management deploying capital because returns are attractive, or because idle cash feels embarrassing?
- What return is implied by the current price?
- Is the company/investor willing to wait when no compelling opportunities exist?

### Performance logic
- Avoiding bad capital allocation is a major source of long-term outperformance.

---

## 5) Seek simplicity; avoid multi-variable fragility

### Principle
Prefer **simple propositions** where the outcome does not depend on many independent things going right.

### Reusable logic
- The more variables that must all break favorably, the lower the true probability of success.
- Complex projects often fail because only one weak link is needed to ruin the whole chain.
- Favor businesses with understandable economics and few critical failure points.

### Evaluation questions
- How many things must go right for this investment thesis to work?
- Which variable is the weak link?
- Is this a “mono-linked chain” or a chain with many failure points?
- Are returns being driven by robust economics or by a fragile sequence of assumptions?

### Performance logic
- Simpler businesses with fewer dependencies have a higher probability of satisfactory long-term outcomes.

---

## 6) Price for profit, not for volume

### Principle
In bad industries or commodity-like markets, **discipline in pricing** matters more than growth in reported volume.

### Reusable logic
- In commodity businesses, customers often do not care who supplies the product if the offering is similar.
- This creates intense price competition and constant temptation to chase volume.
- Rational operators will let business walk away rather than accept inadequate economics.
- Shrinking volume is acceptable if it preserves underwriting or operating discipline.

### Evaluation questions
- Is management sacrificing price discipline to protect revenue growth?
- Does the business accept unattractive customers/contracts/orders just to maintain utilization or scale?
- Would management willingly endure a large drop in volume to preserve economics?
- Does the culture reward profitability or mere growth?

### Performance logic
- In difficult industries, the winners are often those willing to look weak in the short term in order to stay rational.

---

## 7) Culture can be a durable competitive advantage

### Principle
Some advantages are not informational or technological; they are **behavioral and cultural**.

### Reusable logic
- Buffett highlights managerial temperament and institutional culture as core moats.
- A culture that resists volume chasing, leverage abuse, accounting games, and organizational fear is rare.
- Discipline that survives pressure cycles is more valuable than strategy statements.

### Evaluation questions
- What does management do when the easy money is in doing the wrong thing?
- Does the culture survive adversity, or only look disciplined in good times?
- Are employees pressured into rationalizing bad decisions to protect jobs or status?
- Are incentives aligned with long-term rationality?

### Performance logic
- Culture is revealed during downturns, pricing pressure, and periods when “doing less” is the correct move.

---

## 8) Incentives drive behavior; structure them carefully

### Principle
A business should be designed so people do not need to choose between self-preservation and rational economics.

### Reusable logic
- Fear of layoffs, status loss, or missed bonuses can push employees toward bad underwriting, bad pricing, or bad growth.
- Good systems reduce the pressure to act irrationally.
- Incentives should reward economic reality, not flattering accounting marks or superficial volume growth.

### Evaluation questions
- What behaviors are current incentives encouraging?
- Are people rewarded for growth, reported earnings, marks, adjusted metrics, or actual economics?
- Do employees/managers face pressure that could rationalize poor decisions?
- Are compensation and governance mechanisms likely to improve or degrade decision quality?

### Performance logic
- Misaligned incentives can destroy economics long before the accounting shows it.

---

## 9) In commodity businesses, low cost is a major moat

### Principle
If the product is largely undifferentiated, one way to win is to be the **lowest-cost operator**.

### Reusable logic
- A true cost advantage permits both growth and satisfactory profitability.
- Low-cost operators can pass savings to customers, gain share, and still earn attractive returns.
- Cost advantage is especially powerful when distribution is structurally more efficient.

### Evaluation questions
- Is the business genuinely lower cost, or just temporarily cheaper?
- What structural feature creates the cost advantage: distribution, scale, process, sourcing, or asset density?
- Can competitors replicate the cost structure?
- Is the cost advantage being converted into share gains and owner returns?

### Performance logic
- In commodity settings, a durable cost edge can be more valuable than branding rhetoric.

---

## 10) When promises matter, counterparty quality matters

### Principle
In insurance and reinsurance, the product is a **promise**, so solvency and reliability are part of the product.

### Reusable logic
- The value of a contractual promise depends on the issuer’s ability and willingness to perform under stress.
- Counterparty selection is part of underwriting and risk management.
- A cheap promise from a weak counterparty can be more expensive than a costly promise from a strong one.

### Evaluation questions
- Is the counterparty’s promise credible in a stress scenario?
- How exposed is the business to the failure of insurers, reinsurers, lenders, or derivative counterparties?
- Does management plan for “unthinkable” but plausible stress events?
- Would the company’s obligations still be honored after a severe shock?

### Performance logic
- A business that can unquestionably perform in crisis gains trust, share, and long-term advantage.

---

## 11) Prepare for severe but plausible tail risk

### Principle
A sound business must be able to survive and function after large shocks, not merely during normal years.

### Reusable logic
- Stress events that many call “unthinkable” may still be economically plausible.
- Capital structure and liquidity should be evaluated against tail events, not only expected cases.
- The ability to write checks the day after a catastrophe is itself a competitive advantage.

### Evaluation questions
- What severe but plausible scenarios could hit this business?
- Can it survive without needing heroic refinancing, dilution, or government rescue?
- How much capital/liquidity is available under stress?
- Would customers, creditors, and counterparties trust it after a shock?

### Performance logic
- Resilience creates option value: weak competitors retreat after shocks, while strong firms can gain share.

---

## 12) Float, deferred obligations, and other liabilities can be valuable funding only if costed correctly

### Principle
Not all liabilities are bad; some are valuable sources of funding if their **true economic cost** is low or negative.

### Reusable logic
- Insurance float is attractive only when underwriting is at least break-even, and exceptional when underwriting is profitable.
- The right question is not whether funding is available, but what it truly costs.
- Some liabilities appear cheap until loss estimates catch up with reality.

### Evaluation questions
- What is the economic cost of this funding source?
- Is the business being paid to hold funds, breaking even, or paying too much for them?
- How reliable are reserve estimates or future obligation assumptions?
- Is the liability stable and durable enough to support long-term investment?

### Performance logic
- A business with low-cost, well-managed liabilities can enjoy a structural financing advantage.

---

## 13) Beware accounting that front-loads or flatters economics

### Principle
Reported earnings can mislead when accounting recognizes gains early, delays losses, or relies on soft marks.

### Reusable logic
- Derivatives, securitizations, options, reserve estimates, and mark-to-market conventions can create phantom profits.
- Rapid growth can mask underlying weaknesses.
- The real test of earning power often appears when growth stops and economics must stand on their own.

### Evaluation questions
- Which earnings components are cash, which are estimates, and which are marks?
- What assumptions determine reported profitability?
- How would earnings look in a no-growth or runoff mode?
- Is the company using accounting conventions to accelerate recognition of value?

### Performance logic
- The more judgment-heavy the accounting, the more important skepticism becomes.

---

## 14) A no-growth test often reveals the truth

### Principle
Some financial businesses look best while expanding. To judge real economics, examine performance in a **no-growth** or runoff state.

### Reusable logic
- Growth can hide poor underwriting, weak loan quality, bad marks, or inadequate reserves.
- Mature books often reveal whether prior reported profits were real.
- Businesses that only look good when expanding deserve suspicion.

### Evaluation questions
- What happens to returns if growth stops tomorrow?
- What does the existing book earn absent new volume?
- Are historical profits being validated as the book matures?
- Is growth creating value or delaying recognition of problems?

### Performance logic
- True earning power should survive a no-growth environment.

---

## 15) Use leverage sparingly and only when the economics are straightforward

### Principle
Debt is not forbidden, but it should be used only in **limited, intelligible, and controllable** circumstances.

### Reusable logic
- Buffett generally shuns debt except where assets and cash flows are well understood and the spread is attractive.
- Borrowing against profitable, interest-bearing receivables can be sensible when asset quality and funding terms are clear.
- Debt at subsidiaries should be separated from parent obligations when risk containment is important.

### Evaluation questions
- Why is leverage being used here?
- Is the borrowed money funding productive, understandable assets or speculative hopes?
- Are asset-liability characteristics matched?
- Could this leverage impair survival under stress?

### Performance logic
- Leverage can enhance good economics but can rapidly destroy a business with fragile or opaque economics.

---

## 16) Capital allocation is part of management quality

### Principle
A great operating business can still disappoint owners if capital is allocated poorly.

### Reusable logic
- Managers should run their businesses as if the business were the family’s only asset for 100 years.
- Excess cash should be sent to the best allocator rather than wasted internally.
- Capital allocation choices include acquisitions, securities purchases, debt management, repurchases, retention, and inaction.

### Evaluation questions
- Does management deploy excess cash intelligently?
- Are acquisitions judged by per-share value creation, not empire building?
- Is capital retained only when reinvestment opportunities are compelling?
- Does management candidly admit when allocation was poor?

### Performance logic
- Over time, management’s allocation skill can matter as much as operating skill.

---

## 17) Favor managers who are candid, rational, and decentralized

### Principle
Buffett values managers who are honest about mistakes, think like owners, and can operate autonomously.

### Reusable logic
- Candor is a sign of rationality, not weakness.
- Superior managers can be left alone; micromanagement is not the preferred model.
- Delegation works when responsibility is genuinely handed off.

### Evaluation questions
- Does management speak plainly about failures and trade-offs?
- Are local operators treated like responsible owners?
- Does headquarters add value or merely bureaucracy?
- Is decentralization supported by trust and accountability?

### Performance logic
- Autonomy paired with integrity often yields better long-term decisions than centralized control.

---

## 18) Judge management by behavior under temptation

### Principle
Managerial quality shows up when leaders face the temptation to chase volume, hide losses, pursue fashionable deals, or flatter earnings.

### Reusable logic
- Integrity and rationality are most visible when the crowd is doing the opposite.
- Avoiding mistakes can be more important than brilliance.
- A manager who “takes the walk rather than swing at a bad pitch” is often superior to a more active but less disciplined operator.

### Evaluation questions
- What did management do when opportunities were abundant but unattractive?
- Does the manager accept inactivity, shrinkage, or criticism when economics demand it?
- Are mistakes acknowledged quickly?
- Does the manager protect the downside first?

### Performance logic
- Temperament is a core driver of investment and operating outcomes.

---

## 19) Evaluate returns on tangible capital, while respecting purchase price

### Principle
Buffett separates the operating quality of a business from the return achieved on the owner’s carrying value.

### Reusable logic
- A business can earn strong returns on tangible net worth while delivering a lower return on the acquirer’s cost if bought at a premium.
- Good business quality does not automatically mean a good investment.
- The purchase price determines investor returns even when the underlying business is excellent.

### Evaluation questions
- What is the return on tangible capital in the operating business?
- What return is being earned on the actual acquisition price or carrying value?
- Is goodwill/premium justified by future economics?
- Am I evaluating the business quality separately from the investment return?

### Performance logic
- Great businesses can be mediocre investments if overpaid for.

---

## 20) Capital intensity matters; utilization often drives returns

### Principle
In capital-heavy businesses, high margins alone are not enough; returns depend heavily on **asset utilization**.

### Reusable logic
- Businesses requiring large capital per unit of revenue need either high utilization, high pricing power, or both.
- Incremental changes in utilization can dramatically affect returns.
- Asset-heavy businesses should be assessed on return on capital, not just operating margin.

### Evaluation questions
- How much capital is required to produce each dollar of revenue?
- What utilization rate is needed for acceptable returns?
- How cyclical is utilization?
- Can management maintain pricing and demand in downturns?

### Performance logic
- Capital intensity raises the hurdle for acceptable economics.

---

## 21) A moat can be brand, cost, distribution, trust, location, or embedded habit

### Principle
Competitive advantage comes in multiple forms, and Buffett looks for practical evidence rather than abstract labels.

### Reusable logic
Possible moats highlighted or implied in the letter include:
- **Low-cost structure**
- **Brand and consumer preference**
- **Distribution efficiency**
- **Reputation/trust in critical promises**
- **Local market strength**
- **Customer habit and repeat behavior**
- **Managerial excellence in acquisition/integration**

### Evaluation questions
- What specifically keeps competitors from taking share or destroying margins?
- Is the moat visible in customer behavior, economics, or both?
- Is the moat local, national, or global?
- Has the moat survived adverse conditions?

### Performance logic
- A moat is real only if it supports durable returns and resilience across cycles.

---

## 22) Separate cyclical pain from structural weakness

### Principle
Some businesses are cyclical but attractive; others are structurally impaired. Buffett distinguishes the two.

### Reusable logic
- Temporary weakness can coexist with good long-term economics.
- A cyclical business can still deserve enthusiasm if it has good management, a durable position, and long-run consolidation opportunities.
- Structural decline requires more skepticism than cyclical fluctuation.

### Evaluation questions
- Is the current problem cyclical, temporary, financing-related, or structural?
- Will normalized conditions restore attractive economics?
- Is management using the downturn to strengthen the franchise?
- Does the business have a path to long-term growth despite near-term cyclicality?

### Performance logic
- Buying or holding through cyclical weakness can be intelligent if structural quality remains intact.

---

## 23) Acquisition logic: buy more earnings streams, but only at reasonable prices

### Principle
Acquisitions are desirable when they add meaningful, durable earnings streams at prices that imply good future returns.

### Reusable logic
- Size alone is not enough; acquisitions must improve long-term owner economics.
- The key test is per-share value creation.
- Bolt-on acquisitions can be especially attractive when an excellent operator has a proven integration model.

### Evaluation questions
- Does this acquisition add durable earning power?
- Is the price reasonable relative to normalized earnings and reinvestment needs?
- Will it improve per-share intrinsic value?
- Does management have demonstrated skill in integrating acquisitions?

### Performance logic
- Acquisition success depends on both asset quality and purchase price discipline.

---

## 24) Ignore realized/unrealized distinctions when they obscure economics

### Principle
Whether gains are realized this quarter or remain unrealized often says little about intrinsic value.

### Reusable logic
- Selling activity can be driven by tax, liquidity, or opportunity considerations rather than “earnings management.”
- Accounting recognition timing can obscure the economic reality of value creation.
- Investors should track business economics, not the superficial pattern of reported gains.

### Evaluation questions
- Is reported gain/loss timing economically meaningful?
- What has actually changed in intrinsic value?
- Is management selling because better uses exist, because risk changed, or because accounting optics look good?

### Performance logic
- Economic value, not recognition timing, should drive interpretation.

---

## 25) Governance should focus on the few questions that actually matter

### Principle
Governance is effective when it addresses a small number of consequential decisions, not a long checklist of formalities.

### Reusable logic
Buffett highlights three central governance questions:
1. Does the company have the right CEO?
2. Is CEO compensation excessive?
3. Are proposed acquisitions likely to create or destroy per-share value?

### Evaluation questions
- Is the CEO rational, candid, skilled, and owner-oriented?
- Is compensation aligned with owner outcomes?
- Are board decisions focused on per-share value, not activity or optics?
- Do directors have incentives and temperament to challenge management when needed?

### Performance logic
- Good governance protects owners from misallocation, overpayment, and managerial self-interest.

---

## 26) Independence is economic, not cosmetic

### Principle
True independence comes from aligned economic interests, not merely from formal labels.

### Reusable logic
- Directors with meaningful ownership bought with their own money may be more independent than fee-dependent “independent” directors.
- Small holdings plus large board fees can distort incentives.
- Ownership alignment often matters more than governance box-checking.

### Evaluation questions
- How much stock do directors truly own relative to their wealth?
- Was the stock purchased personally or granted by the company?
- Are board fees economically meaningful to directors?
- Are directors more aligned with owners or with management continuity?

### Performance logic
- Governance quality improves when decision-makers travel the same road as shareholders.

---

## 27) Treat stock options and adjusted pay narratives skeptically

### Principle
Compensation should be accounted for honestly; non-expensing or soft treatment of options distorts economics.

### Reusable logic
- Options are a real cost.
- Failing to expense them overstates profitability and can inflate executive compensation.
- Incentive tools may be useful, but they should be structured rationally and costed honestly.

### Evaluation questions
- Is compensation fully reflected in reported economics?
- Do option structures reward true value creation or easy gains from retention and market lift?
- Are investors being asked to ignore real costs?

### Performance logic
- Understated compensation expense can cause systematic overvaluation of businesses.

---

## 28) Prefer businesses that can redeploy excess cash intelligently

### Principle
A high-quality business is even better when it can either reinvest at good returns or remit capital to superior allocators.

### Reusable logic
- Businesses should fund their genuine needs first, then send excess cash to where it can earn the highest return.
- A decentralized group benefits when subsidiaries do not hoard cash for low-return projects.
- Internal capital markets work only if top allocators are unusually disciplined.

### Evaluation questions
- Does the business generate excess cash after maintenance needs?
- Can it reinvest internally at high returns?
- If not, will cash be distributed, repurchased, or redirected intelligently?

### Performance logic
- Cash generation without allocation discipline can still produce mediocre shareholder results.

---

## 29) Use normalized earnings, not peak or distorted figures

### Principle
A sound appraisal relies on **normal earnings power**, not one-off gains, temporary write-offs, or bubble conditions.

### Reusable logic
- Separate recurring economics from unusual charges, accounting quirks, and extraordinary market swings.
- A business should be valued on sustainable earning power.
- Market gains/losses can diverge sharply from business performance in the short term.

### Evaluation questions
- What are normalized earnings after stripping out one-offs?
- Is current profitability above, below, or near normal?
- Does market price reflect temporary excitement or durable economics?

### Performance logic
- Buying against normalized earning power reduces the odds of overpaying for temporary conditions.

---

## 30) The investor’s enemies are costs, excitement, tips, and bad timing

### Principle
Poor investor results often come from behavior, not from the underlying productivity of business.

### Reusable logic
- Excessive fees and trading costs erode returns.
- Fads and tips replace quantified thinking with impulse.
- Start-stop market participation leads to buying after rises and selling after declines.
- Emotional control is an investing edge.

### Evaluation questions
- Is the decision rooted in business analysis or in excitement?
- How much frictional cost am I incurring?
- Am I acting because prices moved, or because value and price diverged?
- Am I being greedy when others are fearful, or merely following mood?

### Performance logic
- Sound behavior compounds just as surely as sound businesses do.

---

## 31) Buffett-style business analysis checklist distilled from the 2004 letter

### Business quality
- Is the business understandable and relatively simple?
- Does it have a moat: cost, brand, trust, distribution, local dominance, or habit?
- Are returns on tangible capital attractive?
- Is capital intensity reasonable, and is utilization under control?
- Is the business cyclical or structurally impaired?

### Management quality
- Does management think and act like owners?
- Will management sacrifice volume to preserve economics?
- Is management candid about mistakes?
- Are incentives aligned with rational behavior?
- Can responsibility be decentralized safely?

### Financial quality
- What is normalized earning power?
- What is the true cost of liabilities/funding?
- Is leverage limited, intelligible, and survivable?
- Are accounting numbers economically meaningful or distorted?
- Can the company survive severe stress?

### Capital allocation
- Can retained earnings be reinvested at high returns?
- Are acquisitions likely to create per-share value?
- Is excess cash deployed rationally, or hoarded/forced into weak opportunities?
- Are buy/sell decisions grounded in value rather than optics?

### Valuation
- What is intrinsic value per share?
- What return does the current price imply?
- Am I paying too much even for a great business?
- Is the gap between price and value wide enough to offer a reasonable return?

### Governance
- Is the CEO right for the job?
- Is compensation honest and sensible?
- Do directors have real owner alignment?
- Are major decisions evaluated on per-share value creation?

---

## 32) Compact reusable prompt for future company analysis

Use this when analyzing a public company in a Buffett-like way:

> Analyze this company as a fractional ownership interest in a business, not as a stock symbol. Estimate the drivers of per-share intrinsic value. Assess business quality, moat, simplicity, normalized earnings power, return on tangible capital, capital intensity, management quality, incentive alignment, capital allocation skill, balance-sheet resilience, tail-risk survivability, and governance quality. Identify whether growth is value-creating or merely volume-driven. Distinguish cyclical issues from structural weakness. Evaluate whether reported accounting reflects economic reality, including compensation, reserves, marks, and leverage. Conclude with the key reasons this business may or may not compound per-share intrinsic value at an attractive rate from the current price.
