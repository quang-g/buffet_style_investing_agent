# Buffett Reusable Investment / Business Analysis Framework  
## Extracted from Berkshire Hathaway Chairman's Letter (1996)

**Source letter:** Berkshire Hathaway Chairman's Letter, 1996.  
**Extraction objective:** isolate **reusable investment principles, evaluation criteria, mental models, diagnostic questions, and performance logic** that can be applied to analyzing a stock, company, or business in a Buffett-like way.  
**Important:** This is **not** a summary of 1996 events. It is a framework distilled from the letter.

---

## 1) Core Meta-Principles

### 1.1 Intrinsic value matters more than book value
Buffett explicitly distinguishes **intrinsic value** from **book value**. Book value may be a rough proxy for economic progress, but the true objective is growth in intrinsic value.

**Reusable principle**
- Never mistake an accounting metric for economic reality.
- Use reported numbers as clues, not as the final answer.
- Ask what the business is *actually worth* based on future cash-generation ability.

**Application**
- When evaluating a company, treat book value, GAAP earnings, and reported ratios as starting points.
- Build toward an estimate of intrinsic value using business economics, reinvestment ability, durability, and capital allocation.

**Key diagnostic question**
- What economic value exists that standard accounting does not fully capture?

---

### 1.2 Market price and business value eventually converge
Buffett stresses that while stock prices can overperform or underperform intrinsic value temporarily, over time shareholder gains must track business gains.

**Reusable principle**
- In the long run, stock returns are governed by business performance.
- Temporary divergence between price and value creates transfers between buyers and sellers.
- Long holding periods reduce the importance of entry/exit distortions and increase the importance of business quality.

**Application**
- Focus first on the trajectory of business value.
- Treat price/value gaps as important, but secondary to whether the business itself compounds.

**Key diagnostic questions**
- Is the stock’s recent performance ahead of, behind, or in line with intrinsic value growth?
- If the market closed for 10 years, would the business progress still justify ownership?

---

### 1.3 The right goal is not smooth results, but superior long-term economics
Buffett prefers “a lumpy 15% over time” to a smooth 12%.

**Reusable principle**
- Volatility in reported earnings is acceptable if long-term economics are attractive.
- Do not reject a business merely because accounting outcomes are uneven.
- Distinguish **volatility of results** from **permanent impairment of value**.

**Application**
- For cyclical, insurance, commodity, or catastrophe-exposed businesses, analyze long-run expected economics rather than demanding smooth annual performance.
- Ask whether temporary volatility is being compensated by superior normalized returns.

**Key diagnostic questions**
- Is earnings volatility structural and destructive, or tolerable and well-priced?
- Does the business earn enough over time to justify unpleasant short-term fluctuations?

---

### 1.4 Costs matter enormously
Buffett emphasizes that frictional costs compound against owners over time, whether in mutual funds or operating businesses.

**Reusable principle**
- Low cost structures create strategic flexibility and can widen competitive advantage.
- At the investor level, fees and expenses heavily erode long-term compounding.
- At the business level, lower costs can support lower prices, stronger customer value, and better economics.

**Application**
- Look for businesses with persistent cost advantages.
- Prefer simple, low-overhead structures.
- Penalize high-fee intermediated products and bloated operating models.

**Key diagnostic questions**
- Is the company a structurally low-cost operator?
- Do expense levels strengthen or weaken its moat?
- How much of gross economic gain actually reaches the owner?

## 2) Business Quality Framework

### 2.1 Buy businesses with excellent economics and outstanding managers
When describing acquisitions, Buffett says Berkshire seeks exactly two things: **excellent business economics** and **an outstanding manager**.

**Reusable principle**
A great investment often requires both:
1. A business with favorable underlying economics.
2. A manager who thinks and behaves like an owner.

**Application**
Evaluate both the machine and the operator:
- Machine = moat, margins, reinvestment profile, pricing power, cash generation, resilience.
- Operator = honesty, focus, rationality, capital allocation, owner orientation.

**Key diagnostic questions**
- If management changed tomorrow, would the business still be good?
- If the business stayed the same, would this management still create value?
- Does management think of the company as “theirs” in the stewardship sense?

---

### 2.2 Favor businesses likely to remain strong 10–20 years from now
Buffett prefers businesses “unlikely to experience major change” and seeks operations “virtually certain to possess enormous competitive strength ten or twenty years from now.”

**Reusable principle**
- Predictability is a major investing asset.
- Rapid change may create big upside, but it reduces certainty.
- Buffett wants favorable long-duration economics, not excitement.

**Application**
Prefer industries where:
- customer behavior is stable,
- demand drivers are persistent,
- competitive positions are understandable,
- change is evolutionary rather than disruptive.

**Key diagnostic questions**
- What is most likely to matter in this industry 10–20 years from now?
- Can I reasonably predict the company’s competitive position across that horizon?
- Is this a business where change is an ally or a destroyer of forecasting ability?

---

### 2.3 Prefer “Inevitables” and a few “Highly Probables”
Buffett describes a tiny class of businesses whose global dominance and economic strength are so durable that their leadership appears close to inevitable.

**Reusable principle**
- The best businesses are not merely good companies; they are businesses with unusually durable, widening leadership.
- True inevitables are rare.
- Most apparent leaders are impostors.

**Application**
Screen for:
- entrenched consumer preference,
- global brand dominance,
- scale advantages,
- distribution dominance,
- repeat purchase behavior,
- durable habit or identity attachment,
- widening rather than shrinking market share.

**Key diagnostic questions**
- Is this company merely a current leader, or does the business structure create near-inevitability?
- What would have to happen for it to lose leadership?
- Are those failure conditions plausible?

---

### 2.4 A wonderful business can still be a poor investment at the wrong price
Buffett states clearly that investors can overpay even for the best businesses.

**Reusable principle**
- Business quality does not eliminate valuation risk.
- In overheated markets, even outstanding businesses can require long catch-up periods before intrinsic value grows into price.

**Application**
- Separate “Would I love to own this business?” from “At this price, is it a good investment?”
- Require a rational purchase price even for elite franchises.

**Key diagnostic questions**
- What assumptions are embedded in the current price?
- How long would intrinsic value need to compound before today’s price is justified?
- Am I paying for excellence twice: once in fundamentals and again in valuation?

## 3) Competitive Advantage / Moat Logic

### 3.1 Sustainable low cost is a moat
GEICO is presented as a model case: low costs allow low prices; low prices attract and retain customers; customer referrals reduce acquisition costs further; lower acquisition cost lowers costs again.

**Reusable principle**
A cost advantage can create a **self-reinforcing moat loop**:

1. Lower operating costs  
2. Lower customer prices  
3. Better customer acquisition / retention  
4. More scale / more referrals  
5. Lower per-unit costs  
6. Stronger moat

**Application**
Look for businesses where cost leadership is:
- structural, not temporary;
- scalable;
- customer-visible;
- reinforced by behavior and network effects.

**Key diagnostic questions**
- Is the low-cost position structural or cyclical?
- Does cost advantage get passed to customers in a way that expands moat?
- Is there a feedback loop that gets stronger with scale?

---

### 3.2 Scale can deepen penetration and lower costs further
Buffett notes GEICO does best where market penetration is high; as policy count grows, costs should decline further.

**Reusable principle**
- Scale is most powerful when it improves unit economics.
- The best scale advantages reduce cost, improve customer value, and reinforce share gains.

**Application**
- Examine whether growth increases efficiency or merely increases complexity.
- Reward companies where additional scale improves economics for both firm and customer.

**Key diagnostic questions**
- Does scale improve economics per unit?
- In which geographies, segments, or channels is the company strongest, and why?
- Does growth widen or dilute the moat?

---

### 3.3 Reputation for certainty of performance is a competitive advantage
In super-cat reinsurance, Buffett highlights Berkshire’s ability and willingness to pay under extreme stress. That reliability itself attracts business.

**Reusable principle**
- In certain industries, **counterparty trust** is a moat.
- Financial strength plus credible behavior can create competitive differentiation.
- Reliability during stress can matter more than headline price.

**Application**
Look for businesses where customers prize certainty:
- insurance,
- financial services,
- enterprise infrastructure,
- mission-critical B2B services,
- regulated or safety-sensitive industries.

**Key diagnostic questions**
- Do customers choose this company because it will perform when others may fail?
- Is balance-sheet strength itself part of the product?
- Does crisis reliability create repeat business or preferred-customer status?

---

### 3.4 Large-capacity ability can be a moat
Buffett points out Berkshire can offer very large reinsurance coverages quickly, something few can match.

**Reusable principle**
- Capacity, speed, and decisiveness can be strategic advantages.
- The ability to solve unusually large problems can attract premium opportunities.

**Application**
- In analysis, test whether the company has capabilities competitors cannot easily match because of capital, infrastructure, trust, or decision speed.

**Key diagnostic questions**
- What can this firm do at scale that competitors cannot?
- Does unique capacity convert into better customers, better terms, or better economics?

---

## 4) Management Quality Framework

### 4.1 Owner-oriented managers are prized
Buffett praises managers who think of the company as “his.”

**Reusable principle**
- The best managers act like long-term owner-stewards, not salaried caretakers.
- Owner-mindedness often shows up in discipline, intensity, cost consciousness, and rational capital allocation.

**Signals**
- candid communication,
- focus on economics rather than optics,
- refusal to empire-build,
- comfort with decentralization and responsibility,
- pride in the business rather than in corporate theatrics.

**Key diagnostic questions**
- Does management behave like stewards of capital?
- Do they optimize long-term value or short-term appearance?
- Are they emotionally attached to business quality or to size/status?

---

### 4.2 Great businesses can be damaged by distracted management
Buffett warns that one of the biggest risks is when management neglects a wonderful base business and buys mediocre or unrelated businesses.

**Reusable principle**
- A superior core franchise can be undermined by managerial loss of focus.
- Diworsification destroys value.
- Hubris and boredom are recurring managerial failure modes.

**Application**
Penalize:
- unrelated acquisitions,
- constant reinvention away from the successful core,
- conglomerate drift,
- managerial fascination with novelty over strength.

**Key diagnostic questions**
- Is management protecting and extending the core, or wandering away from it?
- Are acquisitions strategic and disciplined, or ego-driven?
- Is capital being reinvested where the company truly has an edge?

---

### 4.3 We never fool with success
Buffett’s principle in acquired companies is to keep great managers running the business without unnecessary interference.

**Reusable principle**
- When something works, preserve the conditions that make it work.
- Avoid central interference that damages local excellence.

**Application**
- In evaluating conglomerates or holding companies, look for whether capital allocation is centralized but operations are left to capable operators.
- Assess whether parent-level intervention is helpful or harmful.

**Key diagnostic questions**
- Does the organization preserve high-performing local cultures?
- Is control exercised where necessary and restraint shown where wise?

---

## 5) Incentive Design Principles

### 5.1 Incentives should fit the economics of the specific business
Buffett explicitly lays out Berkshire’s compensation principles.

**Reusable principle**
Good incentives are:
1. **Tailored to the economics of the specific business**
2. **Simple and measurable**
3. **Directly related to daily activities of participants**

**Application**
- Judge compensation systems not by complexity, but by alignment.
- The best plans tell people exactly what matters and reward the right outcomes.

**Key diagnostic questions**
- Are incentives specific to what truly drives value in this business?
- Can employees understand what is being measured?
- Do participants influence the variables on which they are paid?

---

### 5.2 Avoid “lottery ticket” compensation
Buffett criticizes broad stock-option-like arrangements disconnected from the employee’s real operating control.

**Reusable principle**
- Pay systems should not create huge random outcomes unrelated to controllable performance.
- Incentives disconnected from causality can be wasteful and demotivating.

**Application**
- Be skeptical of compensation tied to broad stock moves, macro conditions, or factors outside management control.
- Prefer role-specific, economics-linked compensation.

**Key diagnostic questions**
- Is pay driven by controllable performance or by randomness?
- Does compensation sharpen focus or diffuse it?

---

### 5.3 Measure different functions by the economics they actually control
Buffett notes that insurance investment managers should be paid for investment performance, not for underwriting outcomes, and vice versa.

**Reusable principle**
- Separate accountability by domain.
- Do not let excellence in one area be canceled in compensation by failure elsewhere.

**Application**
- In multi-part businesses, analyze segment incentives separately.
- Assess whether the organization distinguishes underwriting skill, investment skill, operating skill, and capital allocation skill.

**Key diagnostic questions**
- Are people being judged on results they actually control?
- Does the firm isolate true value creators instead of pooling outcomes?

---

## 6) Insurance / Float Framework

### 6.1 Float is valuable only if its cost is attractive
Buffett defines insurance float as money held but not owned, generated by premiums received before losses are paid. Float is valuable only if its cost is lower than alternative funding costs.

**Reusable principle**
- Insurance value is not just underwriting profit or investment income separately.
- It is the joint economics of:
  1. amount of float generated,
  2. durability of float,
  3. cost of float,
  4. opportunities to invest float intelligently.

**Application**
Analyze insurers by asking:
- How much float do they produce?
- What is its cost over time?
- Is the insurer being paid to hold money, roughly breaking even, or paying dearly for funds?

**Key diagnostic questions**
- Is float an asset or an albatross?
- Over a cycle, is underwriting profitable, break-even, or structurally loss-making?
- Can management invest float safely and well?

---

### 6.2 Negative-cost float is extraordinary
Buffett highlights that underwriting profits make float effectively less than free.

**Reusable principle**
- The ideal insurer both generates investable funds and gets paid to hold them.
- This combination can dramatically enhance intrinsic value.

**Application**
- When analyzing insurers, distinguish between reported book value and the economic value of low-cost or negative-cost float.
- Track combined ratio over long periods, not just one year.

**Key diagnostic questions**
- Does the company have a long record of underwriting discipline?
- Is recent underwriting success structural or lucky?
- How much of value depends on unusual underwriting conditions?

---

### 6.3 Insurance risk must be survivable under extreme scenarios
Buffett insists that even large catastrophe exposures must not meaningfully damage Berkshire’s intrinsic value.

**Reusable principle**
- Never take risks that can seriously impair the enterprise.
- Size positions and exposures so that bad outcomes are painful but non-fatal.

**Application**
For insurers, lenders, banks, and leveraged firms:
- stress-test worst-case scenarios;
- assess aggregation risk;
- examine whether a single event could impair long-term compounding ability.

**Key diagnostic questions**
- What is the worst plausible loss?
- Would that loss merely hurt earnings, or threaten the franchise?
- Is management designing risk assuming bad surprises will occur?

---

### 6.4 “Virtually all surprises are unpleasant”
Buffett uses this as a practical underwriting principle.

**Reusable principle**
- In risky activities, surprises skew negative.
- Build in conservatism.
- Avoid models that create false precision and false confidence.

**Application**
- Require margin for error in underwriting, lending, forecasting, and valuation.
- Penalize businesses whose economics rely on exact assumptions being right.

**Key diagnostic questions**
- Where are the hidden downside asymmetries?
- Is the analysis robust to error, or dependent on fragile precision?
- Are models informing judgment or replacing it?

---

### 6.5 Computer precision can be a chimera
Buffett criticizes overreliance on catastrophe models and points out that apparent precision can mislead decision-makers.

**Reusable principle**
- Quantification is useful, but false precision is dangerous.
- Judgment must dominate models when tail risk and complex systems are involved.

**Application**
- In business analysis, do not confuse a detailed model with a reliable conclusion.
- Prefer approximate truth to precise nonsense.

**Key diagnostic questions**
- Which assumptions in this model are actually knowable?
- Does numeric elegance mask deep uncertainty?
- Would the conclusion hold with rougher, more conservative assumptions?

---

### 6.6 Reverse engineer failure
Buffett cites Charlie Munger’s inversion: know where you could die, then avoid going there.

**Reusable principle**
- Start by identifying what could permanently destroy the business or investment.
- Design decisions to avoid unacceptable outcomes.

**Application**
- Before asking “How much can I make?”, ask “How can I get killed?”
- Test leverage, cyclicality, customer concentration, regulation, technology risk, and liquidity risk.

**Key diagnostic questions**
- What sequence of events could permanently impair this company?
- What conditions would force dilution, distress, or loss of strategic control?
- Has management deliberately avoided these failure paths?

---

## 7) Capital Allocation and Portfolio Construction

### 7.1 Inactivity can be intelligent
Buffett says Berkshire often makes more money “snoring than active.”

**Reusable principle**
- Great investing often requires patience, not activity.
- Trading is not evidence of intelligence.
- Once a wonderful business is bought at a sensible price, the job becomes monitoring rather than constant action.

**Application**
- Prefer long holding periods for superior businesses.
- Avoid reactionary trading driven by macro chatter or market commentary.

**Key diagnostic questions**
- Am I acting because value changed, or because price moved?
- Is activity solving a problem or satisfying restlessness?

---

### 7.2 Public-stock investing and whole-business acquisition should follow the same logic
Buffett says the art of investing in public companies is little different from acquiring subsidiaries.

**Reusable principle**
- Stocks are not lottery tickets; they are part interests in businesses.
- Use the same standards for both public and private investments.

**Application**
For any stock, ask:
- Would I want to own the whole business at this valuation?
- Are economics attractive enough that I would welcome illiquidity?

**Key diagnostic question**
- Am I buying a ticker or a business?

---

### 7.3 Concentration follows from finding exceptional opportunities
Buffett argues that successful investing often results in a few positions becoming very large.

**Reusable principle**
- Concentration is not inherently reckless when driven by knowledge, conviction, and business quality.
- Selling merely because a winner becomes large can be irrational.

**Application**
- Let position size reflect business quality, certainty, and valuation.
- Resist arbitrary diversification rules when they dilute obvious excellence.

**Key diagnostic questions**
- Is concentration arising from informed conviction or unmanaged risk?
- Would reducing this position improve the portfolio, or just make it look more conventional?

---

### 7.4 Circle of competence matters more than breadth
Buffett says you do not need to understand many companies; you only need to evaluate selected businesses within your circle of competence.

**Reusable principle**
- Depth beats breadth.
- Knowing the limits of understanding is a critical investing edge.

**Application**
- Build a narrow, explicit domain where you can judge economics and value with confidence.
- Pass on opportunities outside that boundary.

**Key diagnostic questions**
- Is this business truly understandable to me?
- Do I know what drives its value and failure?
- Am I staying inside or outside my circle?

---

### 7.5 Prefer certainty of a good result over hope for a great one
Buffett says he would rather be certain of a good result than hopeful of a great one.

**Reusable principle**
- Favor high-probability compounding over speculative upside.
- Asymmetric certainty is often superior to glamorous possibility.

**Application**
- Prefer businesses with durable moderate-to-high returns and strong predictability over stories with explosive upside but weak visibility.

**Key diagnostic questions**
- Is my thesis based on evidence of durability or on imagination?
- Am I paying for probability or possibility?

---

## 8) Earnings Quality / Economic Reality Framework

### 8.1 Reported earnings can understate economic earnings
Buffett introduces “look-through earnings” because reported earnings only include dividends received from investees, not Berkshire’s share of retained earnings.

**Reusable principle**
- Accounting earnings may omit economically important earnings retained by investees.
- True owner economics may exceed reported profits.

**Application**
For holdings companies or firms with significant minority investments:
- estimate owner-level share of investee earnings,
- adjust for taxes that would be due if earnings were distributed,
- distinguish recurring operating economics from accounting presentation.

**Key diagnostic questions**
- What earnings attributable to ownership are not flowing through reported income?
- Are retained earnings at investees creating value through high-return reinvestment?

---

### 8.2 Retained earnings are valuable only if reinvested well
Buffett is happy not to receive dividends if investees can reinvest at high rates.

**Reusable principle**
- Retention is beneficial only when reinvestment returns exceed what owners could achieve elsewhere.
- Dividend policy should be judged by capital allocation quality, not by payout preference alone.

**Application**
- Examine whether retained cash is producing incremental value.
- Be skeptical of retained earnings in mediocre businesses with weak reinvestment opportunities.

**Key diagnostic questions**
- What return is management earning on retained capital?
- Is retained cash compounding intrinsic value or merely accumulating?

---

### 8.3 Separate recurring operating earnings from non-recurring gains
Buffett distinguishes operating earnings from securities gains and other non-recurring items.

**Reusable principle**
- To value a business, isolate repeatable earning power.
- Non-recurring gains may be real, but they should not be capitalized like core operating earnings.

**Application**
- Strip out one-offs, unusual accounting charges, and episodic gains.
- Build valuation on sustainable economics.

**Key diagnostic questions**
- What portion of earnings is truly repeatable?
- Are reported gains masking weak core economics?

---

### 8.4 Non-GAAP can be useful if it better reflects economics
Buffett uses presentations that differ from GAAP when they better show underlying business performance.

**Reusable principle**
- The right question is not “Is this GAAP?” but “Does this help me understand economic reality better?”
- However, adjustments must be intellectually honest, consistent, and decision-useful.

**Application**
- Accept analytical adjustments when they clarify, not flatter.
- Reject adjusted metrics that simply inflate performance optics.

**Key diagnostic questions**
- Does this adjustment improve insight into owner economics?
- Is management using non-GAAP to illuminate or to obscure?

---

## 9) Price, Temperament, and Market Behavior

### 9.1 Intelligent investing is not complex, but it is not easy
Buffett says the intellectual framework is simple; the challenge is judgment and discipline.

**Reusable principle**
- Successful investing depends more on temperament, selectivity, and discipline than on academic complexity.

**Application**
- Keep the process simple:
  1. understand the business,
  2. value the business,
  3. compare price to value,
  4. act only when odds are favorable,
  5. hold while the thesis remains intact.

**Key diagnostic questions**
- Am I making this harder than necessary?
- Is complexity improving accuracy or merely creating the feeling of sophistication?

---

### 9.2 You do not need advanced finance theory to invest well
Buffett explicitly downplays beta, efficient markets, option pricing, and modern portfolio theory as necessary tools for successful investing.

**Reusable principle**
- The essential disciplines are:
  - knowing how to value a business,
  - knowing how to think about market prices.

**Application**
- Anchor analysis in business valuation and behavioral discipline.
- Use theory only where it helps, not as a substitute for economic reasoning.

**Key diagnostic questions**
- Does this framework help me value the business or think better about price?
- Is the analysis grounded in economics or in abstract theory?

---

### 9.3 If you are not willing to own it for ten years, don’t own it for ten minutes
This is Buffett’s holding-period test.

**Reusable principle**
- Time horizon should match business ownership, not market fluctuation.
- A short-term mindset weakens analysis quality and emotional discipline.

**Application**
- Use a long-duration filter before purchase.
- Ask whether you would be comfortable owning during a period of market closure.

**Key diagnostic question**
- Could I rationally hold this through a long stretch of market indifference?

---

### 9.4 Panic selling on scary news is usually a mistake
Buffett warns against selling fine businesses because of frightening events.

**Reusable principle**
- Scary headlines do not automatically equal impaired intrinsic value.
- Emotional reaction is often the enemy of rational compounding.

**Application**
- During shocks, ask whether business value changed materially or merely sentiment did.
- Separate solvency threats from noise.

**Key diagnostic questions**
- Has intrinsic value been damaged, or is the market simply alarmed?
- Is this event transient, cyclical, or thesis-breaking?

## 10) Practical Buffett-Style Evaluation Checklist

### A. Business Understandability
- Is the business easy enough to understand?
- What are the key drivers of demand, margins, and cash flow?
- Can I predict its broad economics 10–20 years out with reasonable confidence?

### B. Economic Moat
- Does the company have a durable cost advantage, brand advantage, network effect, switching cost, regulatory edge, or scale advantage?
- Is the moat widening, stable, or shrinking?
- Is there a reinforcing loop that strengthens the moat over time?

### C. Management
- Are management’s communications candid and economics-focused?
- Do they behave like owners?
- Are they focused on strengthening the core or distracted by empire-building?

### D. Capital Allocation
- Does management reinvest retained earnings at high rates?
- Are acquisitions disciplined and on-strategy?
- Is leverage used conservatively?

### E. Earnings Quality
- What are normalized operating earnings?
- What economic earnings are hidden by accounting conventions?
- Are there meaningful look-through earnings or off-income-statement value drivers?

### F. Risk
- What can go wrong that causes permanent impairment?
- Are there tail risks, leverage risks, concentration risks, or modeling risks?
- Are exposures survivable under harsh scenarios?

### G. Valuation
- What is a rational estimate of intrinsic value?
- What growth and return assumptions are embedded in the current price?
- Is there a sufficient margin between price and value?

### H. Portfolio Fit
- Is this inside my circle of competence?
- Is conviction high enough to justify a meaningful position?
- Am I choosing certainty of a good result over hope for a great one?

## 11) Performance Logic Buffett Is Using

This letter implies a clear logic chain for evaluating investments:

### Step 1: Identify superior business economics
Look for durable advantages, predictability, and the ability to generate high returns on capital or highly favorable float economics.

### Step 2: Verify management quality
Confirm that management is able, honest, focused, owner-oriented, and rational about incentives and capital allocation.

### Step 3: Estimate economic earnings, not just reported earnings
Adjust for accounting distortions, retained investee earnings, one-offs, and differences between book value and intrinsic value.

### Step 4: Judge risk through survivability and inversion
Ask what can permanently damage the enterprise. Avoid fragility, excessive leverage, and dependence on false precision.

### Step 5: Buy only at a sensible price
Even the best company can be a bad investment if purchased too expensively.

### Step 6: Size meaningfully when certainty is high
Exceptional opportunities deserve meaningful positions.

### Step 7: Hold with patience
If the business keeps compounding intrinsic value, market value should eventually follow.

## 14) One-Sentence Synthesis

The 1996 letter reinforces a Buffett framework centered on **buying understandable, competitively durable, managerially sound businesses at sensible prices; judging them by intrinsic value and long-term economic earnings rather than accounting optics; protecting against permanent loss through conservatism and inversion; and then letting compounding work with minimal activity.**
