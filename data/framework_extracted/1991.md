# Warren Buffett Reusable Stock/Business Analysis Framework — Berkshire Letter 1991

**Source:** Berkshire Hathaway Chairman's Letter (written February 28, 1992, covering 1991)  
**Extraction goal:** Distill reusable investment and business-analysis principles, not year-specific events.

---

## 1) Core analytical lens

The 1991 letter reinforces a recurring Buffett idea: **an investor should evaluate a stock as a partial interest in a business** and judge it by the long-term economics of that business, the quality of management, the capital required to sustain growth, the resilience of the franchise, and the price paid.

This year’s letter particularly sharpens six major analytical lenses:

1. **Look-through economics matter more than reported accounting optics.**
2. **The quality of a business is determined by its ability to earn high returns with little incremental capital.**
3. **A true franchise is qualitatively different from an ordinary business.**
4. **Valuation changes dramatically when assumptions about durability and capital intensity change.**
5. **Management quality should be judged by rationality, owner-orientation, and capital discipline.**
6. **Investment success comes from patience, concentration, and acting decisively when a clearly understandable opportunity appears at a sensible price.**

---

## 2) Reusable principles

### Principle 1: Focus on intrinsic value, not reported yearly swings
**Framework idea:** Annual changes in book value, earnings, or stock prices can be distorted by market revaluation and accounting treatment. What matters is the long-term growth of intrinsic value.

**Reusable implication:**
- Do not mistake a strong year in reported net worth for equally strong business progress.
- Separate **business performance** from **market multiple expansion**.
- Ask whether gains came from:
  - higher operating earnings,
  - higher look-through earnings,
  - better business economics,
  - or simply a richer market valuation.

**Evaluation questions:**
- Did value grow because the business earned more, or because investors paid a higher multiple?
- Is the current result repeatable, or was it a one-time “double dip” from both earnings growth and re-rating?
- What is the likely long-term rate of intrinsic value growth versus the recent reported growth rate?

---

### Principle 2: Use look-through earnings to understand true owner economics
**Framework idea:** For partial ownership stakes, reported dividends understate economic reality. The investor’s real earnings include their share of retained earnings at investees, adjusted for taxes.

**Reusable implication:**
- Treat retained earnings of high-quality investees as part of owner earnings if management reinvests them intelligently.
- Evaluate a portfolio as a collection of businesses producing future look-through earnings.
- Shift attention from near-term stock quotes to long-term earnings power attributable to your ownership.

**Evaluation questions:**
- What are the investee’s true operating earnings attributable to my ownership stake?
- How much of those earnings are distributed versus retained?
- Are retained earnings being reinvested at attractive incremental returns?
- Would my portfolio look stronger or weaker if I judged it by look-through earnings rather than dividend income?

**Performance logic:**
- Over time, **intrinsic value growth should roughly track growth in look-through earnings**.
- Market price is ultimately a scoreboard; **future earnings drive the scoreboard**.

---

### Principle 3: The best businesses grow without needing much additional capital
**Framework idea:** A business deserves a high valuation when it can increase earnings while requiring little incremental capital.

**Reusable implication:**
- The key test is not merely growth, but **growth relative to incremental capital employed**.
- Distinguish between:
  - businesses that must continuously reinvest to grow, and
  - businesses that can grow while sending substantial cash to owners.

**Evaluation questions:**
- How much incremental capital was required to produce the increase in earnings over time?
- Are reported earnings also freely distributable earnings, or must most of them be reinvested?
- Does the company have the ability to raise prices without a commensurate capital burden?
- Is the business a cash generator or a capital consumer?

**Metric ideas:**
- Incremental return on incremental invested capital
- Growth in earnings per dollar of retained earnings
- Capital expenditures relative to depreciation
- Working capital intensity
- Free cash generation versus accounting earnings

---

### Principle 4: The franchise/business distinction is central
**Framework idea:** Buffett explicitly distinguishes an **economic franchise** from an ordinary business.

**A franchise has three traits:**
1. The product or service is needed or desired.
2. Customers perceive no close substitute.
3. The business is not subject to price regulation.

**Observable consequence:**
- A franchise can **regularly price aggressively** and still earn **high returns on capital**.
- A franchise can tolerate some managerial error without suffering mortal damage.

**By contrast, an ordinary business:**
- earns exceptional profits only when it is the low-cost operator or when supply is temporarily tight,
- is more vulnerable to competition,
- and can be badly damaged by poor management.

**Reusable implication:**
- Always determine whether you are analyzing a franchise, a weak franchise, a strong business, or a commodity-like business.
- Misclassification here leads directly to valuation error.

**Evaluation questions:**
- Does the customer see close substitutes?
- Does the company have durable pricing power?
- Are returns on capital sustainably high without leverage?
- Is the company protected by brand, habit, distribution, or embedded consumer preference?
- Could mediocre management still leave the economics intact, or is execution everything?

---

### Principle 5: Small changes in assumptions can cause huge valuation changes
**Framework idea:** Valuation is extremely sensitive to assumptions about durability, growth, and capital needs.

**Reusable implication:**
- A business that appears to be a perpetual grower with little capital requirement may deserve a very high multiple.
- If that same business shifts to a “bob-around” earnings pattern, its valuation may compress dramatically.
- Therefore, **the durability of the business model is often more important than near-term earnings level**.

**Evaluation questions:**
- Is current earnings power part of a durable compounding stream, or merely “normal” earnings that will fluctuate around a flat baseline?
- What happens to valuation if long-term growth falls from durable growth to cyclical stagnation?
- Does secular change alter the company’s economics beyond what cyclical weakness suggests?

**Performance logic:**
- When expectations shift from **durable, capital-light growth** to **cyclical or capital-hungry stagnation**, valuation can collapse even if current earnings do not collapse immediately.

---

### Principle 6: Separate cyclical problems from secular deterioration
**Framework idea:** Cyclical pain may reduce current earnings without damaging intrinsic value, but secular erosion reduces intrinsic value itself.

**Reusable implication:**
- Investors must diagnose whether weakness is temporary or structural.
- A business facing secular deterioration should not be valued using old assumptions.

**Evaluation questions:**
- Is the decline caused by the cycle, by competition, by fragmentation, by technology, or by changing customer behavior?
- Are industry economics reverting after a weak year, or permanently weakening?
- Are managers and analysts anchoring on rear-view-mirror economics?

**Red flag:**
- Businesses once treated like bulletproof franchises may quietly become ordinary businesses.

---

### Principle 7: Balance-sheet strength protects intrinsic value
**Framework idea:** When industry economics weaken, leverage magnifies damage.

**Reusable implication:**
- Strong balance sheets are especially valuable in industries experiencing secular change or cyclical stress.
- Asset shrinkage is far more dangerous when debt is heavy.

**Evaluation questions:**
- How much debt does the company carry relative to current earning power?
- If earnings shrink, does leverage become an amplifier of value destruction?
- Is cash roughly offsetting debt, or is the business truly burdened?
- Would the company remain comfortable if industry conditions stayed poor for years?

**Metric ideas:**
- Debt / net income
- Net debt / EBITDA
- Interest coverage
- Cash versus gross debt
- Debt relative to normalized earnings power

---

### Principle 8: Great management cannot fully rescue bad economics, but it matters enormously in difficult businesses
**Framework idea:** In a franchise, management quality matters, but the business can survive some managerial weakness. In ordinary tough businesses, superior management may be the only reason the company prospers.

**Reusable implication:**
- First judge the business; then judge management within that economic context.
- In tough industries, operator excellence and capital discipline become decisive.

**Evaluation questions:**
- Is management merely benefiting from good economics, or actually creating advantage in a difficult industry?
- Do they avoid foolish acquisitions, leverage, and fashion-driven expansion?
- Have they maintained margins and returns despite a structurally hard environment?
- Do they allocate capital as owners would?

---

### Principle 9: Management should behave like owners, especially in capital use
**Framework idea:** Buffett admires systems that charge managers for capital employed and reward them for true economic profit.

**Reusable implication:**
- Compensation systems should not treat equity capital as free.
- Managers should be evaluated on returns after accounting for capital tied up in the business.

**Evaluation questions:**
- Is management incentivized on volume, adjusted earnings, or true economic profitability?
- Are they charged for the capital they employ?
- Do incentives encourage inventory bloat, receivables growth, empire-building, or real value creation?

**Metric ideas:**
- Return on equity
- Return on tangible capital
- Economic profit after cost of capital
- Cash conversion
- Inventory and receivables efficiency

---

### Principle 10: Buy businesses with demonstrated earning power, not stories
**Framework idea:** Buffett explicitly states what he wants in acquisitions, and the list doubles as a stock-screening framework.

**Preferred characteristics:**
1. Large enough to matter.
2. Demonstrated consistent earning power.
3. Good returns on equity with little or no debt.
4. Management already in place.
5. Simple, understandable business.
6. A sensible offering price.

**Reusable implication:**
- Favor evidence over projections.
- Avoid turnarounds, speculative reinventions, and hard-to-understand technologies outside your circle of competence.

**Evaluation questions:**
- Does the company have a long, demonstrated earnings record?
- Are returns good without leverage?
- Is this understandable enough to underwrite with confidence?
- Am I relying on management promises or observable economics?
- Is there a real price-to-value gap, or just an appealing story?

---

### Principle 11: Circle of competence is a hard filter
**Framework idea:** Buffett rejects opportunities he cannot understand, even if others find them exciting.

**Reusable implication:**
- Complexity is not a virtue.
- The inability to understand the economics is itself a reason to pass.

**Evaluation questions:**
- Can I explain in plain language how this business makes money, why customers stay, and what could impair returns?
- Are the key drivers understandable enough that I can estimate future economics with reasonable confidence?
- Am I being seduced by technology, novelty, or narrative?

---

### Principle 12: Patience and inactivity are competitive advantages
**Framework idea:** The market transfers money from the active to the patient. Great results come from staying with exceptional businesses rather than constantly relocating capital.

**Reusable implication:**
- Do not confuse activity with intelligence.
- Once you own an exceptional business bought at a sensible price, continued ownership is often superior to restless optimization.

**Evaluation questions:**
- Am I selling because fundamentals changed, or because I am restless?
- Have I found a proven winner that should simply be held?
- Is my behavior driven by business facts or market action?

---

### Principle 13: Concentration in a few understandable, superior businesses can reduce risk
**Framework idea:** Over-diversification into businesses you do not understand is not genuine risk reduction.

**Reusable implication:**
- Risk is reduced by depth of understanding and quality of business, not by owning many mediocre positions.
- Concentration is appropriate when knowledge and conviction are high.

**Evaluation questions:**
- Do I know enough about this business to justify meaningful capital?
- Is the business quality high enough to deserve concentration?
- Am I diversifying because of prudence, or because of ignorance?

---

### Principle 14: Opportunity cost includes sins of omission
**Framework idea:** Big investment mistakes often come not from buying bad businesses, but from failing to buy obvious winners that were understandable and attractive.

**Reusable implication:**
- Record missed opportunities where understanding was sufficient but action failed.
- Study hesitation, anchoring, and discomfort with position size.

**Evaluation questions:**
- Did I fail to act despite having understanding, favorable economics, and trustworthy management?
- Did I stop buying merely because the price moved up modestly while value still exceeded price?
- Did my dislike of small positions or desire for perfection cause me to lose a major opportunity?

---

### Principle 15: In insurance, underwriting economics matter more than reported accounting noise
**Framework idea:** For insurers, the central economic question is the **cost of float**, not just the headline combined ratio.

**Reusable implication:**
- Insurance float is valuable only when it is generated at low or negative cost.
- Growth in float is good only if its cost is satisfactory.
- One-year accounting snapshots can mislead in volatile lines like catastrophe reinsurance.

**Evaluation questions:**
- What is the cost of float over time?
- Is underwriting profitable, breakeven, or subsidized by optimistic reserving?
- Is the insurer under-reserving to flatter current results?
- Does management price for long-term profitability rather than current volume?
- Is the company financially strong enough to pay claims under stress, creating a real competitive advantage?

**Metric ideas:**
- Underwriting gain/loss
- Average float
- Cost of float = underwriting loss / average float
- Combined ratio
- Reserve development history
- Premium growth versus loss-cost trend

---

### Principle 16: Beware industries where accounting can obscure weak economics
**Framework idea:** Under-reserving in insurance and flattering assumptions elsewhere can temporarily hide deteriorating economics.

**Reusable implication:**
- Poor economics eventually surface despite accounting maneuvers.
- Reported stability can be false comfort if estimates are aggressive.

**Evaluation questions:**
- What assumptions in reserves, write-downs, asset lives, or “adjusted” earnings could be masking true economics?
- Are managers solving the business problem, or just touching up the x-rays?

---

### Principle 17: Financial strength can be a competitive moat in certain industries
**Framework idea:** In reinsurance, Berkshire’s superior financial strength attracts customers because counterparties care whether claims will actually be paid in stress scenarios.

**Reusable implication:**
- In some sectors, balance-sheet credibility is not just safety; it is a commercial advantage.

**Evaluation questions:**
- Does financial strength win business, better terms, or customer trust?
- Will weaker competitors fail precisely when the product must perform?
- Is solvency itself part of the product offering?

---

### Principle 18: Prefer businesses run by able, shareholder-oriented managers you can leave alone
**Framework idea:** Buffett wants managers in place who love the business, think like owners, and do not require constant oversight.

**Reusable implication:**
- A desirable acquisition or investment should not require the buyer to supply operating talent.
- Decentralization works only when managers are exceptional.

**Evaluation questions:**
- Would the business still perform well if the parent company were largely hands-off?
- Does management love the work, or merely need the compensation?
- Can the company compound without central intervention?

---

### Principle 19: Price discipline remains mandatory even for wonderful businesses
**Framework idea:** Great businesses do not justify unlimited prices.

**Reusable implication:**
- The investment process still requires both:
  - excellent business quality, and
  - a sensible purchase price.

**Evaluation questions:**
- Am I paying a price that still allows an attractive future return?
- How sensitive is my expected return to lower growth, lower multiple, or modest economic disappointment?
- Is the current price embedding “franchise forever” assumptions that may be too generous?

---

### Principle 20: Capital allocation is a primary duty of the investor or holding-company owner
**Framework idea:** Buffett frames his own job as allocating capital generated by strong operating managers.

**Reusable implication:**
- Separate operating excellence from capital allocation excellence.
- In holding companies, conglomerates, or personal portfolios, the core job is deciding where each generated dollar should go.

**Evaluation questions:**
- Is capital being reinvested in the highest-return opportunities available?
- Should cash remain in the business, be paid out, or be redirected elsewhere?
- Does management understand opportunity cost across the whole capital base?

## 3) Reusable Buffett-style business scoring dimensions from the 1991 letter

A practical framework derived from this letter would score a company on the following dimensions:

### A. Business quality
- Is it a franchise or just a business?
- Does it have pricing power?
- Are substitutes weak or strong?
- Is demand durable?
- Are economics getting stronger, stable, cyclical, or structurally weaker?

### B. Capital intensity
- How much capital is required to maintain and grow earnings?
- Can earnings grow without significant reinvestment?
- Are earnings truly distributable?

### C. Return quality
- Are returns on equity/capital high?
- Are returns achieved without heavy debt?
- Are margins and returns resilient across cycles?

### D. Management quality
- Is management rational, owner-oriented, and disciplined?
- Do incentives reflect the cost of capital?
- Have they avoided leverage, foolish acquisitions, and faddish behavior?

### E. Balance-sheet resilience
- Can the company withstand secular or cyclical stress?
- Is leverage low enough that bad industry conditions do not become fatal?
- Is financial strength itself a competitive advantage?

### F. Valuation logic
- What assumptions about growth, franchise strength, and capital needs are embedded in the price?
- What happens to value if the business shifts from compounding to bob-around economics?
- Is the price sensible relative to intrinsic value?

### G. Portfolio fit
- Is this understandable enough to deserve concentration?
- Does it improve long-term look-through earnings of the portfolio?
- Is it meaningfully better than the alternatives for capital allocation?

## 4) Red flags highlighted by the 1991 letter

The letter implies the following warning signs:

- **Secular erosion disguised as cyclical weakness**
- **Valuation based on outdated assumptions of perpetual franchise economics**
- **Leverage layered onto weakening business models**
- **Managers rewarded without regard to capital employed**
- **Turnaround or projection-driven investment cases**
- **Complex businesses outside the investor’s circle of competence**
- **Accounting choices that hide poor underlying economics**
- **Fast-changing industries where future economics are inferred from the rear-view mirror**
- **Selling or failing to buy due to psychology rather than business analysis**
- **Over-diversification into enterprises about which the investor has little reason for special confidence**

## 5) A reusable checklist for future company analysis

Use these questions when analyzing a stock or business in Buffett’s 1991 style:

### Business economics
1. Is this an economic franchise, a weak franchise, a strong business, or a commodity-like business?
2. Does the company have pricing power?
3. Are customers loyal because of habit, brand, convenience, trust, or lack of substitutes?
4. Are industry economics improving, stable, cyclical, or deteriorating structurally?

### Capital requirements
5. How much additional capital is needed to sustain and grow earnings?
6. Are earnings largely distributable, or mostly spoken for by reinvestment needs?
7. What has been the return on incremental retained earnings?

### Management
8. Is management able and shareholder-oriented?
9. Do incentives penalize wasteful use of capital?
10. Has management shown discipline on leverage, acquisitions, and capital allocation?
11. Could I comfortably own this business with minimal intervention from a parent company?

### Financial strength
12. Is the balance sheet conservative enough for the realities of the industry?
13. Would weaker industry conditions expose excessive leverage?
14. Is financial strength a source of competitive advantage?

### Valuation
15. What assumptions are required to justify the current price?
16. What is the valuation if earnings merely “bob around” rather than compound?
17. Am I paying for enduring economics or for a temporary favorable period?

### Investment fit
18. Is this business understandable enough to fall within my circle of competence?
19. Is it good enough to justify concentration?
20. Will owning it likely improve my portfolio’s long-term look-through earnings?

## 6) Performance logic embedded in the letter

The 1991 letter contains several reusable cause-and-effect relationships:

### Performance logic A: Intrinsic value growth
- If **look-through earnings** compound attractively, **intrinsic value** should compound attractively.
- Reported accounting earnings may lag or distort this reality.

### Performance logic B: Franchise value
- If a business has **pricing power + low capital needs + no close substitute**, it can generate high returns and justify a premium valuation.

### Performance logic C: Valuation compression
- If a business shifts from **durable compounding** to **ordinary bob-around economics**, the valuation multiple should shrink sharply.

### Performance logic D: Incremental capital test
- Growth is valuable only when the **incremental earnings** produced are high relative to the **incremental capital** required.

### Performance logic E: Leverage risk
- Weakening industry economics + leverage = amplified destruction of intrinsic value.

### Performance logic F: Portfolio construction
- Long-term investment success is more likely to come from **patient ownership of a few understandable, superior businesses** than from active trading among mediocre ones.

### Performance logic G: Insurance economics
- Insurance float is an asset only if its **cost** is low enough; float growth without underwriting discipline is not genuine value creation.

## 7) What this letter adds to a Buffett knowledge base

Relative to a generic Buffett framework, the 1991 letter makes several especially important contributions:

1. It gives one of Buffett’s clearest explanations of the **franchise vs. business** distinction.
2. It shows how **valuation depends on assumptions about durability and capital intensity**, not just current earnings.
3. It reinforces **look-through earnings** as the correct owner lens for both companies and portfolios.
4. It demonstrates the importance of **incremental capital efficiency** through the See’s example.
5. It provides a concrete acquisition/investment filter that can be reused almost directly as a screening framework.
6. It emphasizes **omission errors** as a major source of investment underperformance.
7. It reframes insurance analysis around **cost of float** rather than superficial accounting metrics.

---

## 8) Condensed reusable framework statement

A Buffett-style investor analyzing a stock or business through the 1991 lens should ask:

> Is this an understandable business with durable franchise characteristics, strong and shareholder-oriented management, high returns on capital without much debt, low incremental capital requirements, and long-term earnings power that can grow meaningfully? If so, can it be bought at a sensible price that does not assume unrealistic permanence or growth?

And once owned:

> Judge the investment by growth in intrinsic value and look-through earnings, not by short-term market movements or accounting presentation.

---

## 9) One-paragraph working synthesis for downstream use

**Working synthesis:** Prefer understandable, capital-light businesses with durable pricing power, little need for debt, and managements that behave like owners. Value them based on long-term look-through earnings and the incremental capital required to sustain growth. Distinguish carefully between franchises and ordinary businesses, and between cyclical setbacks and secular impairment. Maintain patience, concentrate where knowledge is strong, and remember that some of the biggest mistakes come from failing to act on understandable, high-quality opportunities at sensible prices.
