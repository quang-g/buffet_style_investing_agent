# Berkshire Hathaway 1993 Letter — Reusable Buffett Analysis Framework

Source letter: Berkshire Hathaway Chairman's Letter, 1993.

## Purpose of this extraction

This document extracts the **reusable investment and business-analysis framework** embedded in Buffett's 1993 Berkshire Hathaway shareholder letter. It is **not** a summary of 1993 events. It focuses only on principles that can be reused to evaluate a stock, a business, management, capital allocation, insurance economics, governance, and portfolio construction in a Buffett-like way.

---

# 1) Core valuation lens: intrinsic value, not accounting optics

## Principle
The central object of analysis is **per-share intrinsic value**, not book value, reported earnings, or market price.

## Buffett logic
- **Book value** is an accounting measure of capital invested and retained.
- **Intrinsic value** is the present value of cash that can be taken out of a business over its remaining life.
- Market price can diverge widely from intrinsic value in the short run.
- Over time, market price and intrinsic value tend to converge.

## Reusable implications
- Always translate accounting numbers into economic reality.
- Focus on **per-share** value creation, not total size growth.
- Treat market moves as potentially informative, but not decisive.
- Distinguish between:
  - accounting capital,
  - economic earning power,
  - quoted price.

## Analyst questions
- What is the business worth based on future cash extraction power?
- Is value growing **per share**?
- Are reported metrics understating or overstating economic reality?
- Is the stock's current price implying expectations that exceed plausible intrinsic-value growth?

## Framework rule
**Never let accounting form replace economic substance.**

---

# 2) Business performance ultimately drives stock performance

## Principle
A stock cannot permanently outperform the business that underlies it.

## Buffett logic
- In the short run, price can detach from operating progress.
- In the long run, investment outcome is driven by business earnings performance.
- Market enthusiasm and pessimism are temporary; business economics dominate over time.

## Reusable implications
- When a stock surges far ahead of earnings growth, future returns may compress.
- When business performance exceeds stock performance, valuation may become more attractive.
- Long-term investing should anchor on business compounding, not on recent price action.

## Analyst questions
- Has stock appreciation materially outrun operating progress?
- Has price stagnation masked improving business quality or earnings power?
- What part of expected return comes from business growth vs. multiple change?

## Framework rule
**Model the business first; treat stock returns as a delayed reflection of operating outcomes.**

---

# 3) Use long-term economics, not market volatility, to define risk

## Principle
True investment risk is **not price volatility**. Risk is the possibility of a permanent loss of purchasing power or failure to earn an adequate after-tax real return.

## Buffett logic
Buffett explicitly rejects beta as the core measure of investment risk. He defines the real question as whether an investor's aggregate after-tax receipts over the holding period will preserve and grow purchasing power.

## Buffett's five primary risk factors
1. The certainty with which the long-term economic characteristics of the business can be evaluated.
2. The certainty with which management can be evaluated.
3. The certainty that management will channel rewards to shareholders rather than itself.
4. The purchase price paid.
5. The tax and inflation environment that reduces real after-tax returns.

## Reusable implications
- A volatile stock can be low risk if the business is understandable, durable, and bought cheaply.
- A stable-looking stock can be high risk if the business economics are fragile or the price is excessive.
- Risk assessment must combine business quality, management trust, valuation, and macro frictions.

## Analyst questions
- How predictable are this company's economics five to ten years out?
- How much confidence can I place in management's competence and integrity?
- Does management act like an owner or an extractor?
- Is the purchase price low enough to protect purchasing power?
- How much do inflation and taxes erode the economic return?

## Framework rule
**Risk = probability of impaired purchasing power, not share-price fluctuation.**

---

# 4) Stay inside the circle of competence

## Principle
Only invest where long-term economics can be evaluated with useful confidence.

## Buffett logic
- Many businesses are too hard to understand.
- Some industries are difficult because of the analyst's limitations.
- Others are difficult because the industry itself changes too fast to allow reliable long-term forecasts.
- Rapid technological change often destroys forecastability.

## Reusable implications
- Avoid businesses where future economics depend on predicting fast-moving technology, unstable consumer preference, or shifting industry structure.
- Favor businesses whose demand, competitive position, and economics can be understood without heroic assumptions.

## Analyst questions
- Can I explain in plain language how this business will likely make money in ten years?
- Is industry change slow enough to support durable forecasts?
- Am I relying on prediction, or on observable economic strength?

## Framework rule
**Prefer the obvious winner in an understandable field over the exciting mystery in a changing one.**

---

# 5) Seek durable competitive advantage: the moat framework

## Principle
The best businesses possess enduring competitive strengths that protect returns on capital.

## Buffett logic
Coke and Gillette are highlighted as businesses with:
- dominant market shares,
- powerful brands,
- attractive product attributes,
- strong distribution systems,
- the ability to increase share over time.

These features create a **protective moat around the economic castle**.

## Reusable moat indicators
- Brand power that influences repeat purchase or preference.
- Distribution advantages competitors cannot easily replicate.
- Global or category leadership.
- Customer habit or product embeddedness.
- Pricing power without severe volume damage.
- Ability to maintain or expand share over time.
- Lower exposure to commodity-like competition.

## Warning sign
Commodity-like products often carry structurally weak economics because competition erodes wealth.

## Analyst questions
- What specifically prevents competitors from eroding margins and returns?
- Is the moat based on brand, network, distribution, switching cost, cost advantage, regulation, or habit?
- Has market share been stable or rising?
- Is the product becoming more commodity-like?

## Framework rule
**Prefer businesses whose strength is visible in customer behavior and industry structure, not just in recent margins.**

---

# 6) Management quality has two dimensions: ability and character

## Principle
Management must be judged both on **operating competence** and **shareholder alignment**.

## Buffett logic
Buffett repeatedly separates management evaluation into:
- ability to realize the business's potential,
- wisdom in employing cash flows,
- willingness to direct rewards to shareholders rather than to itself.

He also values managers who can run a business without interference and who deserve autonomy.

## Reusable management criteria
### A. Operating ability
- Has management improved economics or reinforced strength?
- Can management compete well even in difficult industries?
- Does management execute consistently over long periods?

### B. Capital allocation ability
- Does management reinvest incremental cash at high returns?
- Does it avoid diworsification and empire-building?
- Does it repurchase, invest, acquire, or distribute capital rationally?

### C. Character and alignment
- Does management treat shareholders as partners?
- Does it avoid self-enrichment at shareholder expense?
- Does it communicate honestly about goals, mistakes, and economics?

## Analyst questions
- Is management excellent only operationally, or also in capital allocation?
- Are incentives aligned with per-share value creation?
- Does management keep promises and preserve culture?
- Would I trust this team to act properly if no one were watching?

## Framework rule
**Back managers who are both skilled and trustworthy; reject those missing either trait.**

---

# 7) Capital allocation is a separate skill from operations

## Principle
Owning great operating businesses does not eliminate the difficulty of allocating the cash they produce.

## Buffett logic
Buffett distinguishes between:
- operating managers running subsidiaries,
- parent-company capital allocators deploying excess cash.

The first may be excellent; the second still faces uncertainty because great businesses and great managers are hard to find at sensible prices.

## Reusable implications
- Separate evaluation of operating excellence from parent-level capital allocation.
- A holding company deserves a premium only if capital is redeployed intelligently.
- Cash accumulation is not automatically strength; it must be converted into good decisions.

## Analyst questions
- Who controls free cash flow, and how strong is their reinvestment record?
- Has retained capital created at least one dollar of market/intrinsic value per dollar retained?
- Does management resist marginal deals when opportunity set is poor?

## Framework rule
**Judge the allocator independently from the operator.**

---

# 8) Do not begin with industry forecasts or grand strategic visions

## Principle
Investment and acquisition decisions should start with **economic characteristics** and **manager quality**, not top-down visions about which sectors to enter.

## Buffett logic
Buffett explicitly rejects entering businesses because of a corporate master plan. He calls such strategic expansion often harmful to shareholders. Instead, he looks for a favorable combination of:
- desirable business economics,
- desirable managers.

## Reusable implications
- Avoid theme-driven expansion without business-level edge.
- Do not force capital into industries because they are fashionable or strategically adjacent.
- Let opportunity emerge from business quality and price.

## Analyst questions
- Is the investment thesis driven by business economics or by narrative?
- Are we buying because the company is good, or because the sector sounds important?
- Is management pursuing disciplined expansion or prestige expansion?

## Framework rule
**Bottom-up economics beats top-down storytelling.**

---

# 9) Buy wonderful businesses and hold them with owner-like tenacity

## Principle
A small stake in an outstanding business should be held with the same mindset as total ownership.

## Buffett logic
- Businesses that are understandable and durably wonderful are very hard to replace.
- Investors should not sell simply because price has risen or because they have a profit.
- What makes sense in wholly owned subsidiaries also makes sense in stocks.

## Reusable implications
- Selling because of activity bias is often a mistake.
- The hurdle to sell should be high when business quality is exceptional.
- Great assets should be treated as scarce.

## Analyst questions
- If I owned 100% of this business, would I want to sell it now?
- Has the quality deteriorated, or am I merely reacting to price?
- Is there a superior reinvestment opportunity with clearly better economics and valuation?

## Framework rule
**Do not lightly part with a great business.**

---

# 10) Price matters enormously, even for great businesses

## Principle
Quality does not remove valuation discipline.

## Buffett logic
Even a wonderful business can be a poor investment if bought at too high a price. Purchase price is one of the primary determinants of risk and return.

## Reusable implications
- Business quality and valuation must be analyzed together.
- High certainty can justify a fuller price than low-quality or low-predictability businesses.
- But expected return still depends on the relationship between price and future distributable cash.

## Analyst questions
- What growth and durability assumptions are embedded in today's price?
- How much of my upside depends on further multiple expansion?
- Does current price leave room for error?

## Framework rule
**Great business + wrong price can still equal bad investment.**

---

# 11) Prefer internal compounding and tax deferral

## Principle
Long holding periods and internal compounding are extremely powerful because taxes are deferred until realization.

## Buffett logic
A single investment compounding internally at a given rate produces far more after-tax wealth than a sequence of realized gains compounding at the same nominal rate.

## Reusable implications
- High-turnover investing faces a severe tax drag.
- Long duration enhances after-tax compounding.
- Companies that can reinvest internally at high rates are especially valuable.

## Analyst questions
- Can this business retain and reinvest earnings at attractive rates?
- How much return is lost if the thesis requires frequent realization?
- Is management creating a long runway for tax-efficient compounding?

## Framework rule
**Favor businesses and holding periods that maximize tax-deferred internal compounding.**

---

# 12) Use look-through earnings, not just reported earnings

## Principle
Economic ownership matters more than accounting consolidation rules.

## Buffett logic
Buffett's “look-through earnings” framework adds Berkshire's operating earnings to its share of retained operating earnings from major investees, net of hypothetical tax. The idea: retained earnings at investees belong economically to the owner even if GAAP does not report them as current earnings.

## Reusable implications
- For holding companies and investment companies, GAAP earnings may understate true economic earnings.
- Undistributed earnings from strong investees can create intrinsic value even without dividend payout.
- A good analyst should evaluate both reported earnings and look-through economics.

## Analyst questions
- What earnings are being retained on my behalf at subsidiaries, associates, or investees?
- Are those retained earnings likely to create at least equivalent intrinsic value?
- Is accounting presentation obscuring economic progress?

## Framework rule
**Count the earnings you economically own, not only the ones accounting rules recognize immediately.**

---

# 13) Retained earnings are justified only if they create proportional value

## Principle
Retained earnings should translate into at least comparable gains in intrinsic value.

## Buffett logic
Buffett expects investees' retained earnings to produce at least equivalent gains in Berkshire's intrinsic value, and preferably more.

## Reusable implications
- Retention is not automatically good.
- A company should only retain capital if it can deploy it productively.
- Persistent low-return retention is value-destructive.

## Analyst questions
- What has management done historically with retained earnings?
- Has incremental retained capital produced strong per-share value growth?
- Would shareholders be better off if excess capital were distributed?

## Framework rule
**Demand evidence that retained capital earns attractive incremental returns.**

---

# 14) Underwriting economics in insurance must be separated from investment results

## Principle
Insurance analysis requires a distinct framework: evaluate underwriting economics and float economics first, then investment performance separately.

## Buffett logic
- Float is policyholder money held before claims are paid.
- Float is valuable only relative to its cost.
- A combined ratio alone is insufficient, because the value of float depends on prevailing interest rates.
- The transfer price of float to the investment operation should be measured using risk-free long-term rates.

## Reusable insurance framework
### A. Measure float
Approximate float = reserves and unearned premiums minus offsets such as agents' balances and deferred items.

### B. Measure cost of float
Cost of float = underwriting loss / average float.
- Underwriting profit => negative cost of float.
- Underwriting loss => positive cost of float.

### C. Compare cost of float to risk-free rate
- Float is attractive when its cost is below risk-free long-term rates.
- Negative-cost float is especially valuable.

### D. Evaluate investment skill separately
Do not confuse excellent investment returns with good underwriting.

## Analyst questions
- Is the insurer generating float cheaply, at break-even, or expensively?
- Are underwriting profits real or merely a lucky year?
- How does cost of float compare to available risk-free yields?
- Is management disciplined enough to shrink volume if pricing deteriorates?

## Framework rule
**Insurance is attractive only when float is ample and cheaply obtained, preferably at an underwriting profit.**

---

# 15) Distinguish genuine economics from good luck in volatile businesses

## Principle
In catastrophe-exposed businesses, one-year profits can be misleading.

## Buffett logic
Buffett warns against inferring structural attractiveness from a lucky year with few losses. Businesses with low-frequency, high-severity risks may appear wonderful for long stretches before a single event exposes inadequate pricing.

## Reusable implications
- Normalize earnings across cycles and tail events.
- Examine whether pricing truly compensates for uncertain catastrophe exposure.
- Avoid being fooled by smooth reported profits in negatively skewed businesses.

## Analyst questions
- What is the downside in a severe but plausible stress scenario?
- Are current profits simply the absence of bad luck?
- Can the company survive a “lulu” loss without permanent impairment?

## Framework rule
**For tail-risk businesses, analyze distribution of outcomes, not just average reported earnings.**

---

# 16) Scale can be a true moat in selected financial businesses

## Principle
In some businesses, extraordinary capital strength materially improves competitive position.

## Buffett logic
In super-cat reinsurance, Berkshire's enormous capital base allows it to offer limits others cannot safely provide. This scale is not cosmetic; it affects customer trust, capacity, and ability to pay under stress.

## Reusable implications
- Size matters only when it changes economics or customer value.
- In businesses where counterparties value certainty of payment, balance-sheet strength can be a competitive advantage.

## Analyst questions
- Does scale improve customer value, resilience, or pricing power?
- Is balance-sheet strength a real differentiator or just an accounting statistic?
- Do sophisticated customers choose the company precisely because it can perform under stress?

## Framework rule
**Treat scale as a moat only when it changes the customer's economic calculus.**

---

# 17) Capacity discipline matters more than volume growth

## Principle
When pricing deteriorates, the rational response is often to reduce volume.

## Buffett logic
Buffett indicates willingness to accept much lower insurance volume rather than chase underpriced business. Capacity should remain available for exceptional opportunities, not be forced into bad ones.

## Reusable implications
- Growth achieved by accepting poor economics is destructive.
- Discipline is visible when management can tolerate inactivity.
- Cash or unused capacity is valuable optionality.

## Analyst questions
- Does management walk away when returns are unattractive?
- Is market share being protected at the expense of underwriting quality or returns?
- Does the company preserve dry powder for better opportunities?

## Framework rule
**Rational inactivity beats busy value destruction.**

---

# 18) Concentration can reduce risk for a knowledgeable investor

## Principle
For investors who can truly assess business economics, sensible concentration may reduce rather than increase risk.

## Buffett logic
- Diversification is protection against ignorance.
- When an investor understands a handful of businesses deeply and buys them sensibly, concentration can improve results and reduce the chance of mistakes born of mediocrity.
- Over-diversification can push money into weaker ideas.

## Reusable implications
- Portfolio design depends on knowledge level.
- Best ideas should receive more capital when certainty is high and valuation is favorable.
- Position sizing should reflect quality of understanding, not just statistical convention.

## Analyst questions
- Am I diversifying because I lack knowledge or because opportunities are truly comparable?
- Is this new position better than adding to my top holdings?
- Do I understand this business well enough to own it meaningfully?

## Framework rule
**For the know-something investor, concentration in best ideas is often more rational than broad mediocrity.**

---

# 19) Diversification is appropriate in two specific cases

## Principle
Wide diversification is useful when either the investor lacks business knowledge or the strategy relies on many probabilistic bets.

## Buffett logic
Two cases justify diversification:
1. Strategies like arbitrage or venture-style investing, where each idea has meaningful risk and success comes from probability-weighted baskets.
2. Investors who do not understand specific business economics and therefore should own a broad index over time.

## Reusable implications
- Diversification is a tool, not a doctrine.
- The right portfolio structure depends on the nature of the edge.

## Analyst questions
- Is my edge in individual business understanding or only in market participation?
- Is this a high-uncertainty basket strategy requiring many independent bets?

## Framework rule
**Diversify when ignorance or probabilistic strategy requires it; otherwise concentrate intelligently.**

---

# 20) Acquisition framework: buy businesses with both economic quality and managerial quality

## Principle
An acquisition should join a strong business with managers Berkshire wants to partner with.

## Buffett logic
The Dexter discussion shows the acquisition template:
- strong business economics,
- outstanding management,
- little need for operational fixing,
- cultural continuity,
- trust in incumbent operators,
- autonomy after purchase.

## Reusable acquisition criteria
- Understandable business economics.
- Durable competitiveness.
- Honest and able management.
- Attractive long-term returns on capital.
- Cultural compatibility.
- Deal structure that respects seller motivations.
- Avoidance of post-deal bureaucracy.

## Analyst questions
- Are we buying a good business or a “turnaround project” dressed as opportunity?
- Are the incumbent managers exceptional enough to leave in charge?
- Does the seller care about legacy, tax efficiency, autonomy, or permanence?
- Can the acquirer truly be a better home than alternatives?

## Framework rule
**The ideal acquisition is a business you don't need to fix, run by people you don't need to replace.**

---

# 21) Owner orientation is a strategic advantage in dealmaking

## Principle
A buyer can gain access to attractive private businesses by offering sellers partnership, permanence, trust, and autonomy rather than just cash.

## Buffett logic
Buffett presents Berkshire as attractive to sellers because it offers:
- no tax-triggering cash-out in stock deals,
- diversification for concentrated owners,
- liquidity over time,
- partnership treatment,
- no options or special insider economics for Buffett,
- durable culture,
- freedom for managers to keep running the business.

## Reusable implications
- Competitive advantage in acquisitions can come from structure and reputation, not only price.
- Seller psychology matters.
- A credible permanent home can win deals unavailable to financial buyers.

## Analyst questions
- What does the seller truly want besides maximum headline price?
- Does the buyer have a reputation for keeping promises and preserving autonomy?
- Is alignment real or marketing language?

## Framework rule
**In acquisitions, trust and structure can be as important as price.**

---

# 22) Governance analysis must start with ownership structure

## Principle
Corporate governance cannot be assessed abstractly; it must be analyzed in the context of who controls the company.

## Buffett logic
Buffett outlines three governance situations:
1. No controlling shareholder.
2. Controlling owner is also manager.
3. Controlling owner is not manager.

The effectiveness of outside directors differs materially in each case.

## Reusable governance framework
### Case 1: No controlling owner
- Directors must act for the absentee owner.
- They should replace weak management and restrain overreach.
- Board should be small, mostly outside, owner-oriented, and willing to evaluate the CEO independently.

### Case 2: Controlling owner-manager
- Board influence is limited to persuasion.
- If serious issues persist, outside directors may need to resign.

### Case 3: Controlling non-manager owner
- Often the best arrangement.
- Outside directors can report directly to an interested owner who can act decisively.

## Reusable director-quality criteria
- Business savvy.
- Genuine interest in the role.
- Owner orientation.
- Independence of thought.
- Willingness to act on serious issues.

## Analyst questions
- Which governance case applies here?
- Can directors actually influence outcomes, or only advise?
- Are directors selected for usefulness or for prestige?
- Is the CEO evaluated by independent-minded owners/directors?

## Framework rule
**Governance quality depends not only on rules, but on who has power and whether that power is aligned.**

---

# 23) Shareholder alignment requires equitable economic treatment

## Principle
Managers should share the same gain-loss equation as owners.

## Buffett logic
Buffett emphasizes that his own economic outcome parallels other shareholders because he has a large long-term stake and receives neither stock options nor special insider advantages.

## Reusable implications
- Alignment improves when managers own meaningful common equity purchased or held on similar terms to owners.
- Option-heavy or asymmetric pay structures can distort incentives.

## Analyst questions
- Does management win only when shareholders win?
- Are insiders insulated from downside?
- Are incentive structures simple and owner-like?

## Framework rule
**Prefer management teams whose economics resemble those of outside owners.**

---

# 24) Honest communication is part of managerial quality

## Principle
A high-quality manager reports to owners as they would want reports if positions were reversed.

## Buffett logic
Buffett states that Berkshire aims to provide the financial information he and Charlie would want as owners. He also sets explicit targets in advance rather than redefining success later.

## Reusable implications
- Candor is a real asset.
- Good management explains economics, not just compliance figures.
- Pre-committing to targets is superior to retroactive self-congratulation.

## Analyst questions
- Does management discuss economic reality or hide behind adjusted labels?
- Are goals set before outcomes are known?
- Does the company explain both successes and limitations plainly?

## Framework rule
**Treat candor as evidence of both intelligence and integrity.**

---

# 25) Use explicit performance targets, but do not force action to meet them

## Principle
Goals are useful only if they sharpen discipline rather than encourage marginal decisions.

## Buffett logic
Buffett states a long-term intrinsic-value growth goal, but also warns against doing something mediocre merely because Berkshire has excess cash.

## Reusable implications
- Targets should guide judgment, not override it.
- Pressure to deploy capital can lower standards.
- Opportunity set matters as much as ambition.

## Analyst questions
- Is management sacrificing quality to hit numeric targets?
- Are idle cash and patience being used intelligently?
- Does the company distinguish between activity and value creation?

## Framework rule
**Ambition is useful; forced action is dangerous.**

---

# 26) Economic goodwill can exceed book value by a wide margin

## Principle
Some businesses are worth far more than book value because their earning power, float, brand, or moat creates economic value not captured by accounting net worth.

## Buffett logic
Buffett notes that Berkshire's insurance business has intrinsic value well above book value. More generally, book value is often a poor proxy for economics.

## Reusable implications
- Asset-light, high-return businesses often look expensive on book but cheap on earning power.
- Financial businesses with attractive float economics may deserve valuations above stated equity.

## Analyst questions
- What value-creating assets or structures are absent from book value?
- Is the business generating returns on tangible capital that imply hidden goodwill?
- Is accounting equity understating franchise strength?

## Framework rule
**Treat book value as input, not verdict.**

---

# 27) Trust can substitute for friction in exceptional cases

## Principle
In rare situations, extraordinary integrity can lower transaction costs and due-diligence burdens.

## Buffett logic
Buffett highlights buying Nebraska Furniture Mart without audited statements or warranties because Mrs. B's word was enough.

## Reusable implications
- Character has measurable economic value.
- This principle applies only rarely and only with exceptional evidence.
- Trust can speed deals, preserve goodwill, and reduce contractual complexity.

## Analyst questions
- Is management's reputation for honesty deep enough to be economically meaningful?
- Has the company or owner earned unusual trust through decades of behavior?

## Framework rule
**Integrity is not soft; in the right hands, it is an economic asset.**

---

# 28) A practical Buffett-style checklist derived from the 1993 letter

## A. Business quality
- Is the business understandable?
- Are long-term economics predictable?
- Does it have a durable moat?
- Is competition likely to erode returns?
- Does it avoid rapid technological obsolescence?

## B. Management
- Is management able?
- Is management honest?
- Is capital allocation intelligent?
- Are managers owner-oriented?
- Will management share rewards with shareholders fairly?

## C. Financial/economic quality
- What is intrinsic value per share?
- Are retained earnings earning attractive returns?
- Do reported earnings understate or overstate look-through economics?
- Is the balance sheet supportive of resilience and opportunity?

## D. Valuation
- What price am I paying relative to intrinsic value?
- What assumptions are embedded in the price?
- Is there a margin of safety?

## E. Risk
- What could permanently impair purchasing power?
- How certain are the long-term economics?
- What role do taxes and inflation play?
- Am I mistaking volatility for risk?

## F. Portfolio fit
- Is this among my best-understood ideas?
- Should this be concentrated or merely observed?
- Am I adding because it is excellent, or because I want activity?

## G. Special case: insurance
- How much float exists?
- What is the cost of float?
- Is underwriting truly sound or just temporarily lucky?
- How does cost of float compare with risk-free rates?
- Is pricing discipline intact?

# 29) Condensed decision rules for a Buffett-like analyst

1. **Value the business, not the ticker.**
2. **Measure success per share.**
3. **Use intrinsic value as the anchor.**
4. **Ignore short-run market voting; focus on long-run weighing.**
5. **Define risk as permanent purchasing-power loss.**
6. **Stay inside understandable, durable businesses.**
7. **Prefer wide-moat companies over commodity businesses.**
8. **Demand both management competence and integrity.**
9. **Judge capital allocation separately from operations.**
10. **Retained earnings must create at least equivalent value.**
11. **Favor long-duration internal compounding and tax deferral.**
12. **Hold exceptional businesses with owner-like patience.**
13. **Concentrate when knowledge is strong; diversify when it is not.**
14. **In insurance, evaluate float economics explicitly.**
15. **Do not confuse good luck with good economics.**
16. **Be willing to do nothing when prices are poor.**
17. **Treat governance through the lens of control and incentives.**
18. **Seek alignment where managers share owners' economic fate.**
19. **Prefer candid reporting over cosmetic performance narratives.**
20. **Avoid forced action driven by cash, fashion, or strategic theater.**

# 30) Most reusable concepts from the 1993 letter

If this letter were converted into a knowledge base for stock analysis, its highest-value reusable concepts would be:

- Intrinsic value vs. book value vs. market price
- Long-run business performance as the source of stock returns
- Risk as purchasing-power impairment rather than volatility
- The five-factor business-risk framework
- Circle of competence
- Moat analysis via brand, distribution, and market share durability
- Management evaluation: ability, capital allocation, and integrity
- Look-through earnings
- Retained-earnings productivity
- Tax-efficient compounding
- Insurance float economics and cost-of-float analysis
- Concentration vs. diversification based on knowledge
- Governance analysis based on ownership/control structure
- Owner orientation as a differentiator in both management and acquisitions

# Final extraction summary

The 1993 letter contributes a highly reusable Buffett framework centered on the following master idea:

> **A superior investment is a sensibly priced interest in an understandable business with durable competitive advantages, trustworthy and capable management, intelligent capital allocation, and economics that can compound per-share intrinsic value over long periods with limited risk of permanent purchasing-power loss.**

Everything else in the letter largely supports, sharpens, or operationalizes that idea.
