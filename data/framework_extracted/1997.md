## Purpose of this extraction

This document extracts the **reusable investment and business-analysis framework** embedded in Berkshire Hathaway's 1997 shareholder letter. It does **not** aim to summarize the year. Instead, it converts Buffett's comments into a set of durable principles, evaluation criteria, mental models, diagnostic questions, and performance logic that can be reused to analyze stocks, companies, acquisitions, insurance businesses, and capital allocation decisions.

---

## 1) Core measurement logic: judge performance by intrinsic value, not surface results

### Principle
The ultimate scorecard is **growth in per-share intrinsic business value**, not reported earnings, market excitement, or even book value by itself.

### Reusable logic
- Book value can be a **rough tracking proxy** for intrinsic value, but it is not the destination.
- Reported earnings can materially **understate economic reality** when accounting rules omit the investor's share of retained earnings at investees.
- Performance must be assessed **per share**, not in aggregate, because capital raises, share issuance, repurchases, and retained earnings all affect owner economics.

### Buffett-style evaluation questions
- What is the best estimate of **per-share intrinsic value**?
- Is book value a reasonable proxy here, or does it materially misstate economics?
- What earnings belong economically to owners but are not fully visible in GAAP/IFRS figures?
- Is intrinsic value compounding at an acceptable rate **per share**?

### Performance logic
A business is attractive when:
- per-share intrinsic value grows consistently,
- the gap between accounting earnings and economic earnings is understood,
- management allocates retained capital in ways that raise future per-share value.

## 2) Relative performance logic: compare against a rational baseline, not absolute applause

### Principle
In a strong market, high returns alone prove little. Performance must be judged **against an appropriate alternative**, not against raw gains.

### Reusable logic
- A manager should compare results against a passive benchmark or obvious alternative.
- Outperformance should be evaluated **after frictional disadvantages**, such as taxes, structural costs, or permanent handicaps.
- Temporary outperformance in a euphoric market is less meaningful than durable edge over time.

### Buffett-style evaluation questions
- What is the correct baseline: index, peer set, bond yield, replacement cost, or cost of funds?
- Did management outperform because of skill, or because the environment lifted nearly everything?
- Does the business face structural handicaps that must be overcome to justify active management?
- Over a full cycle, is there evidence of a **repeatable edge**?

### Performance logic
Good performance is not “the stock went up.” Good performance is:
- beating the rational alternative over time,
- after taxes, costs, and capital structure realities,
- without relying on favorable market weather.

## 3) Two-part valuation framework: separate investments from operating businesses

### Principle
To value a complex conglomerate or holding company, split it into:
1. **investment assets**, and
2. **operating earnings power**.

### Reusable logic
Buffett isolates two key components:
- per-share investments (including cash), and
- per-share pre-tax earnings from operating businesses, excluding investment income.

This prevents analysts from:
- valuing the company only as an investment portfolio,
- ignoring the economics of wholly owned operating businesses,
- double-counting investment income.

### Buffett-style evaluation questions
- What portion of value comes from **marketable assets**?
- What portion comes from **owned operating businesses**?
- Are corporate costs properly assigned when evaluating segment economics?
- Is the market valuing one component while ignoring the other?

### Performance logic
A business deserves higher value when:
- both asset value and operating earnings power grow,
- the two components reinforce each other,
- the market underappreciates the operating side because it is overshadowed by investment assets.

## 4) Scale law: success reduces the opportunity set

### Principle
As capital base grows, maintaining excess returns becomes harder.

### Reusable logic
- Small pools of capital can exploit opportunities too tiny for large institutions.
- Size reduces flexibility and makes extraordinary compounding less likely.
- Future returns should be judged against the **current capital base**, not against earlier, smaller eras.

### Buffett-style evaluation questions
- How large is the capital base relative to the available opportunity set?
- Does the company need very large deals just to move the needle?
- Are historical returns inflated by a period when the business was much smaller?
- Is management honest about the declining ability to outperform at scale?

### Performance logic
Large size is not automatically bad, but it lowers the odds of exceptional returns unless the company has:
- a very large reinvestment runway,
- structurally advantaged businesses,
- unusually strong capital allocation discipline.

## 5) Opportunity-cost discipline: wait for the fat pitch

### Principle
Investment inactivity is often superior to forced action. The right comparison is not “invest vs. do nothing,” but “this opportunity vs. the best available alternative.”

### Reusable logic
- When great businesses are not available at sensible prices, hold high-quality short-term instruments.
- Patience is a real asset.
- The inability to predict when better opportunities will appear does not justify accepting poor expected returns now.

### Mental model
**Ted Williams strike zone**:
- Great investors define their sweet spot.
- Swinging at mediocre pitches locks in mediocre returns.
- There is no penalty for not swinging at marginal ideas.

### Buffett-style evaluation questions
- Is this opportunity clearly inside the investor's “best cell”?
- What prospective return is available at today's price?
- Am I acting because the opportunity is excellent, or because cash feels uncomfortable?
- What is the cost of waiting?

### Performance logic
Superior long-term investing often comes from:
- selectivity,
- inactivity during overpriced markets,
- willingness to hold liquidity until odds become favorable.

## 6) Preferred target: wonderful business + sensible price

### Principle
The favored commitment is a **well-run, understandable business with fine economics, bought at a sensible price**.

### Reusable acquisition/investment criteria
A target should ideally be:
- understandable,
- run by outstanding and trustworthy people,
- economically strong,
- priced to allow attractive forward returns.

### Buffett-style evaluation questions
- Is the business understandable enough to forecast with reasonable confidence?
- Are economics structurally attractive or merely cyclically strong?
- Is management both able and trustworthy?
- Does the purchase price leave room for a satisfactory return without heroic assumptions?

### Performance logic
When Buffett finds this type of opportunity, he believes profit is not merely probable but highly likely; the main uncertainty is timing, not eventual outcome.

## 7) Alternative commitments: distinguish “know” from “think”

### Principle
Some non-core or macro-based investments may be profitable, but they lack the near-certainty attached to a wonderful business bought right.

### Reusable logic
- Separate investments into two buckets:
  - **high-confidence business investments**: “we know we are going to make money, timing aside”
  - **probabilistic alternative commitments**: “we think we are going to make money, but losses are possible, sometimes large”
- Acknowledge different confidence levels explicitly.

### Buffett-style evaluation questions
- Is this an investment in business value, or a probabilistic market/macro bet?
- What is my actual edge?
- What conditions would make me wrong?
- Could losses be large even if the thesis sounded reasonable?

### Performance logic
Alternative commitments can be justified when odds are favorable, but they should not be confused with the highest-quality business investments.

## 8) Market fluctuation framework: lower prices help net buyers

### Principle
For a long-term net saver, declining asset prices are often good news.

### Reusable logic
- If an investor will be a **net buyer** of stocks over time, lower prices improve future returns.
- This is even more true when the underlying entity retains earnings and redeploys capital on behalf of owners.
- Share repurchases create more value when done at lower prices.

### Mental models
- **Hamburger analogy**: consumers should prefer lower prices for things they will keep buying.
- **Disinvestor vs. investor distinction**: falling markets hurt near-term sellers but help long-term buyers.

### Buffett-style evaluation questions
- Am I a net buyer or a net seller over the relevant horizon?
- Does the business itself act as a saver by retaining earnings and redeploying capital?
- Are repurchases being made at prices that meaningfully increase per-share ownership?

### Performance logic
Market declines can improve owner outcomes when:
- the owner is accumulating,
- the company is repurchasing shares intelligently,
- retained earnings are being invested at better prices.

---

## 9) Capital allocation under high prices: expected return matters more than activity

### Principle
When asset prices are high, fresh capital earns less. The investor should focus on **prospective earnings yield and forward return**, not on staying busy.

### Reusable logic
- High prices in both stocks and businesses reduce future returns.
- Lack of market opinion is compatible with valuation discipline.
- One can refuse to predict short-term market direction while still judging expected return as unattractive.

### Buffett-style evaluation questions
- What prospective earnings or cash yield am I locking in at this price?
- Am I confusing “market may keep rising” with “investment is attractive today”?
- Is the opportunity set genuinely poor, even if current holdings remain excellent?

### Performance logic
Wise capital allocation can mean:
- praising what is already owned,
- while simultaneously disliking the return on new commitments at current prices.

---

## 10) Insurance framework: float is a funding source whose value depends on cost

### Principle
Insurance float is valuable only when its **cost** is below alternative funding costs; it is extraordinary when cost is negative.

### Reusable logic
- Float is money held but not owned.
- The economics of float are determined by underwriting outcome.
- Underwriting loss = cost of float.
- Underwriting profit = negative cost of float.
- A liability can be economically better than equity if it is durable and low-cost enough.

### Buffett-style evaluation questions
- How large is float relative to premium volume and equity?
- What has been the long-term cost of float?
- Is float growing? Is it durable?
- Would the company be better off borrowing conventionally instead?
- Is management conservative in reserving?

### Performance logic
Insurance operations are attractive when:
- float is large and durable,
- underwriting is disciplined,
- long-term cost of float is below market funding rates,
- management values reserve conservatism over reported earnings smoothness.

---

## 11) Insurance accounting skepticism: reserve estimates can distort reality

### Principle
Insurance accounting requires skepticism because reserve estimates are highly judgmental.

### Reusable logic
- Underwriting results can be materially distorted by reserve assumptions.
- Even audited numbers may not fully protect investors from optimistic reserving.
- Conservative reserving is a mark of quality.

### Buffett-style evaluation questions
- Are reserve assumptions conservative, neutral, or promotional?
- Has management built credibility by tolerating short-term understatement rather than optimistic booking?
- Are underwriting profits supported by cash economics, or by reserve releases and estimates?

### Performance logic
An insurer deserves trust when management behaves as if future surprises in insurance are more likely to be adverse than favorable.

---

## 12) Catastrophe insurance logic: infrequent events can conceal severe mispricing

### Principle
Rare-event businesses must be evaluated over long horizons; years of profit can coexist with fundamentally bad pricing.

### Reusable logic
- Catastrophe risk is inherently lumpy.
- A long run of good results does not prove underwriting skill.
- Small probabilities with massive losses can fool both investors and managers.
- Absence of losses is not evidence of adequate pricing.

### Mental model
**Dice analogy**:
- A risk can look profitable for years while being mathematically ruinous.
- Low-frequency, high-severity exposures require expected-value discipline, not recent-track-record comfort.

### Buffett-style evaluation questions
- Is the business exposed to low-frequency, high-severity losses?
- Could many profitable years simply reflect luck?
- Is pricing sufficient for worst-case or fat-tail exposure?
- Does management walk away when prices deteriorate?

### Performance logic
In volatile underwriting lines, superior operators show:
- willingness to write less when prices fall,
- capacity to survive extreme events,
- explicit recognition that a terrible year is inevitable eventually.

## 13) Risk capacity + pricing discipline = durable edge in insurance

### Principle
In insurance, financial strength matters only if paired with pricing discipline.

### Reusable logic
- Large capital and superior claims-paying ability create competitive advantage.
- But the advantage is destroyed if management writes inadequately priced business just to grow.
- The best underwriter can both imagine unusual deals and refuse bad ones.

### Buffett-style evaluation questions
- Does the company have unusual capacity to bear risk?
- Is management disciplined enough to leave business unwritten when prices are wrong?
- Is “market leadership” based on strength and judgment, or on aggressiveness?

### Performance logic
The best insurer is not the one writing the most premium; it is the one writing business whose long-term expected return is attractive relative to risk.

---

## 14) Consumer franchise insurance framework: growth + seasoned profitability

### Principle
In customer-acquisition businesses, reported current profit can understate franchise quality if acquisition costs are expensed upfront. Measure both growth and mature-cohort profitability.

### Reusable logic from GEICO
Buffett evaluates GEICO with two primary metrics:
1. net increase in voluntary policies,
2. profitability of seasoned business.

This separates:
- franchise expansion economics,
- from temporarily depressed profits caused by acquisition spending.

### Buffett-style evaluation questions
- What is the unit economics of mature cohorts?
- Are new customers profitable after seasoning?
- Is growth being bought recklessly, or does it flow from structural cost advantage?
- Are price cuts or marketing spend strengthening long-term franchise position?

### Performance logic
A consumer finance/insurance franchise is especially attractive when:
- it has a structural cost advantage,
- it can pass some of that advantage to customers,
- growth accelerates without underwriting deterioration,
- mature cohorts remain strongly profitable.

## 15) Low-cost producer advantage: competition can strengthen the best operator

### Principle
In commoditized or heavily competed markets, the low-cost operator often benefits over time from intense competition.

### Reusable logic
- When competition intensifies, margins compress.
- High-cost operators suffer most.
- Low-cost leaders can cut price, keep customers, and widen long-term relative advantage.

### Buffett-style evaluation questions
- Is the company the low-cost producer?
- Can it pass savings to customers and still earn acceptable returns?
- Would a tougher market weaken weaker rivals more than this business?

### Performance logic
A tough market is not uniformly bad. It can reinforce the moat of a structurally advantaged operator.

---

## 16) Compensation design framework: pay for business performance, not stock price theater

### Principle
Compensation should track variables that managers directly influence, not random stock market movements.

### Reusable logic
- Tie incentives to unit performance.
- Use metrics tied to owner value creation.
- Avoid lottery-ticket compensation structures disconnected from actual operating achievement.

### Buffett-style evaluation questions
- Are managers paid for controllable performance variables?
- Do incentives reward growth with profitability, not just volume?
- Is compensation aligned with owner economics at the subsidiary/unit level?

### Performance logic
Good compensation systems:
- reward real operating results,
- avoid windfalls from market re-rating,
- attract managers motivated by achievement, not promotional optics.

## 17) Look-through earnings framework: retained earnings of investees matter

### Principle
For minority investments in strong businesses, the investor's economic earnings include both received dividends and the investor's share of retained earnings.

### Reusable logic
- Reported dividends may represent only a small fraction of the owner's economic claim on investee earnings.
- Retained earnings are valuable when the investee can reinvest them at high returns.
- A better estimate of economic earnings is:
  - reported operating earnings,
  - plus share of retained operating earnings at major investees,
  - less the tax that would have applied if those earnings had been distributed.

### Buffett-style evaluation questions
- What are the investees actually earning on my behalf?
- How much of that is undistributed and therefore hidden from reported earnings?
- Are retained earnings being reinvested at high incremental returns?
- Should I prefer retention over dividends in this case?

### Performance logic
Minority holdings in great businesses become more valuable when management retains earnings and compounds them well. Apparent low current yield can coexist with high economic return.

---

## 18) Earnings retention test: retained earnings must create at least one dollar of value per dollar retained

### Principle
Retained earnings are desirable only when management can deploy them at attractive rates.

### Reusable logic
- Do not treat retained earnings as automatically beneficial.
- Their value depends on what management does with them.
- Strong reinvestment economics justify low payout ratios.

### Buffett-style evaluation questions
- On each retained dollar, how much additional intrinsic value is management creating?
- Are returns on incremental capital still strong, or only historical returns on legacy assets?
- Is management retaining because it has opportunity, or because it dislikes distributing capital?

### Performance logic
A business deserves trust in capital retention when it can consistently convert retained capital into higher future owner earnings.

---

## 19) Acquisition framework: buy understandable businesses with excellent economics and outstanding people

### Principle
The best acquisition targets are simple to understand, strong economically, and run by exceptional people whose character reduces friction.

### Reusable logic
Buffett explicitly says the 1997 acquisitions met his criteria because they were:
- understandable,
- possessed excellent economics,
- run by outstanding people.

He also shows a preference for:
- honest operators,
- decentralized management,
- minimal post-deal meddling,
- trust-based transactions where possible.

### Buffett-style evaluation questions
- Is the business inside the circle of competence?
- Are economics proven and attractive?
- Are the people both talented and trustworthy?
- Can this company continue thriving without integration theatrics?

### Performance logic
The ideal acquisition requires little reinvention after purchase; it should already be a strong business with strong people.

---

## 20) Character as an economic variable

### Principle
Integrity is not a soft variable; it reduces transaction cost, oversight burden, and post-acquisition risk.

### Reusable logic
- Buffett repeatedly emphasizes trust and owner-orientation.
- Character allows faster deals, lighter contracts, and more durable partnerships.
- Generosity toward employees and fairness in conduct are signals of managerial quality.

### Buffett-style evaluation questions
- Would I want to partner with these people without elaborate protections?
- Do they behave fairly when they are not forced to?
- Is their owner orientation visible in action, not just language?

### Performance logic
High-character managers improve capital allocation outcomes because they reduce agency costs and preserve culture.

---

## 21) Acquisition currency framework: issuing undervalued stock is expensive

### Principle
Paying with stock is economically equivalent to selling part of your business. If your stock is undervalued, stock-financed acquisitions can destroy owner value.

### Reusable logic
- Issuing shares dilutes owners in every business already owned.
- Even buying a good business can be a bad deal if the currency used is too valuable.
- Paying cash is often preferable when the acquirer's own shares represent extraordinary underlying assets.

### Mental model
**Do not trade a .380 hitter for a .350 hitter.**

### Buffett-style evaluation questions
- What exactly are owners giving up by issuing shares?
- Is our stock overvalued, fairly valued, or undervalued relative to the target?
- Would existing owners be better off keeping their stake in current businesses rather than exchanging part of it for the target?

### Performance logic
A good acquisition requires evaluating both:
- the value of the target,
- and the value of the consideration being paid.

## 22) Takeover premium test: premiums require either relative overvaluation or real synergy

### Principle
Paying a premium in an acquisition is justified only if:
1. the buyer's stock is overvalued relative to the seller, or
2. genuine synergies create additional combined earnings.

### Reusable logic
- If neither condition exists, the premium comes out of buyer-owner pockets.
- Claimed synergies deserve skepticism, especially in decentralized acquisitions where operations remain largely unchanged.
- Advisory models often rationalize management desire rather than reveal truth.

### Buffett-style evaluation questions
- Why does the premium make sense economically?
- Is the buyer using expensive stock as currency?
- Are synergies concrete, measurable, and believable?
- Would the combined companies truly earn more than separately?

### Performance logic
A rational acquirer must ground takeover price in either valuation arbitrage or credible incremental economics.

## 23) Accounting realism: option expense and acquisition accounting can hide true economics

### Principle
Accounting convention can overstate or distort true earning power.

### Reusable logic
- Option grants are a real compensation cost even if accounting treatment minimizes them.
- Purchase accounting can obscure operating comparability across subsidiaries.
- Investors should restate economics into forms that better mirror business reality.

### Buffett-style evaluation questions
- What expenses are economically real but lightly reflected in accounting earnings?
- Are acquisition-related accounting conventions masking operating performance?
- What adjusted presentation would help an owner think more clearly?

### Performance logic
Better analysis comes from reconstructing owner economics, not merely repeating reported figures.

## 24) Subsidiary evaluation: judge earnings together with capital demands

### Principle
A subsidiary that remits all earnings to the parent may be economically superior to a peer with similar earnings that must retain large capital to grow.

### Reusable logic
- Public-company peers often show results boosted by retained earnings kept inside the business.
- A Berkshire-style subsidiary may look similar on earnings but superior on owner utility because it distributes all earnings upstream.
- Upstreamed cash has value if the parent reallocates it better than the subsidiary could.

### Buffett-style evaluation questions
- How much capital must this business retain to sustain and grow earnings?
- Can it distribute most earnings without harming the franchise?
- Where can retained capital earn the highest incremental return: here or elsewhere?

### Performance logic
A “cash-generating” subsidiary is especially valuable when it needs little incremental capital and its remitted cash can be reinvested at attractive rates elsewhere.

## 25) Management quality framework: focus, rationality, and owner orientation matter more than charisma

### Principle
Outstanding managers combine ability, focus, urgency, and owner-mindedness.

### Reusable traits Buffett highlights
- clear strategic vision,
- ability to translate cost advantage into growth,
- discipline in pricing and underwriting,
- joy in achievement rather than dependence on pay,
- preparation for succession,
- willingness to act decisively.

### Buffett-style evaluation questions
- Does management know exactly where it is going and why?
- Does it act with urgency?
- Does it communicate like owners?
- Has it built systems that survive beyond one personality?

### Performance logic
Superior businesses often look ordinary in financial statements until exceptional management compounds their latent advantages.

## 26) Succession as part of business quality

### Principle
A great business prepares for seamless succession before it becomes urgent.

### Reusable logic
- Succession planning protects momentum and reduces key-person risk.
- Management quality includes institutional continuity, not just current brilliance.

### Buffett-style evaluation questions
- Is the business dependent on one irreplaceable leader?
- Has succession been prepared rationally?
- Would strategic momentum continue through transition?

### Performance logic
The moat is stronger when managerial excellence is being institutionalized rather than merely admired.

## 27) Market valuation framework: stocks are not automatically overvalued because prices are high

### Principle
Overall market valuation depends on the relationship between:
- interest rates,
- returns on equity/business profitability,
- and price.

### Reusable logic
- Lower interest rates can justify higher equity values.
- High returns on equity can also justify higher prices.
- But elevated prices reduce margin of safety even when not obviously irrational.

### Buffett-style evaluation questions
- What are long-term interest rates?
- Are corporate returns on equity sustainably high or unusually elevated?
- How much margin of safety remains at current prices?
- Am I relying on favorable conditions persisting indefinitely?

### Performance logic
A market can be defensible on valuation grounds while still offering thinner future returns because margin of safety has narrowed.

## 28) Margin of safety remains central even in a changed environment

### Principle
High business quality and lower rates do not eliminate the need for margin of safety.

### Reusable logic
- Better businesses and cheaper money may justify higher valuations than in the past.
- But shrinking margin of safety increases vulnerability to disappointment.

### Buffett-style evaluation questions
- How much error can this valuation tolerate?
- If profitability normalizes or rates rise, what happens to value?
- Am I paying a price that leaves little room for being wrong?

### Performance logic
The less margin of safety, the more the investment outcome depends on optimistic conditions persisting.

---

## 29) Public stock selection framework: favor dominant franchises with reinvestment opportunities and shareholder-friendly economics

### Principle
Buffett's major holdings reflect a preference for companies with:
- strong competitive positions,
- high returns on capital,
- reinvestment opportunity,
- capable management,
- and beneficial share repurchase behavior.

### Reusable stock-selection questions
- Does the business have durable market dominance or a brand/franchise moat?
- Can it reinvest retained earnings at high rates?
- Does management allocate capital intelligently, including repurchases?
- Is long-term owner value likely to grow materially even without multiple expansion?

### Performance logic
Great common-stock investments often resemble partial ownership of wonderful businesses run by rational managers.

---

## 30) Repurchase framework: buybacks are only as good as the price paid

### Principle
Share repurchases become more valuable as share price falls relative to intrinsic value.

### Reusable logic
- Repurchases at low prices increase owner percentage more efficiently.
- Repurchases at high prices may do much less for continuing shareholders.

### Buffett-style evaluation questions
- Is the company repurchasing below intrinsic value?
- How much does each dollar spent actually increase continuing owners' share of future earnings?

### Performance logic
A repurchase program should be judged as a capital allocation decision, not as a symbolic signal.

---

## 31) Error-management framework: admit mistakes by category, not just by outcome

### Principle
Buffett openly distinguishes between:
- errors of purchase,
- errors of sale,
- errors of financing/currency,
- and errors of pricing assumptions.

### Reusable logic
Learning improves when mistakes are classified by mechanism.

### Buffett-style evaluation questions
- Was the mistake in business quality assessment, price paid, financing method, or thesis durability?
- Was the error analytical, behavioral, or structural?
- Did the investment later work only because circumstances improved unexpectedly?

### Performance logic
A good investment process requires postmortems that focus on decision quality, not only end results.

---

## 32) Circle of competence + informed insight

### Principle
Edge often comes from combining a known circle of competence with insights from truly knowledgeable operators.

### Reusable logic
- Buffett does not pretend omniscience.
- He improves judgments by learning from people with genuine industry understanding.
- But he still filters that input through economic logic.

### Buffett-style evaluation questions
- Do I genuinely understand this business?
- If not, who understands the economics firsthand?
- Am I relying on promotional experts, or on people with real operating knowledge and aligned incentives?

### Performance logic
Good investing often requires disciplined humility: know enough to judge, and know when to learn from practitioners.

---

## 33) Promotional skepticism: incentives distort expert opinion

### Principle
Where fees are earned upfront and downside is borne by others, expert reassurance deserves suspicion.

### Reusable logic
- Promoters, advisors, and paid experts may rationalize risk transfer.
- The person making the prediction is often not bearing the economic consequence.

### Buffett-style evaluation questions
- Who gets paid regardless of whether the forecast is right?
- Who actually bears the downside?
- Are the loudest experts economically aligned with the recommendation?

### Performance logic
The reliability of any forecast rises when the forecaster has real downside exposure.

## 34) Practical reusable checklist

Use this as a Buffett-style first-pass screen for a stock, business, or acquisition:

### Business quality
- Is the business understandable?
- Does it have durable favorable economics?
- Is there a moat: cost advantage, brand, network, regulatory position, scale, switching costs, or habit?
- Are returns on incremental capital attractive?

### Management quality
- Are managers able, honest, and owner-oriented?
- Do they communicate candidly?
- Are they rational in capital allocation?
- Is succession addressed?

### Financial quality
- What is true owner earnings / look-through earnings?
- How much capital must be retained to maintain the franchise?
- If insurance/financial: what is the cost and durability of float/funding?
- Are accounting earnings masking or exaggerating economics?

### Capital allocation
- Is retained capital invested well?
- Are repurchases price-sensitive and intelligent?
- Are acquisitions done at sensible prices and with sensible currency?
- Is cash held patiently when opportunities are poor?

### Valuation
- What is intrinsic value per share?
- What assumptions are embedded in the current price?
- How much margin of safety exists?
- What forward return is implied at today's price?

### Risk
- What could produce permanent capital loss?
- Are there rare-event or tail risks hidden by calm recent history?
- Is management willing to walk away from mispriced business?
- Does size reduce future return potential?

## 36) Best reusable framework elements from the 1997 letter

The 1997 letter is especially rich in reusable thinking about:
- intrinsic value vs. accounting measures,
- opportunity-cost discipline in expensive markets,
- insurance float economics,
- rare-event risk and catastrophe pricing,
- look-through earnings,
- acquisition currency and the hidden cost of stock issuance,
- management incentives and owner alignment,
- the value of lower prices for long-term savers.

These are not year-specific observations. They are durable analytical tools.
